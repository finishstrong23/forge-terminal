# Chapter Twelve: The Cold Lives in the Skin

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 5,251 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 6

---

I had been wrong about sparring.
When he said training, I had understood sparring — the kind of physical engagement I was already familiar with, two people in a space working through an understood vocabulary of movement and counter-movement, which I had been doing since I was sixteen and had opinions about. What he had said was training, and what training actually meant, I was discovering three weeks into it, was considerably more than what I had understood.
Sparring was a component. But the training for the deep Hollow was also an excuse — proximity with a functional justification, contact with a clear purpose. Both things were true. The training was real. The other thing was also real.
All of this involved him. Present, close, teaching with the same completeness and efficiency he brought to everything, and the training had become — over three weeks of daily sessions in the north courtyard that had sometimes extended into the lower territory — the part of the day I was structuring everything else around.
I was not going to examine this directly. I was going to note it and move on.
What I was also noting, and also moving on from with varying degrees of success, was the physical contact.
He had not touched me outside of sparring before Chapter Ten — the pin on the dark earth, and the hand pulled upright after, and the cold of both that had lasted an hour. Those had been incidental. Combat-adjacent. The kind of contact that had a clear and functional explanation that did not require any other examination.
The training contact was different. Not in kind — it was entirely functional, entirely purposeful, always brief — but in frequency and in specificity. He corrected. When my stance was wrong he corrected it. When my grip on a position was wrong he corrected it. When my weight distribution for the deep Hollow’s ground was wrong he corrected it, and the corrections were always exactly as brief as they needed to be and not a moment longer — a precision I had catalogued across three weeks and that was doing something to my ability to treat it as ordinary.
The cold lived in the skin.
That was the thing. Not for two seconds, not for a minute. It lived there, in the skin, in the specific locations of contact, for the full duration of each training session and for a significant portion of the time after.
I had been tracking it. Left wrist, inner surface: approximately forty-five minutes. The back of my right hand when he repositioned my grip: an hour, sometimes longer. The point between my shoulder blades where he had adjusted my posture for the Hollow-walking position twice and where I had subsequently maintained perfect posture without being asked and without admitting why: still present, faintly, when I went to sleep.
I was not going to write this in my notes.
I was absolutely going to write this in my notes.
* * *
The session that particular morning began the way they had all begun since the first week: him in the courtyard when I arrived, already there, which I had never been able to catch him arriving at and had stopped trying to, in the center of the dark earth with his hands at his sides and the particular quality of his stillness that I had now spent four weeks watching and that I was, at this point, almost able to read as well as his almost-not-a-smile.
Almost. He was still significantly ahead of me in terms of illegibility and I was not going to pretend otherwise.
“Today is the ground work,” he said.
“I know,” I said. He had told me yesterday. The deep Hollow’s ground work — not combat, not navigation, but the specific physical relationship between a body and ground that did not behave like ground. He had been building toward this for three weeks, in the specific way of someone who taught by constructing foundations before introducing the thing that required them, and I had understood this was coming and had been paying attention to the foundations accordingly.
“You’ll have questions,” he said.
“I always have questions,” I said.
“These will be different questions.” He looked at me with the silver eyes that caught the Hollow’s mid-morning light and held it. “The deep Hollow’s ground is alive in the way that the Hollow itself is alive — not passive terrain. It responds to what moves over it. Your body has to learn to move in a way that the ground reads as belonging rather than intruding.”
I thought about the book in the first language and the mark that had said beginning and the Hollow’s general tendency to treat me as something it recognized. “And if it already reads me as belonging?”
Something happened in his expression — the specific recalibration he produced when something had updated his assessment, faster and smaller than it used to be because he was getting better at containing it or because I was getting better at reading the contained version. “Then it will be easier,” he said. “But we train for the version where it isn’t.”
“Because I should be able to do it regardless of whether the Hollow cooperates,” I said.
“Yes.”
“That is a very sensible approach to training,” I said.
“I’ve had some time to refine it,” he said, in the dry way that I was increasingly certain was his specific register for things that were technically jokes, and that I had decided to receive as jokes regardless of whether he intended them as such, because the alternative was treating an ancient being’s dry observations about a thousand years of experience as straight statements and that seemed like a waste.
I did not laugh. I produced a sound that was adjacent to a laugh and then looked at the opposite wall and said: “Show me.”
The corner of his expression moved.
* * *
The ground work required a specific stance.
He demonstrated it first — that was always how he taught, demonstration before instruction, showing the full thing before breaking it into components — and watching him in the stance was its own category of experience, the way watching something very old doing something it had done many times was its own category. The stance was low and centered, weight distributed differently from combat stance, the relationship between the feet and the ground deliberate in a way that was not bracing but settling. Like the ground was a conversation partner and you were indicating you intended to participate.
I tried it.
“Left foot,” he said.
I adjusted.
“Further,” he said. “The weight needs to—” He stopped. He crossed to where I was standing and crouched beside me, which brought him to approximately the level of my knee, and he placed both hands around my left ankle and moved my foot to the exact position it needed to be in.
His hands were cold.
I knew they were cold. I had known for four weeks that they were cold, this was established, documented, the data was complete. The cold of his hands around my ankle, brief and purposeful, repositioning my foot with an efficiency that required approximately three seconds of contact before he stood and stepped back.
Three seconds.
The cold lived in my ankle.
“Better,” he said, from behind me now, assessing the stance from the angle that showed the weight distribution. “Your right shoulder is compensating. Drop it.”
I dropped it.
“You’re anticipating the correction,” he said. “You’ve been doing that since the second week. When you hear the shoulder is wrong you correct before I finish the sentence.”
“Is that a problem,” I said.
“No,” he said. “It means you’re learning to feel the wrong before it’s named. That’s what I’m training toward.” A pause. He moved around me, the slow circuit he made when he was looking at all angles of a position, and I tracked him through the binding — the directional sense that had become as natural as any other sense, no longer effortful, just present. He stopped at my right side. “Here,” he said, and his hand came to my right arm, just above the elbow, repositioning the angle.
Three seconds. Exactly. The cold came in and settled and he moved away and I was in the correct position and the cold remained, specific and precise, in the exact location of contact.
“Hold it,” he said.
We worked through the stance for twenty minutes before he was satisfied, which was approximately the time it took for me to stop thinking about the ankle and the arm and simply inhabit the position correctly. The mind understood things immediately and stored them abstractly. The body needed repetition and sensation and the feel of being shown rather than told.
He was teaching my body. This was a thing I was going to hold at arm’s length. My legs were shaking from the sustained precision the stance demanded. intellectually and simply execute practically, because the alternative was not going to help anyone.
* * *
The second part of the session was movement across variable ground, which the north courtyard simulated imperfectly but adequately. He had brought in material from the lower territory — dark, dense patches of deep-Hollow ground that sat in the courtyard’s corners like displaced landscape, which was exactly what they were — and the training involved moving across them in the stance while he made corrections.
The corrections were, again, physical.
I was aware of this in advance. I had been aware of it in advance for the full twenty minutes of the stance work, in the specific anticipatory way of knowing something is coming and having complicated feelings about the knowing. The functional layer was learning to move across ground that was alive and that I needed to be able to navigate.
He was precise about the corrections. He was completely aware of every correction. He knew exactly where his hands were and exactly how long they needed to be there and he did not extend a single contact beyond what was necessary. Brief and purposeful, always exactly that, never more.
The never more was doing something.
Not the presence of contact — the boundary of it. The contact was always exactly enough and never more, and that precision was not restraint. It was calibration.
His hands were always where they were for a reason.
This was — I needed a word that was not the word I kept finding. It was significant. It was information. It was going in the notes.
I was crossing the third patch of deep-Hollow ground in the correct stance, with the weight settled the way he had shown me — the conversation-partner quality, the settling rather than the bracing — when he said “stop” and I stopped. He came around to my right side. “The stance is holding but your centre is drifting right on this ground. It’s the density variation — this patch has a heavier concentration here.” “There.”
There.
The cold came in through my ribs. Not through the fabric — through it, in the way his cold moved through things, the internal temperature of him that did not require direct skin contact to register. I had discovered this in the second week and had subsequently been very calm about it and was being very calm about it now, because calmness was available to me as a choice and I was choosing it.
“Feel the difference,” he said.
I felt the difference. Between the drift and the centre, between the wrong position and the right one, between the stance that was technically correct and the stance that was actually settled. He was right — the density variation in the ground was pulling my centre without my registering it, and his hand had showed me where my centre actually was, and the stance from the correct centre felt different from the outside and different from the inside and the difference was significant.
“Yes,” I said.
He moved his hand. He stepped back. He said: “Again from the beginning of the patch.”
I went again from the beginning of the patch.
The cold lived in my ribs. I crossed the patch twice more in the correct stance and did not drift and registered the density variation consciously this time, feeling where the ground was pulling and choosing the correction rather than having it made for me, and when I reached the end of the third crossing he said “better” in the tone he used when something was better and I registered the feel of that better in the place I registered things I was not going to write in my notes and then absolutely was going to write in my notes.
* * *
We worked for two hours. By the end of the two hours I had cold in my left wrist, my right arm above the elbow, the side of my ribcage, the back of my stance-hand twice, and a brief contact at my left shoulder when he had caught my weight-shift before it became a fall on the variable ground, which had been fast and certain and had lasted perhaps one second and that I was filing under things I was going to think about later with enormous thoroughness.
He stopped the session the way he always stopped it — not a declared ending, just the shift in his quality of presence that indicated the functional portion was complete and the remainder was optional. I had learned to read this shift the way I learned to read everything about him: by paying attention until the pattern was clear, and then trusting the pattern.
“The stance held,” he said. “By the third crossing you were feeling the density variation before it pulled rather than correcting after.”
“I was trying to anticipate it,” I said. “Get ahead of the drift.”
“That’s the correct approach,” he said. “The deep Hollow rewards anticipation over correction. It moves faster than you can correct in real time.” He looked at me with the silver eyes — assessment, the kind he made at the end of sessions, measuring what had changed. “You retained everything from the first week. All of it.”
“I retain most things,” I said.
“Most people retain sixty percent after three weeks,” he said. “The rest drifts. The body forgets when the repetition stops.”
I thought about the point between my shoulder blades where the posture correction had been made two weeks ago and that I had maintained perfectly every session since without it being mentioned again. “Maybe I have an unusual relationship with forgetting,” I said.
He looked at me for a moment. “Maybe,” he said, and the word had something in it I did not have a complete vocabulary for yet — a quality I had been encountering more frequently in the past two weeks, something that was not quite the almost-not-a-smile but was in the same family, something that lived in the spaces between what he said and what he meant. I was working on it.
I retrieved the coat from the courtyard wall. He stayed while I put it on — not demonstrably, not in the way of waiting, just present in the way he was present when the session had ended and neither of us had left yet, which had become, I realized, a regular feature. The sessions ended and there was a period of some minutes that was neither session nor departure and that we occupied in the same space without apparent difficulty.
I did not know when this had started. I knew it was regular.
“The weather’s shifting,” I said, because the sky above the courtyard was doing the thing I had learned to read as an atmospheric change — not the deepening of the regular cycle but a different quality, a lateral change, the Hollow’s mood rather than its time. “Tomorrow’s session will be different.”
He looked up at the sky. “Yes,” he said. “The lower territory has a pressure change coming. Two days, maybe three.” He looked back at me. “We’ll use it. The deep Hollow’s atmospheric shifts are part of the training.”
“You were going to teach me to read the weather anyway,” I said.
“I was,” he confirmed.
“And the timing is convenient.”
“The Hollow’s timing is very rarely coincidental,” he said, and I filed this in the place I was keeping things Sable had said were groundwork — the Hollow responding, the recognition, the beginning — and thought about the atmospheric shift arriving exactly when the training required it, and thought about the territory’s behavior in general toward me, and thought about what Vasilisa had said about the Hall being patient for something.
I was going to have to ask him about the Hollow soon. Properly ask, not the careful edge-finding of the first week, but directly, with the specific question that Sable kept saying was the right one and that I was building toward with the accumulated groundwork of five weeks.
Not today. Today I had enough.
“Same time tomorrow,” I said.
“Same time,” he said.
He went toward the Hall’s north entrance. I went toward the east, because the shadow-fox was at the east courtyard wall and I had taken to passing it on the way back — and the shadow-fox had taken to dropping from the wall when I passed and walking approximately six feet behind me to the Hall’s east door before melting back into the territory.
It did this today. I heard it land behind me — not a sound exactly, the cold-light creatures did not fully commit to physical sound, more the suggestion of a landing — and walked to the east door with my coat pulled close and the cold living in six specific places on my body and the Hollow’s atmospheric shift moving in the sky above.
I said good morning to the shadow-fox at the door. It sat and attended, ears forward.
I went inside. The cold in my marked hand had not faded. If anything it was deeper — settling into the bone like something that intended to stay.
* * *
Sable was in the library. She was in my chair, because she was always in my chair, and she was reading something from the left-side shelves that was not one of the books I had been working through. She had her own relationship with the library’s contents that predated and ran parallel to mine and that I was increasingly curious about.
She looked up when I came in. She looked at me in the complete way she looked at things — taking in whatever the complete way involved, which I had given up trying to enumerate and had simply accepted as: Sable sees everything — and then she looked back at her book.
She said nothing.
This was, again, the Sable calibration — the knowledge of which moments needed silence. I sat in the right chair and pulled my notes toward me and looked at the page I had been working on yesterday — a revised sequence of the questions with the right one working its way up from the bottom, and I tried to read it.
I read the same three lines four times.
The cold in my ribs was specific and present and entirely too distracting for a person who considered herself in good control of her attention.
I turned to a fresh page.
“The stance work for the deep Hollow requires physical corrections,” I said, to my notes rather than to Sable, because I was not having this conversation with Sable, I was simply saying something to the air that Sable happened to be in.
“I imagine it does,” Sable said, to her book.
“His hands are very cold.”
“They would be,” she said. “He runs cold in the literal sense. It’s a Vorath characteristic — the oldest of them run the coldest. He’s been the coldest in the territory for a thousand years.” A pause. “The cold tends to linger on contact.”
“For about an hour,” I said. “On average. Depending on location.”
Sable turned a page of her book. She said nothing.
The nothing was extremely loud.
“I’m simply observing it,” I said. “As a physiological characteristic of the situation.”
“Of course,” Sable said.
“It’s relevant data.”
“Entirely,” she agreed.
I looked at my fresh page. I looked at the pen in my hand. I looked at the cold in my ribs, which was currently my most present sensory experience and had been for forty minutes and was going to be for another twenty at minimum.
“Sable,” I said.
“Mm.”
“If you have something to say, you can say it.”
She looked up from her book. She looked at me with both membrane-colored eyes in the library’s quiet Hollow-light, and she had the expression that meant: I have something to say and I am deciding whether now is the moment. Then she decided.
“I have been alive since before the separation of the realms,” she said. “I have watched a very large number of people navigate a very large number of situations. I have watched, specifically, Aleksei across eight hundred years of this Hall and everything it has contained.” She paused. “I have not, in all of that time, seen him make a correction twice.” She looked back at her book. “The stance correction at the shoulder blades was made in the second week and has not been mentioned since. You’ve held the position without being asked.”
I was very still.
“He notices,” she said, in the tone of someone delivering a fact rather than an opinion. “He noticed immediately. He hasn’t said anything about it.” She turned a page. “He also noticed, in the fourth session, that you had stopped using his right-side lag as an approach. You’d closed it — absorbed it, made the knowledge part of your training rather than a tactical tool. He adapted his right side without telling you he had.” A pause. “He could have left it. It was, by that point, not a real vulnerability. He closed it anyway.”
I thought about this. About the symmetry of it — her absorbing information about him and incorporating it rather than deploying it, and him closing the gap anyway, and neither of them acknowledging it, and both of them knowing. “Why are you telling me this,” I said.
“Groundwork,” she said. She closed her book — fully, the way she closed things when she was done with them rather than pausing — and stood. “The right question is getting closer. I want the foundation to be solid when you get there.”
She left. I sat in the right chair with the cold living in six places on my body and the fresh page in front of me and the quality of the library around me — the old attention of it, the patient books on their shelves, the presences in the upper reaches that were warm and quiet and knowing.
I wrote, at the top of the fresh page:
What I know about the training, recorded accurately.
Below that, in my regular handwriting, not the small marginal kind:
He corrects once. I hold the position. He has noticed that I hold it. He closed a vulnerability I stopped exploiting without being asked. Neither of us has said anything.
I looked at this. Then I wrote below it, in the medium handwriting, the one between full and marginal that I was apparently developing as a third category for things that were too significant for the margin and too unresolved for full view:
We are both doing the same thing. Operating on information received and held rather than stated. Choosing not to name it. Something about the not-naming feels —
I stopped. I held the pen above the page for a moment.
Then I wrote the end of the sentence.
— correct. Like a thing that will mean more when it is finally named because of how long it went unnamed.
I looked at what I had written.
I looked at the cold in my ribs, which was still there, still present, still the most immediate sensory fact in my current experience.
The cold lived in the skin. I had been documenting this for four weeks as data. I was going to keep documenting it as data, and alongside the documentation I was going to let the other layer exist — the layer that was not data, the layer that was the simple and inconvenient truth that it was his cold and that I knew exactly whose it was every time it arrived, and that I had not, once, in four weeks of daily sessions and daily cold, wanted it to stop.
I was going to keep documentation and the other layer and I was going to call both things what they were, at least in my own notes, at least on this page, in the medium handwriting that was neither marginal nor full view.
I turned the page. I went back to the question sequence. I worked for two hours with the particular focused attention of someone who has said a true thing and is now able to think about other things without the unsaid true thing sitting in the way of the thinking.
The cold lasted the full hour.
* * *
I went to the eastern boundary alone.
Not spontaneously — I had been thinking about it for three days, since the session where Aleksei had walked me through membrane-reading theory but refused to let me attempt it practically. Too soon, he’d said. Foundation first. Understanding before application.
I understood foundation. I understood theory. What I did not understand was why I should wait for permission to attempt something I was already capable of doing.
The eastern boundary was a twenty-minute walk from the Hall, through the silver forest where the trees ran their steady light and the shadow-fox followed at its devoted distance. I did not tell Aleksei I was going. The binding would tell him my general location and wellbeing — it always did. But general was not specific, and if I was going to attempt this, I wanted to attempt it without the pressure of him watching.
The membrane at the boundary was not visible in the ordinary sense. It was felt — a change in the quality of the air, a pressure that suggested threshold, the specific feeling of standing at the edge of something that had rules about crossing.
I stood three feet back from where the pressure intensified. I breathed. I did what Aleksei had taught me: found the deep Hollow stance, located the membrane’s frequency, extended my awareness toward it the way you extended your hand toward warmth.
The membrane responded.
Not gently. Not the way it had responded to Aleksei when he demonstrated. It surged toward my awareness like something that had been waiting, and the contact was immediate and overwhelming and too much.
I stumbled backward. The membrane followed — not physically, but in my awareness, pressing against the edges of my perception like water pressing against a door that was not meant to hold water. I tried to pull back. The membrane held.
The binding flared.
Not gently. Aleksei’s state shifted from steady to sharp alarm in the space of a single heartbeat, and I felt him moving through the territory, fast, closing the distance between the Hall and the eastern boundary.
The membrane was still holding. I could not pull my awareness back. I tried the deep Hollow stance again — tried to find the frequency, tried to match it rather than resist it. The membrane shifted slightly. Not releasing. Adjusting.
And then Aleksei was there, physically present at the boundary, and his hand was on my shoulder and his voice was saying something in the first language — not a command, not a mark, something else. The membrane released. My awareness snapped back into my body with the feel of something that had been stretched too far and was now returning too fast.
I sat down. Not by choice. My legs simply stopped holding me.
Aleksei knelt beside me. Not speaking. His hand was still on my shoulder — the cold present, steady, grounding.
“I thought I could do it,” I said. After a long moment. When I could speak without my voice shaking.
“You could,” he said. The even voice doing considerable work. “That was the problem.”
I looked at him. “What.”
“The membrane responded to you because you have the Claim,” he said. “It recognized what you are. It responded at full strength rather than the measured introduction I would have given you.” He paused. “If I had not been here to release you, you would have been locked in contact until the membrane decided to release you on its own. Which might have been minutes. Or hours.”
“Oh,” I said.
“Yes,” he said.
We sat at the eastern boundary for a while. The shadow-fox arrived and settled into its attending position. The silver forest ran its light. Aleksei’s hand stayed on my shoulder, the cold constant and present.
“I should have asked,” I said.
“Yes,” he said.
“I wanted to prove I could do it.”
“I know,” he said. “The binding told me. Your state shifted three days ago — from accepting the training pace to resenting it.” He looked at the membrane, at the place where the air changed quality. “I should have addressed it then. I was giving you time to decide whether to ask or attempt on your own.”
“And I attempted on my own,” I said.
“Yes,” he said. “Which tells me something useful about how you solve problems.” Not disapproving. Simply noting. “And it tells me I need to adjust the training pace. If you are ready to push boundaries, then the boundaries need to be somewhere you can push them safely.”
He helped me stand. We walked back to the Hall together. The binding settled back to its normal register — his state steady again, mine recovering but not yet steady.
“Tomorrow we attempt membrane-reading,” he said at the Hall’s threshold. “Properly. With the measured introduction. With me present to release you if the contact overwhelms.”
“All right,” I said.
“And if you decide to test boundaries again,” he said, “tell me first. The Hollow will not always release you simply because you ask. Some things require preparation. Some things require having someone present who knows how to undo what you’ve done.”
I looked at him. “Are you angry.”
“No,” he said. The even voice. The truth. “I am recalibrating. You are faster than I planned for. I need to plan differently.”
He went inside. I stood at the threshold for a moment, thinking about the membrane and the contact and the way it had held me, and thinking about the fact that being fast was not the same as being ready and that I had just learned the difference at the eastern boundary.
The shadow-fox made its quiet vibration from the courtyard wall.
I went inside.
I tracked it, as I always tracked it.
But the fox’s vibration had not been the attending sound. It had been the other one — the lower register, the one Aleksei had not yet told me about.
In the morning I would learn what it meant. I would wish I had asked sooner.
