# Chapter Ten: The Weight Shift

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 4,156 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 4

---

It was Aleksei who suggested the sparring, which I had not expected, and which I spent approximately ten minutes trying to talk myself out of reading into.
Two weeks in, and the days had a shape. Mornings: exercises in the east courtyard. Midmorning: the library, working through the books. Afternoons: the sessions. Evenings: the territory, alone, learning which paths the silver trees preferred and which the shadow-fox used.
The shadow-fox had reached the Hall’s grounds’ edge four days ago. I had said good morning to it. It had tilted both ears forward. This was the closest thing to a conversation we’d had and it was genuinely the highlight of my week, which I was choosing not to examine in terms of what it said about my social situation.
Evenings: the main hall with Sable and whatever food the Hall had decided we were having. Then my room, the desk, the list that was no longer a list of unknowns but a working document of things I was assembling toward the right question, which Sable was delivering groundwork for at the pace of someone who had decided on the pace and was not going to be hurried about it.
Aleksei: present at edges. The north study adjacent to the library. The main hall sometimes in the mornings, briefly, where we had developed the specific wordless efficiency of two people who occupied the same space at the same time regularly enough to have established its choreography without discussing it — he moved left, I moved right, neither of us announced the adjustment.
I was cataloguing all of this. I catalogued it in the document and I catalogued it in the part of my awareness that the binding lived in and I was doing very well at treating both as information rather than anything else.
Then he appeared in the library doorway on a Thursday — I was maintaining a loose approximation of the mortal week on the grounds that structure was useful even when arbitrary — and said: “The training begins today.”
“The territory navigation training,” I said. “You said a week. It has been two weeks.”
“You were acclimating,” he said.
“I told you I’d acclimated on day three.”
“You had not acclimated on day three,” he said, in the tone of someone stating a fact and not a judgment. “You had adjusted to the sensory register. Acclimation includes the body. Your body needed two weeks.”
I thought about this. I thought about the exercises in the east courtyard and the way the Hollow’s air had felt different in my lungs for the first week — not bad, just different, denser somehow, more present, as if the air itself had more content than Zemya’s. I thought about how by the second week it had stopped feeling like anything at all. “Fine,” I said. “Today.”
“Today,” he confirmed. A pause — the specific productive quality of a pause in which he was deciding whether to say something additional. “The territory training will include physical preparation,” he said. “The deep Hollow requires — certain capabilities. I’ll begin training those alongside the navigation.”
I looked at him. He looked back with the silver eyes and the nothing expression that I had, in two weeks, developed a significant vocabulary for and that I was fairly certain I was still only reading correctly about forty percent of the time. “Physical preparation,” I said.
“Sparring,” he said.
Sable, who had been reading in my chair with the apparent lack of interest of someone who was paying very close attention, turned a page.
* * *
The courtyard he took me to was not the east courtyard I used for my morning exercises. The walls were high. The sky above was in its mid-morning register, the deepest blue before noon, and the light it produced fell evenly into the courtyard without the shadows that would have complicated the space. It was, I noticed immediately, a very well-designed training space. Everything about it was deliberate.
He had been here before. Many times. The ground knew it.
I left the coat at the courtyard entrance — it was his coat, he had made it, and I was not going to damage it through careless application to a sparring session even if it was mine now through a genuine transfer of ownership — and I came onto the dark earth in my shirtsleeves. The Hollow’s air was cool on my arms. Not cold — the acclimation had adjusted my baseline in ways I was still mapping — but present, noticeable, the particular temperature of a realm that ran several degrees below what I was accustomed to and that I was now accustomed to.
He was already in the center of the courtyard. I had not previously had occasion to notice the feel of how he was built, because the coat and the layers between us had managed the situation. The layers were not currently managing the situation.
I noticed.
I stopped noticing, because noticing was not useful right now and I had a sparring session to approach strategically.
“Tell me what you know,” he said.
“About sparring.”
“About fighting,” he said. “What you have and what you don’t.”
This was the right question to start with and I appreciated that he had asked it rather than assuming. “I’m fast,” I said. “Faster than I look, faster than most people expect. I’m accurate — I don’t waste movement. I’m good with a knife at close range and reasonable at longer distances.” I paused. “I am very good at not being where someone expects me to be.”
He listened to all of this with the attention he gave everything — complete, without interruption, the composure of someone who was actually receiving information rather than waiting for their turn to speak. “You’ve been in real situations,” he said. It was not a question.
“Several,” I said.
“How did they end.”
“I’m here,” I said.
The corner of his expression moved. The almost-not-a-smile, the one I had catalogued eleven times now and that was becoming, daily, slightly less almost. “Good,” he said. “Show me.”
* * *
He came at me slowly the first time.
Slowly, I understood immediately, not because he was being careful with me but because he was being educational — each movement deliberate, each piece of his approach visible and readable, a demonstration in the form of an attack. He wanted me to see the components. I saw them. I moved left, used his reach rather than fighting it, put distance between us with the minimum motion required, and ended up where I’d intended to end up, which was behind and to his right where the follow-through of his reach made recovery slower.
He turned. He looked at where I was. Something recalibrated in his expression — the smallest adjustment, the thing I had started to think of as the specific look he produced when something had updated his assessment of a situation.
“Again,” he said.
We went again. Speed was where the gap between big and careful and small and precise closed into something workable. I moved, he moved, I moved again, and we circled for thirty seconds with neither of us making contact, both of us reading the other with the focus of two people who were doing this in earnest.
He was reading me. I had none of that on him. The asymmetry was significant and I was not going to close it today and I was going to do my best to make it irrelevant.
I went for the left side.
He was there before I arrived, which I had expected — it was a test, not an attempt, a question about his reflexes rather than a serious approach. His reflex was approximately what I had estimated: extraordinary, but not instantaneous, with a slight preference for the left that I noted and stored.
“You weren’t actually going for that,” he said.
“No,” I agreed pleasantly. “I was finding out how fast you are on that side.”
Another recalibration. Smaller this time, more internal — the adjustment going somewhere I couldn’t read. “And?” he said.
“Fast,” I said. “But the left is faster. Which means the right is the approach.”
He looked at me for a moment. “Most people don’t tell the person they’re sparring with their strategy,” he said.
“Most people aren’t sparring with someone who can read a binding,” I said. “You already know my state. The least I can do is demonstrate that I’m thinking.”
He was quiet for a beat. Then: “Come from the right,” he said. Not permission — a challenge. Come from the right and see.
I came from the right. My shoulder would carry the bruise for a week — the shape of his grip, pressed into muscle by the precision of a hold that had been exactly enough and not a fraction more.
* * *
We worked for an hour.
It became clear very quickly that there were several layers to this session and that they were not all the same layer. Layer two was his assessment — systematic, thorough, the process of establishing what I had and what I didn’t with enough precision to know what to develop. I understood both of these layers and was fine with both of them.
Layer three was mine and I was not going to pretend it wasn’t there.
The courtyard had two exits. The ground was the silver forest’s surface — root and earth and the luminescent moss that grew between the trees. It was the first time I had trained on living ground, and the ground had opinions about my stance.
The deep territory was not somewhere I could survive without his protection, which he had made clear, and which I believed — the Hollow outside Nochval had regions that were genuinely dangerous and I had enough respect for the Hollow by now to take the warning seriously. This was information. I collected information reflexively, about exits and options and the mechanisms of available movement, the same way I breathed — not because I was going to use it immediately, but because information was what kept you alive in the specific category of situations where everything had gone wrong simultaneously and you had about four seconds to do something about it.
He knew I was doing this. I was confident he knew. He said nothing about it, which told me something important about him, which was that he understood the difference between a person who was gathering information as a reflex of survival and a person who was actively planning to use it, and he was not going to treat the first as if it were the second.
I respected this more than I was going to say.
We cycled through positions and approaches for an hour. The corrections were efficient. No wasted instruction, no repetition, everything landing in the exact register it needed to land in to be retained. He had taught before. He was good at it.
He was also, quite clearly, not trying.
Not in the way of someone condescending — he was fully present, fully engaged, his attention on me with the complete quality it had on everything. But he was operating at a fraction of what was available to him, which I had established in the first ten minutes and which was simply the truth of the gap between us. He could end this session at any point, from any position I took, in less time than it would take me to register that he had moved.
I knew this. He knew I knew this. The knowing shared between us without being stated was one of the many things we had apparently decided to share without stating, and I had filed it in the place I kept those.
I was going to get him once. Not through superior skill — I did not have superior skill and I was not going to pretend otherwise. Through information. Through the slight right-side lag I had identified and confirmed across three separate approaches, through the specific shift in his weight that preceded his right-side reach, which I had been reading and reading and reading across an hour of careful attention until I had the shape of it the way I had the shape of Drev’s expression when he was deciding which lie to try next.
He was not Drev. The comparison was absurd. But weight shifts were weight shifts and tells were tells and the part of my brain that read people had been reading him for an hour and it had something.
I waited for the right moment — the moment he shifted his weight to the right in preparation for the reach I’d been provoking him toward for the past five minutes by making his left side look like the better option. The weight shifted. I was already moving when it did — not toward the reach, not the obvious counter, but low and inside, inside his reach before it extended, into the space between his arm and his body that became briefly available in the moment of commitment.
My shoulder connected with his side.
It was not elegant. What it did was make contact, genuine contact, the kind that in a real situation would have followed with something more consequential. I had gotten inside his reach. I had read his tell and used it and I had gotten inside.
He went still.
A full stop — not the practiced stillness of his default state but something sharper, the involuntary kind, the kind that happened when something had occurred that a body needed a moment to register. It lasted perhaps one second.
Then his arm came around.
* * *
It happened faster than I could track — not the educational slow of the first approach, not the careful calibrated speed of the past hour, but something closer to what he actually was. One moment I was inside his reach with my shoulder against his side, and the next moment I was not. There was a brief, vertiginous sense of having my momentum redirected — cleanly, with a precision that was almost considerate in its efficiency, nothing rough about it — and then the dark earth of the courtyard was at my back and his forearm was across my collarbone and the rest of the geometry of the situation had resolved in his favor with a completeness that left very little to be unclear about.
I was pinned. Comprehensively. His forearm across my collarbone, his weight distributed with the specific economy of someone who knew exactly how much pressure to apply to be effective without being excessive, and his face was close — the proximity of it something I had not encountered before, this particular close, the silver eyes in the Hollow’s mid-morning light finding mine with the focused attention of his full presence rather than the managed distance he maintained across a room.
He said nothing.
I was not in pain. He had been completely precise about that — not a bruise, not a strain, nothing that would matter afterward except in the most relevant sense, which was that I had lost and he had demonstrated it. But I was very completely not going anywhere; the point, and I was aware of this the way you were aware of a fire in a room: the primary fact in the register, everything else secondary.
“That was good,” he said.
His voice at this proximity was different from across a room. Not louder — never louder, he did not operate in louder, volume was not a variable he seemed to use. Closer. More present in a way that was purely physical, the way all sounds were more present close up, because the distance the sound had to travel had become negligible.
I became aware that I was not breathing with ideal efficiency.
“The tell,” I said. My own voice was even. I was very pleased about this. “The weight shift before the right reach.”
“You read it for forty minutes before you used it,” he said.
“I wanted to be certain.”
“You were certain after twenty,” he said.
I stared at him. He was correct. I had been certain after twenty and I had kept reading it anyway, running the same assessment repeatedly, confirming what I already knew. “How did you know that,” I said.
“You stopped testing the left after twenty minutes,” he said. “You only went back to it to establish the contrast.” A pause, and in the pause, something that I was almost entirely certain was not nothing. “You’re thorough,” he said. Not a criticism. Something in the precise opposite direction, stated with the flatness he applied to all genuine assessments.
The forearm across my collarbone had not moved.
I was aware of the weight of it — not heavy, not uncomfortable, the specific awareness of something in contact with the skin at a location that was not a location I had many things in contact with. His arm was cold. Not the ambient cold of the Hollow, not the cold of stone or air — the cold of him specifically, the temperature that was his own and that I had known abstractly from the theory of what Vorath were and that was now immediately, specifically, undeniably present against my collarbone and the side of my throat.
It was very cold.
I had not expected it to be cold the way it was cold. This was not that. This was its own cold, internal, the cold of something that produced its own temperature the way a fire produced its own heat, and the cold of it was — I was going to find a word for it that was not the word I was currently reaching for, and I was going to find it shortly, and in the meantime I was going to focus very carefully on the conversation.
“The gate,” he said. “North wall. You’ve looked at it four times.”
“Five,” I said. “You missed the second one.”
He looked at me. I looked at him. We were extremely close and we were looking at each other, and his forearm was against my collarbone, and I had just told him I had been counting my exit assessments and corrected his count, and neither of us was going to address any of this directly.
“The bar on the north gate takes four seconds to lift from the inside,” he said. “You’d calculated two.”
“The latch is weighted,” I said. “I didn’t account for the weight.”
“It’s weighted to keep it from rattling in the Hollow’s atmospheric shifts,” he said. “It was not designed as a security feature. If you gripped it here—” He described a position with specificity that indicated he had thought about this. He had thought about this. He had watched me look at the north gate and worked out my calculation and identified its flaw and was now correcting it — not a thing I had expected and that I had no adequate response to. “—you could do it in two seconds.”
I looked at him for a long moment. He looked back.
“You’re teaching me how to escape faster,” I said.
“I’m teaching you accurate information about your environment,” he said. “What you do with it is yours.”
The cold of his forearm was against my skin and his face was close and he was teaching me how to lift the latch on the gate more efficiently and I was making a decision about what this all meant that I was going to need considerably more time to make.
“I think we’re done for today,” I said.
He stepped back. He extended a hand. I looked at it. I took it, and he pulled me upright with exactly the force required to do that and no additional force whatsoever, which was another thing I was filing.
“Tomorrow,” he said. “Same time.”
“Tomorrow,” I agreed.
He went to retrieve his outer layers from the wall where he’d left them. The coat smelled like the Hollow and woodsmoke and something underneath that I had still not categorized to my satisfaction. I pulled it close.
He passed me on the way out of the courtyard. He did not look at me. He looked at the north gate, briefly, and then at me, with the specific look of someone confirming that information had been received. I held his gaze.
“The tell’s gone now,” I said. “You’ll have corrected for it.”
“Yes,” he said.
“So I’ll have to find a different one.”
The corner of his expression moved. Smaller than usual — almost nothing. But the almost-nothing was becoming, I had established over two weeks of observation, one of the most reliable indicators of what was happening behind the controlled surface. “Yes,” he said. “You will.”
He left the courtyard. I stayed for a moment longer, looking at the north gate, at the latch, at the position he had described for lifting it most efficiently. Then I looked at my hand — the one he had pulled upright, the one that had briefly been in his.
Cold. Still. My hand was still cold, faintly, the ghost of it — not cold the way the air was cold but cold with the register of his cold, the internal cold, the temperature that was his own. I had held his hand for approximately two seconds and the cold of it was still in my palm and the side of my fingers.
I closed my hand.
I stood in the north courtyard with my hand closed and the coat around me and the Hollow’s mid-morning sky above, and I looked at the north gate, and I thought about accurate information, and I thought about a person who taught you how to escape more efficiently and called it what you do with it is yours, and I thought about the cold of a forearm against my collarbone that I was fairly sure I was going to be thinking about for the rest of the day.
I went inside.
I went to the library, where Sable was in my chair.
She looked up when I came in. She looked at me. She looked at my hand, which I was still holding slightly closed. Her membrane-colored eyes did a small, complete circuit of available information and arrived at a conclusion with the speed of something that had been paying attention to context for longer than most things had existed.
She said nothing.
She turned back to her book.
This was, I was beginning to understand, one of the most valuable things about Sable: she knew the exact moments that required silence the way she knew the exact moments that required the placed observation. She had calibrated this over more centuries than I had been alive, and the calibration was, in its way, a form of care.
I sat in the right-side chair.
I picked up my notes.
I read the same paragraph four times without retaining it.
The cold lasted in my collarbone and the side of my hand for an hour. I tracked it precisely, with the attention I applied to information I intended to use, and when it faded I noted the fading with the same precision, and I sat with the fact that I had tracked it and noted it and that tracking and noting was the thing I did with information I intended to use, and I thought about what it meant that I was treating the ghost of his cold on my skin as information I intended to use.
I turned to a fresh page in my notes.
At the top, because I was not going to lie to myself in my own notes, I wrote:
He taught me how to escape faster.
And below it, after a moment, in the small marginal handwriting:
I did not think about escaping for the entire last half of the session.
I looked at this for a while. Sable turned a page. The shadow-fox, which had taken up its position on the east courtyard wall again at some point, was visible through the library’s narrow windows — the cold-light quality of it patient and attending in the way it always attended.
Tomorrow, he had said. Same time.
I wrote, below the two lines, so small it was barely legible:
Good.
Outside the narrow windows, the silver trees leaned again, as if the realm itself approved.
And in the courtyard, the shadow-fox made its quiet vibration — the first time it had done that inside the Hall’s boundaries. The creature’s range had contracted in recent months — it used to go to the territory’s eastern edge. Now it stayed within the inner ring, the brighter trees. It had moved inward the way a person moved away from a drafty window.
The first time. Not the last.
