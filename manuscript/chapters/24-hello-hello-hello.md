# Chapter Twenty-Four: Hello, Hello, Hello

**POV:** Aleksei · **Words (v1):** 5,961 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 6

I wrote to Fen and Cael in the morning before she came downstairs.
Not because the morning was the best time for it — the best time for those letters was three days ago, and the urgency of the political situation had simply not fully resolved into action until Vasilisa’s visit provided the clear outline of what action was required. I wrote them now because they needed to exist before the convening and because the letters were a thing I could do while the other thing that was happening in the Hall today was happening, and having a task whose requirements were clear and whose completion was measurable was, this morning, useful.
The fifth mark.
I had told her what it contained, in the entrance hall, the binding reporting her state as full focused attention. And the thing she had been circling for ten weeks — the thing neither of us had named yet, the thing the book’s marks had been pointing toward since the abstract future carrier — was going to put her finger on it this afternoon.
I wrote to Fen and Cael. The letters went out through the membrane’s correspondence channel — encoded, directional, arriving in their territories within the hour. I wrote to Fen about the restoration evidence. I wrote to Cael about the convening count. Both letters ended with the same request: be there.
The binding told me she was in the east courtyard.
It was the tone of her in the working state — the exercises, the daily sequence, the shadow-fox on the wall and its attending. I had been reading this quality through the binding for ten weeks and it was as familiar as the Hall’s warmth and the deep current’s note, as ordinary as the second cup and as significant when you understood what they were and why they were that.
I finished the letters. I sent them. I sat for a moment in the north study with the morning’s quality — the quality all mornings had now — running at the new frequency, with the fifth mark in it today like the older territory’s high signal, present and building.
The Hall was warm. They knew what today was. They had been in this Hall since before I was in this Hall, and they had been here for Vasilisa’s visits and for Radovan’s visit and for the night Sable arrived and for the morning I had walked into the east corridor and found that the book had moved. They had been here for all of it.
They were here for this.
I sat a moment longer. Then I picked up my coat — I was going to the territory after the session, and the older territory in the late afternoon had its own temperature regardless of the Hollow’s ambient — and I went downstairs.

The morning session was the boundary membrane work, the continuation of what we had been building since Zora’s appearance at the eastern edge.

I looked at her. “That is a significantly more advanced skill than boundary-signature reading,” I said.
“I know,” she said. “Tell me if I have the foundation for it.”
I considered what I knew of her foundation, which was everything, because I had been building it deliberately and tracking it precisely for ten weeks. “You have the foundation,” I said. “The skill requires the Claim’s active presence to supplement the membrane-reading — the surface reading alone isn’t enough to penetrate the boundary. Two weeks ago I would have said no. Now—”
“Now,” she said.
“Try it,” I said.
She settled into the stance and found the eastern boundary’s signature — she could do this in under a minute now, the muscle of it developed — and then she did something I had not watched anyone do in a very long time: she pushed the reading through the boundary rather than stopping at it. Not physically, not with force. With the quality of the Claim that was a property rather than a tool, the specific nature of a thing that the membrane recognized and did not resist, that moved through the boundary the way light moved through glass rather than stopping at the surface.
The reading took four minutes. She held the stance for all four of them with the composure of someone who had been doing this long enough for the body to manage the maintenance while the attention went elsewhere.
She came back slowly, the specific care of someone returning from a concentrated depth. She opened her eyes.
“Cold,” she said. “On the other side. Not the boundary cold — a cold that is also a presence. A territory with a temperature that is a decision rather than a climate.” She paused, working through the description with her usual precision. “And something watching. The same watching I felt before, but from the other side now — I can feel the watching looking in the direction of Nochval. Looking for the signal.”
“Zora’s territory,” I said. “As experienced from within rather than from the boundary.”
“And she’s still looking,” Kira said. “She hasn’t moved. She measured the signal and she’s waiting.”
“For the convening to be called,” I said. “She can’t act unilaterally — the protocol requires the formal meeting. So she waits and she aligns with Maren and she builds her position while we build ours.”
“Good,” Kira said. “Waiting means we have the two weeks.” She pulled out of the stance with the practiced ease of someone at home in their own body. “I can do that at the northern and southern boundaries too. Before the convening. Know what every territory feels like from the outside, so when those Vorath are in the room I know the quality of the ground they’re standing on.”
I looked at her for a moment. At the specific clarity of the mind working, the sequence building, the instinct that was not instinct but a trained skill running at full speed. “Yes,” I said. “Over the next three days. One boundary per session.”
She pulled the coat on. “Tomorrow the north,” she said. “That’s Radovan’s direction.”
“It is,” I said.
“I already know how that one will feel,” she said, pleasantly, and went inside.
I stood in the courtyard for a moment with the shadow-fox on the wall and the morning’s quality, and I thought: in a thousand years of the Hunt and the territory and this Hall, she is the only person who has ever told me that Radovan’s direction would feel warm and said it like a mild observation about weather.
I went inside. I had things to prepare before the afternoon.

I left the Hall at the second hour of the afternoon.
She was in the library. The binding gave me her state: present, still, the particular feel I had learned to read as: she has just sat down in front of something significant and has not yet done the significant thing. The quality of standing at the edge before you step forward. She had the book. She was going to receive the fifth mark.
I needed to not be in the Hall when she did.
This was not because the receiving would be difficult to witness — I had been in the north study for the third mark and had felt it through the wall and it had been the most significant thing the Hall had contained in decades. The fifth mark would be more significant, and I had written it, and what it was going to give her was what I had understood love to be in the first world’s full language, and the specific fact of her receiving that in the library on the other side of the wall from the north study was a thing I could not be adjacent to while it was happening. Not without the management collapsing entirely.
I went to the older territory. The shadow-fox emerged from the forest’s interior as I entered and moved to my left with the accompanying-without-following quality it had been using with me since it started using it with her. We had not discussed this, the shadow-fox and I. It had simply begun.
I walked deep. Through the outer forest and into the territory where the trees grew taller and the canopy closed above and the stratified light of the ancient growth fell in columns. It did not become ordinary. It remained the feel of something that had waited a hundred years and had ended its waiting, the arrival registering every time.
I found the oldest tree — the one at the territory’s deepest point, the one that had been here since before the Hall existed, whose bioluminescence was the deepest silver of everything in Nochval and whose roots ran down further than any reading I had ever done could follow. I stood near it. Not touching — I did not touch the oldest tree. But in its proximity, which had its own quality, the feel of something that had accumulated more time than most things in existence and wore that time as weight and light simultaneously.
The shadow-fox sat to my left and attended.
I waited.
* * *
The Hall changed from the older territory.
I felt it through the Hunt-sense — the broad awareness that was mine as the Hollow’s keeper, the same domain sense that had felt Zora at the boundary and Radovan at the northern edge. The Hall was within that awareness always, a constant known presence, its quality as familiar as my own temperature. And the Hall’s quality shifted, there in the older territory with forty minutes of silver forest between me and its walls.
The presences. All of them, the full eight hundred years of accumulated awareness, attending in the single direction with the held-breath quality. The threshold quality. The moment of something significant passing through the Hall’s space.
She had her finger on the mark.
I had felt the third mark from the north study, through the wall, and what I had felt was the Claim encountering its own nature described accurately. The fifth mark’s concept was not a description of the Claim’s nature. It was a description of the Claim’s destination — the thing the Claim had been oriented toward, the thing the seven years of transit had been transit toward, the thing the first world had understood when it chose its carriers and knew that the choosing was not random, that what was chosen in a carrier and what the carrier was in transit toward were the same nature expressed in two different forms.
What I felt from the older territory, standing near the oldest tree with the shadow-fox to my left, was the Hall experiencing something it had not experienced in its eight hundred years of existence. The presences going very still. Not the afraid-still of Zora’s visit. The still of arrival.
The deep current in the ground surged.
Not by much. Not dramatically. Three seconds of the deep current running at a higher signal than it had reached since the separation.
Then it settled. Back to the changed note — the hello quality, the warmth — but slightly higher than it had been this morning. Incrementally higher every day. The recovery proceeding, the Hollow’s century of diminishment reversing by degrees, the deep current finding its old volume. 
The oldest tree’s bioluminescence brightened. By a degree, a single degree, the silver deepening fractionally toward the color it had been before the separation, before the dying. I had been looking at this tree for eight hundred years and I knew its light precisely and the light was different now and I was, I found, not sufficiently composed to stand here and receive that alone.
The shadow-fox stood. It looked not at the tree but at the direction of the Hall. At her.
“Yes,” I said.
It began to move through the forest, not toward the Hall — the long diagonal route that went through the oldest section of the territory and came around to the Hall from the south rather than the direct route. The accompanying quality, not the leading quality. It expected me to follow.
I followed.
* * *
She found me before I found her.
I was at the silver forest’s outer edge, coming through the last of the tall trees toward the Hall’s grounds, and the binding gave me her location — moving, through the forest, from the Hall’s direction inward. Moving toward the older territory. Moving with the purpose-direction quality, not the training pace, not the measured careful movement of someone navigating unfamiliar ground. She knew this ground now. She was moving through it with the ease of someone who had been learning this forest for weeks and had the Claim to supplement the reading of it.
She was coming toward me.
I stopped at the outer forest’s edge and I waited, and the shadow-fox stopped beside me, and the silver forest was its steady afternoon light around us — the bioluminescence shifting register as the Hollow moved toward evening, the stratified columns in the older section visible through the outer trees, and the oldest tree’s light very slightly deeper than this morning.
She came through the outer trees and saw me and did not stop. She stopped at the distance of — something closer. The distance of two people who have decided there is no reason to be further away.
I looked at her.
The fifth mark was in her. I could not see the concept — the marks gave their content only to the carrier and not to the person who had written them. Open, and something more — the bearing of someone who had received something that confirmed rather than introduced. Not surprised. Arrived.
“The oldest tree is brighter,” she said. She had looked at it on her way through — she knew this forest, she had learned to read its light, of course she had noticed.
“Yes,” I said.
“The deep current surged,” she said. “When the mark answered. I felt it through the ground.”
“I felt it from here,” I said.
She looked at me. “You left the Hall,” she said.
“Yes.”
“Because you couldn’t be there when I received it.”
She was not asking. She was stating, with the accuracy she brought to everything she read correctly, and she was reading this correctly. I held her gaze. “Yes,” I said.
Something moved through her expression — not the surprised version, the recognized version. The version where confirmation arrived of something already understood. She looked at the forest around us, at the older section’s light visible through the outer trees, at the shadow-fox to my left attending with its usual composed patience. She looked at her hands.
“The sixth component,” she said. She had been counting — receiving all six simultaneously, distinguishing each one. “The last piece. And you’re saying this is the one that changes the legal structure of the covenant.”
I was very still.
“The sixth component,” she said, steadily, with the composure that was never performed, “is that you cannot stop paying attention to them. Not as a quality of the love — as the indicator of it. The person who is a necessity is the person your attention returns to regardless of direction to north regardless of how you turn it. Involuntarily. Before you decide.” She held my gaze. “The first world considered that the most reliable indicator. That the attention is not chosen. That it simply orients.”
The silver forest was around us. The oldest tree was its slightly deeper light at the territory’s center. The shadow-fox attended with the composure of something that had been waiting for this specific clearing, this specific light, this specific distance between these specific two people, for longer than anyone had been keeping count.
“Ten weeks,” I said. My voice was even. It was not costing anything to keep it even. The evenness was simply what the truth sounded like when it was not being managed. “My attention has been orienting toward you since the night of the Hunt. Before I made any decision about it. Before I had adequate information to make any decision about it.” I paused. “You were in an alley with a knife that would not work against anything I was, and my attention oriented toward you before the Hunt-sense had finished identifying the source of the Old Claim, and it has not reoriented since.”
She looked at me with the open expression and the fifth mark in her and the deep current in the ground and the feel of the evening coming into the forest around us, the bioluminescence brightening in its incremental response to the Hollow’s fading sky.
“I know,” she said. Quietly. “I have been watching it orient since the first morning. The way you position in a room. The way the binding runs. The way the north study is adjacent to the library corridor.” A pause, the small kind. “The way you put both cups out.”
I had no response to this that was not the full thing. The full thing was present in me with the complete weight of ten weeks and the fifth mark and the deep current and the oldest tree brighter by one degree, and the management had been getting lighter since the disclosure and since the chair beside rather than across and since her hand on the arm of the chair, and there was, tonight, in this forest, with her at this distance, essentially nothing left of it.

Not the correction proximity — not the heel of the hand at the ribs or the forearm across the collarbone or the brief purposeful contact of the training. My hand open, offered, in the space between us at the distance that was closer than any previous distance, and she looked at my hand and she looked at me and she put her hand in mine.
The cold.
Mine, specifically — internal, my own temperature rather than borrowed from the Hollow’s air, the cold that had lived in her skin at six locations across ten weeks of corrections. In her hand now. Not briefly, not the correction’s duration. Simply present. Her hand in mine with the cold coming through and her warmth coming back in the opposite direction and the two of them meeting at the surface of the skin with the quality of the first world’s understanding of what happened when two things that were each other’s necessity finally stopped being separated by the management of the distance between them.
She did not pull away. She held the contact with the same composure she held everything — completely, present, giving it the full weight of her attention without performing the giving. Her hand was warm the way I had been tracking for ten weeks the mind decided to notice it a room.
We stood in the silver forest in the evening light and I held her hand and neither of us said anything for a long time.
The shadow-fox made the sound — the sub-linguistic vibration she had described from the morning, the membrane-register below hearing. It lasted three seconds. It left the forest feeling, in its wake, slightly more itself than it had been before.
“That’s the second time it’s done that,” she said. Her voice had the composure of someone speaking carefully so as not to disturb something.
“I know what it means now,” I said.
She turned slightly to look at me without releasing my hand. “Tell me.”
“It’s the Hollow’s acknowledgment,” I said. “The sub-linguistic register is how the realm communicates things that don’t have marks yet — things that are happening for the first time and haven’t been named in any existing language. The shadow-fox carries the register more clearly than anything else in the territory.” I looked at the fox, which had resumed its attending posture with the satisfied quality of something that had done what it came to do. “It made the sound the morning after the third mark. And now.”
“Twice,” she said. She was looking at our hands — mine around hers, the cold and warmth at the meeting of them. “The Hollow is noting things that don’t have names yet.”
“Yes,” I said.
She was quiet for a moment. Then: “Then I won’t name them yet either.”
“No,” I said. “Not yet.”
We walked back through the silver forest side by side with the evening coming into the trees and the bioluminescence rising in its incremental response, each tree brightening as the Hollow’s sky cycled toward its deeper register. She kept her hand in mine for the full length of the forest and I kept mine in hers, and the cold lived in her hand with the same quality it had lived in her ribs and her wrist and the back of her hand for ten weeks, except that tonight it was there because she had put her hand in mine and not because a session required it.
The shadow-fox moved ahead of us, then alongside, then ahead again — the patrol pattern, the territory being as it was. The oldest trees were at their deep silver at the forest’s heart and the outer trees were the silvered-blue of their evening register and the deep current was in the ground below saying hello, and the Hall’s warmth was visible ahead as we came to the forest’s edge — the bioluminescence of the building’s east wing, the quality of its light that I had been looking at from this direction for eight hundred years and that had not, until ten weeks ago, had this quality of arrival in it. The quality of coming home to a Hall that knew you were coming.
We came out of the forest onto the Hall’s grounds. The shadow-fox went to the east courtyard wall. The Hall was warm and present and the presences were doing the thing they had been doing since the morning — the warmth of accumulated patience in the present tense — and the evening sky above Nochval was cycling toward indigo, the deepest blue before the nadir.
At the Hall’s door she looked at me. Her hand was still in mine and she looked at me with the open expression and the fifth mark in her and ten weeks and everything that was in the accounting between us and the not-named thing in the silver forest that the Hollow had acknowledged in the register below language.
“The table,” she said.
“Both cups,” I said.
“Both cups,” she confirmed.
She went inside. I stayed on the Hall’s threshold for a moment with the cold of her hand still in my hand — the specific thing — she was inside and I was outside and the cold was hers now, in my hand, the same way my cold had lived in her skin for ten weeks. I stood on the threshold and I looked at the silver forest and I felt the deep current in the ground and I thought about the sixth component and the compass that does not decide and I thought about the not-named thing and I thought about how the Hollow had made a sound for it, in the register below language, because it did not yet have a mark.
It would have one.
I knew, standing on the Hall’s threshold in the evening with the silver forest behind me and the Hall’s warmth before me, that I was going to write it. Not tonight, not tomorrow — there were marks between here and that one, marks in the book that she had not yet received, and the convening approaching with its political weight, and the two weeks of foundation still being built. But I had written twenty-three marks before the separation and I had felt, writing all of them, the quality of the first world’s language arriving at a concept it had not previously captured, and what had happened in the silver forest this evening had that quality.
The Hollow had made the sound for it.
I would find the mark.
I went inside. The table had both cups. She was at the table with the coat on her shoulders and her second notebook open to a fresh page and the pen in her hand, and she looked up when I came in and the evening was what it was, which was what it had been for the days since the chair moved beside rather than across, the ordinary running at the frequency that was now simply the ordinary.
I sat down. I took the second cup.
“The fifth mark,” she said. “Tell me what you felt from the older territory.”
I told her. All of it — the Hall’s quality, the presences, the deep current’s three-second surge, the oldest tree’s light. The pen moving. The table between us. Both cups. The Hall warm and the silver forest outside and the deep current in the ground below saying what it had been saying since the old territory session, steady and present and not going to stop.
At some point during the telling she rested her left hand on the table.
Open. Palm up. Not reaching — resting, the specific position of something available rather than extended.
I set down the cup. I put my hand over hers. The cold arriving in the space between them, hers coming back, the same as the forest — the meeting at the skin’s surface, the two temperatures finding their equilibrium.
She kept writing.
The pen moved across the page. My hand over hers on the table. The evening cycling through its registers outside. The Hall warm around us with the eight hundred years of its patience and the presences doing their warmth in the upper reaches and the deep current in the ground below.
I had been in this Hall for eight hundred years.
I had been cold for a very long time.
I sat at the table with her warmth in my hand and I thought about the first world and the separation and the hundred years of dying and the seven years she had been in transit and the ten weeks since the alley, and I thought about the mark I was going to write, and I thought about how it was going to feel to write it in the first language with the full weight of the concept behind it — the specific not-yet-named thing that the Hollow had made a sound for in the silver forest — and I thought about her putting her finger on it in the afternoon light and what the mark would give her.
What I knew was this: her warmth in my hand was the most specific thing I had felt in eight hundred years of the Hollow and the Hall and everything in it, and the specificity of it did not diminish with repetition, it deepened, the way the Hollow accumulated rather than depleted, and I had a long time — longer than she had, longer than most things had — and I intended to spend it paying attention.

I found Sable in the library three days before the convening.
She was at the left-side table, which she preferred when she was working through something that required concentration. I did not interrupt immediately — I stood in the doorway for a moment, deciding whether what I was about to ask was reasonable or overstepping.
Sable looked up. “You have a question that is making the binding pulse in a way it has not pulsed before.”
“Does the binding tell you that,” I said.
“The binding tells him that. He is in the north study pretending to work but actually tracking whatever you are doing in the library doorway.” She closed her book. “Ask the question.”
I came into the library. I sat in the chair across from her. I said: “Can you contact Radovan for me.”
Sable went very still. Not the ordinary stillness — the other kind. The kind that meant she was paying complete attention. “Why.”
“Because he told Aleksei that the eastern territories are talking,” I said. “About restoration. About political opposition. And I think he was trying to tell us something else underneath that — something about which territories might support us and which might not.”
“And you want to ask him directly,” Sable said.
“I want to know if there’s anyone we can count on besides Vasilisa,” I said. “The convening is in three days. If there are Vorath who support restoration but haven’t said so publicly, I want to know before we walk into that hall. I want to know what we’re actually facing.”
Sable looked at me for a long moment. “Aleksei does not know you are asking this.”
“No,” I said.

She left. I sat at the left-side table and looked at the old-language books on their shelf and thought about the fact that I had just made a political move without consulting the person I was supposedly partnering with, and that the move would either give us useful information or create a complication I would have to explain.
The binding pulsed. Aleksei in the north study, his state shifting from focused to alert. He knew I was doing something. He did not know what.
Sable returned fifteen minutes later. “Radovan will meet you at the eastern boundary in one hour,” she said. “He was — intrigued by the request.”

If it goes well, you will owe me a significant favor.” She sat back down at her table. “And you will need to tell Aleksei before the meeting. He will feel you arrive at the boundary. He will know you are there with someone. If you do not tell him, the binding will tell him you are concealing something, and that will go worse than simply asking for his trust now.”
She was right. I knew she was right.
I went to find him.
* * *
He was in the north study, as Sable had said, pretending to work. I stood in the doorway. He looked up immediately — he had known I was coming, the binding had told him I was moving through the Hall toward him.
“I asked Sable to contact Radovan,” I said. Before he could ask. “I want to know which Vorath might support us at the convening. Radovan has information he didn’t share fully when he visited. I want that information.”
Aleksei set down his pen. “You went around me.”
“I took initiative,” I said. “Which is not the same thing.”
“It is when the initiative involves political contacts I did not approve,” he said. The even voice. Not angry. Something else — the manner of someone recalibrating on the spot. “What did you tell Sable to ask him.”
“To meet me at the eastern boundary in an hour,” I said. “To tell me what he knows about which territories support restoration and which don’t. To give me information we can use.”
Aleksei was quiet for a long moment. “You understand that this creates a perception problem,” he said finally. “That it looks like you are building your own political network. Independently of me.”
“Yes,” I said. “I understand that. I’m doing it anyway.”
He looked at me. The silver eyes steady, assessing. Not deciding whether to allow it — I had already arranged it, the decision was made. Deciding how to respond to it.
“All right,” he said.
I had been prepared for resistance. The all right was not resistance. “All right,” I said.
“You will tell me what he says,” Aleksei said. “Immediately after the meeting. Everything he says. The political landscape is more complex than you know, and some information is useful and some information is misdirection and you will not always be able to tell the difference.”
“Agreed,” I said.
“And if you take political initiative again,” he said, “tell me before rather than after. I cannot help you navigate consequences if I do not know the action is happening.”
“Agreed,” I said again.
He picked up his pen. “Go to your meeting. I will be here when you return.”
I went to my meeting.
And Radovan, when I met him at the eastern boundary an hour later, told me three things.
The deep current was audible in his territory for the first time in three centuries. The restoration was spreading.
Vasilisa had secured Kael’s support. Our count was six.
Then: Maren had filed a formal challenge to the covenant’s neutrality. On the grounds that the personal bond between carrier and territory keeper constituted undue influence. The challenge would be heard at the convening.
“She’s using the old law,” Radovan said gently. He was only gentle when the situation required it. “The provision about covenant integrity. If the bond between you and the carrier influenced the covenant’s formation —”
“It did not.”
“I know. But knowing and demonstrating are different verbs, and Maren is skilled at exploiting the distance between them.”
If the challenge succeeded: the covenant dissolved. The marks unstable. The restoration stalled. Kira would begin again in a different territory, under a different Vorath.
The thing we had built was the thing that could destroy the work.
It was, I thought as I walked back to the Hall, worth the overstep.
The pen moved. The fire burned. The evening turned toward night.
I put my hand over hers. Both cups on the table, side by side. And I looked at them and understood that anyone who walked into this Hall would see what they meant.
Two cups. One table. No distance.
The evidence Maren needed was sitting on our table every evening, and we had put it there ourselves.
