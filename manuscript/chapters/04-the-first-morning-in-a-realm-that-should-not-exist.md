# Chapter Four: The First Morning in a Realm That Should Not Exist

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 4,344 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

I woke up already cataloguing exits.
This was not unusual. I had woken up cataloguing exits since I was sixteen, the age at which sleeping in places other than where I was supposed to be had become a regular feature of my life. It was the kind of habit that started as a necessity and eventually just became the way your brain arranged itself during the transition from sleep to waking — a fast, involuntary survey of the environment, a check of available options, a brief assessment of whether the options had changed since you closed your eyes.
The options had not changed. The window was still too far down. The door was still the only real exit. I had memorized the number of steps between the bed and the door last night before I slept — twelve — not enough distance to matter against something that moved the way he moved, but information was information and I collected it regardless of immediate utility.
I lay still for a moment, looking at the ceiling.
The ceiling was stone, high and vaulted. The room was large enough to be called a hall itself. The bed was enormous and low-framed. Everything in the room was beautiful in a way I found suspicious — the beauty of things that had been made for someone specific.
That column was very full.
I sat up. I was still wearing the coat.
I looked at the coat. I needed to make a decision about the coat. It was his coat, clearly, despite the fact that he had handed it over with the tone of a person giving away something that no longer required a decision — here, this is yours now, the matter is settled — and it was extremely warm and it smelled like the Hollow and like woodsmoke and like something I was choosing not to examine, and I had slept in it, which I was also choosing not to examine.
I took it off. I folded it over the back of the chair at the desk with what I felt was an appropriate amount of care for something that was not mine and that I was going to return.
Then I stood up and began to properly look at the room.
* * *
The first thing I did in a new place was inventory. Not sentimental. Practical.
The room was five paces by eight. The bed was against the wall, the desk near the window, the wardrobe against the far wall. Everything was made from the same blue-grey stone that produced its own ambient light. The room was beautiful and practical and clearly built for someone who was expected to stay.
The window.
I had noticed the window last night, in the way that you noticed something whose full implications you were not ready to process at the moment — you filed it for later, for the part of the evening after the immediate situation was dealt with. Now I went to it.
The silver forest began perhaps thirty feet from the Hall’s outer wall, which meant from the window I was looking directly into it — directly at the silver-barked trees with their interior luminescence and their extraordinary height and the way they moved without wind, a slow, considered motion that was not quite like anything I had a word for. Not swaying. Not reaching. Something between the two. Something purposeful.
The sky above the forest was the same deep, impossible cycling dark I’d seen on arrival, moving through blues and greens and the deep purple that pressed against the eye like held breath. I had no idea what time it was. There was no sun. I was good with time. I needed to stay good with time.
The trees closest to the Hall were bright — the silver bark luminescent, beautiful in the way of things alive in ways I had no framework for. But further out, where the trunks were wider and older, the light was thinner. Not dimmer exactly. Still lit. Still functioning. But something underneath was not being replenished as fast as it was being used.
I did not know enough about the Hollow to know what that meant, which was becoming a theme. I filed it under things to ask about when asking becomes an option.
Below the window: a straight drop of what I calculated to be four floors. Stone wall, no purchase, nothing to break the fall until the ground, which was also stone.
I had known this last night. It was still annoying to confirm.
I turned from the window and looked at the room, at all of it, the whole of it together, and for the first time I let myself notice what I had been skirting around since I woke up.
The room was perfect.
Not perfect the way a very good room was perfect — comfortable, well-appointed, tasteful. Perfect in a different sense, a more unsettling one. Perfect in the way of a room that had been assembled with careful research and considerable precision by someone who had not furnished a room for a mortal in a very long time and had gone about the project the way you went about something you were determined to do correctly.
The blankets were the exact weight I would have chosen, if asked. I had not been asked. Someone had anticipated that I might want to write in quantity. Work through problems on paper. Think by writing.
They were correct. I did.
But no one knew that. No one who had known me for the span of one alley conversation and a tour of a corridor could have known that, because it was not the kind of thing that announced itself, and I was specifically not the kind of person who announced things that could be used to read her.
The room knew anyway. Or whoever had prepared it did.
I went to the cabinet by the window and opened it. Inside: food. Enough for several days.
Someone had sourced mortal food from Zemya. For me. Before I had agreed to stay.
I looked at this for a moment. I ate the bread and the cheese, standing by the window, watching the silver forest.
But further out, in the ring of oldest trees, the light was thinner than yesterday. Not dimmer — thinner, like gas lamps on Saltfish Market running low on fuel. Still lit. Something underneath not being replenished as fast as it was being used.
* * *
The door had no lock on the inside.
I found this after breakfast, when I conducted the closer inspection of the room that thoroughness required. No lock, no bolt, no latch on my side — the door opened from outside with a handle I could not reach from within, and from inside with a handle I could reach, meaning I could leave but not lock myself in — either an oversight or a statement, and I didn’t think he was a person who made oversights.
I tested the door. It opened. The corridor beyond was empty, lit by the same slow luminescence in the stone, running in both directions into a distance that should not have been possible given the building’s external dimensions. No guards. No barrier. No visible indication that I was meant to stay in the room rather than leave it.
He had told me last night that I was not a prisoner in the physical sense. I understood that he meant this very literally — there was no mechanism of physical confinement. There was only the binding, the constant awareness that cut both directions, and the knowledge that Nochval’s boundaries were not something I could cross without his knowledge.
The binding was a more elegant constraint than a lock. A lock could be picked. A lock required only skill and time and the right piece of wire. The knowing was quiet, like the awareness you had of your own heartbeat: always present, only noticed when you paid attention to it.
I paid attention to it, briefly. North. Far. Outdoors.
I stepped out of my room and went in the opposite direction.
* * *
The Hall was larger than it had looked from outside. Corridors intersected at intervals that felt deliberate, as if the building had opinions about where people should go. And on the second floor, between two corridors that should have been identical, I passed a room with no door that contained something I could not explain: an hourglass of extraordinary size in which nothing was moving.
I looked at the hourglass for a moment. It held the Hollow’s light in its glass in the way everything here held light, pooled at the bottom with the sand and not moving.
I kept going.
There were more corridors. One had a quality of silence behind it that was different from the silence of an empty room. One had a door that was darker than the others, darker than the stone around it, with a heaviness to it that was not entirely physical, and I stood in front of it for a moment feeling the specific quality of that heaviness and then I moved on, because some doors were not mine yet.
Yet. I noticed that word in my own thinking and I set it aside to be annoyed about later.
I found the library by smell — the correct way to find a library. The smell of old paper and binding leather and the specific cool dryness of a room that had been maintained at the right conditions for a very long time — it came from under a door at the end of a corridor that bent slightly, as if the Hall had changed its mind mid-construction, and I followed it without hesitation because if there was a library I was going to know about it.
The library was enormous. I opened the door and stood in the entrance for a moment, adjusting.
Floor-to-ceiling shelves on every available wall, and free-standing shelves in rows down the center of the room, all of them full — not the careful, curated fullness of a collection maintained for display, but the dense, practical fullness of a room that was used. Some shelves were neat. Others had books stacked horizontally on top of vertical ones where the shelf had run out of space, and papers tucked into gaps, and in one place a small object being used as a bookmark that I crossed the room to look at and found was a smooth river stone, gray and unremarkable — the kind of bookmark that happened when you were in the middle of reading something and needed to stop and grabbed the nearest thing.
The nearest thing, in this library, might have been from any century.
There were tables — two of them, long and dark, with chairs that had the worn quality of furniture that had been sat in regularly over a very long time. There was a fireplace here too, smaller than the one in my room, cold at the moment. One of them I stood in front of for a long moment, the script catching at something in the back of my mind the way a word in a half-remembered conversation catches — familiar without being known, the impression of understanding without its substance.
I pulled the book from the shelf.
It was old. The cover was dark and smooth and gave slightly under my fingers in the way of leather that had been conditioned for centuries by dry hands. The script on the cover matched the spine — flowing, angular, with a rhythm to it that suggested it was meant to be read aloud.
I opened it.
The interior pages were the same script, small and precise, in an ink that had not faded. I held it at the angle where the Hollow’s ambient light caught it best, and I looked at the first page, and I did not understand it, and I did.
Both things were true simultaneously — not a state I was accustomed to. I could not read the words. The individual marks meant nothing to me. Almost. Almost. Like hearing your name said just quietly enough that you couldn’t be certain.
I stood in the library of an ancient being in a realm that should not exist, holding a book in a language no one living should have been able to recognize, and I almost recognized it.
I put it back on the shelf.
I put it back where it had been — I had noted the position when I took it, the exact angle it sat at, the way it pressed against the books on either side. I slid it into place until it sat exactly as it had. Then I stepped back and looked at the shelf and breathed slowly and thought about the fact that the binding told me Aleksei was still outdoors and had been for several hours and I had no idea what an ancient Vorath lord did outdoors alone for several hours but I was not going to go and look.
I was going to go back to my room. I had seen enough of the Hall for one morning. I was going to go back to my room and I was going to sit at the desk with the practical paper and I was going to make an organized list of everything I did not understand, because that was how I dealt with things I did not understand, and then I was going to start at the top of the list and work through it systematically until I understood.
This was the plan. It was a good plan. I left the library and retraced my route back through the Hall.
* * *
The windowsill in my room had something on it.
I noticed it when I came back through the door — I had left the door slightly ajar when I went out, a habit, and the Hollow’s ambient light from the corridor fell at an angle across the room that it hadn’t had before, catching the windowsill in a way that made the object on it visible from the entrance. Small. Low. Dark.
I had not seen it last night. I had inventoried this room carefully last night, twice — once when I arrived, once before I slept — and this object had not been here.
Which meant either the room had placed it there while I slept — a thing the Hollow was probably capable of, or someone had.
I crossed to the window.
It was small enough to fit in my closed hand. The carving was a fox. Sitting, ears forward, tail curled around its feet with the specific composed alertness of a fox that had decided to be still and was being absolutely, deliberately still. The carving was good — genuinely good, the kind of good that came from long practice and caring about the outcome. The lines were not ornate; they were precise. Every edge that needed to be sharp was sharp. Every surface that needed to be smooth had been worked until it was.
It had been handled. For a very long time, by hands that returned to it regularly: the high points of the carving were worn in the specific way of things touched often, the ears and the bridge of the nose and the raised line of the tail slightly smoother than the recessed surfaces. The wood had the feel of something that had absorbed decades of warmth from human — or not human — contact, and had changed in response to it, and was still changing.
I turned it over. No marks on the base. No inscription. Nothing to explain it.
I turned it back right side up. The fox looked at nothing with its composed, deliberate expression. I looked at the fox.
I thought about putting it in my pocket. I thought about the weight of it, which was heavier than such a small thing had a right to be — the density of the wood, the accumulated weight of however long it had been in existence, the specific kind of heavy that things acquired when they had been important for a long time. I thought about the fact that it had been placed here intentionally, which meant it was here for a reason, and taking it from where it had been placed would change something about that reason in ways I couldn’t fully anticipate.
I put it back.
Exactly where it had been — I had noted the position when I picked it up, the slight angle of it toward the window, the way the tip of the tail nearly touched the window frame. I set it back with the care I’d used with the book in the library, one position restored to its original configuration.
I went to the desk. I sat down. I pulled the top sheet of paper toward me and I wrote at the top:
Things I Do Not Currently Understand (In Order of Urgency).
I wrote for forty minutes. The list was longer than I would have preferred.
* * *
He appeared in the doorway sometime around what I was choosing to designate as mid-morning, though the Hollow’s sky gave me nothing to confirm this and I was operating on appetite and the biological sense of time that the body maintained regardless of available light.
He appeared the way he did everything: without announcement, without noise, simply present where he had not been a moment before. I was at the desk with my list. I heard nothing. I looked up because the binding shifted — not dramatically, just the specific recalibration of nearness, the awareness settling from far-north-outdoors to here — and he was standing in the open doorway with the door behind him and his hands at his sides and that particular quality of stillness that I was beginning to understand was simply his default state, the way some people were in constant motion and he was in constant stillness.
He was looking at the windowsill.
Not at me. At the windowsill, at the fox, at the exact position it sat in. The look lasted only a moment — the specific, involuntary quality of a glance that lands before you’ve decided to direct it — and then his eyes moved to me.
I met them. I said nothing.
He said nothing.
Something passed between us in that silence — not words, not information exactly, more like the acknowledgment that something had occurred and had been observed by both parties and did not require discussion. He had looked at the fox. I had seen him look at the fox. He knew I had seen him look. I had put the fox back exactly where it was, which was also a piece of information, and he had received it, and we were both going to proceed as if none of this had happened because that was, apparently, the kind of arrangement we were going to have.
I was unexpectedly fine with this.
“How do you determine time here,” I said. “There’s no sun.”
He came into the room — not all the way, just across the threshold, so he was in it without occupying it, which I was beginning to think was characteristic of how he inhabited spaces. Near walls. Near exits. Never in the center of a room. “The Hollow has a cycle,” he said. “The sky deepens to black at the nadir and lightens toward blue at the peak. The nadir is midnight. The peak is noon.” He paused. “You learn to read it.”
“How long does it take to learn to read it?”
“A few weeks, for most.”
“And for mortals.”
“I don’t have a comparison point,” he said. “You’re the first mortal to stay here in three centuries.”
I absorbed this. Three centuries was a long time by any measure I had available to me. “Why did the last one stay?” I asked.
“They were caught in the Hunt,” he said, which answered nothing and everything simultaneously.
“And after them,” I said. “Three hundred years of Hunts and none of them stayed.”
“None of them stayed.” Something in his delivery — not emphasis, but the absence of it, the flat neutrality that might have been covering something or might have been nothing at all. I had not yet developed the tools to tell the difference.
“But they were all caught,” I said.
“The Hunt catches mortals occasionally,” he said. “Yes.”
“And they all chose the memory option.”
A pause. Shorter than the ones last night, but present. “They were all given the memory option,” he said.
I looked at him. He looked at me.
“You offered me three options,” I said.
“I did.”
“And the last mortal caught — three hundred years ago — they were offered how many.”
He held my gaze without difficulty. The silver eyes were very still, very present, and they gave me nothing. “One,” he said.
I sat with that for a moment. One option. Three hundred years of one option. Last night, without explanation, three. I thought about adding this to my list and decided it was already on my list under a different heading, one I had written in the third line:
Why was I given options.
“Right,” I said. I looked back at my paper. “How do I access the rest of the Hall? Are there rooms that are locked?”
“Most of the Hall is accessible to you,” he said. “There are two rooms that are not. You will know them when you reach them.”
“How will I know?”
“They’ll feel closed,” he said. “The Hall communicates in impressions.”
“The Hall,” I said, “communicates.”
“It is old and it is alive and it has opinions,” he said, in the tone of someone who had been working around this fact for a long time. “You will adjust.”
I thought about the library, about the book I’d almost-read, about the language that had pulled at the back of my mind. I thought about writing this on my list and decided it was already there, fourth line:
The book in the language I should not recognize.
I did not mention the library. Not yet. I was still turning it over, still looking at both sides of it, and I had learned a long time ago not to offer information before you understood what it meant.
“The coat,” I said.
His eyes moved to the chair where I had folded it.
“It’s yours,” he said.
He said it the same way he’d said it last night — flatly, conclusively, the matter settled before I’d finished asking the question. I looked at the coat. I looked at him. He was already looking back at me with the specific patient quality of someone who had made a statement and did not intend to revisit it.
“I was going to return it,” I said.
“It’s yours,” he said again. Not louder. Not warmer. Just: again, in the same tone, with the same finality, like a second reading of a verdict.
I looked at the coat. At the way I had folded it — carefully, the kind of careful you used with something that belonged to someone else. And then at his face, which was showing me nothing except the silver eyes that caught the Hollow’s light and gave it back wrong.
“Thank you,” I said.
Something happened in his expression — small, gone before I finished the word. He turned toward the door.
“There will be food brought to the Hall’s main room in the evenings,” he said, from the doorway. “The Hall itself will show you where, when the time comes. If you require anything specific, you can — the binding will convey it.” He paused, as if reconsidering the sentence. “You can simply need something. I’ll know.”
The intimacy of this — the idea of simply needing something and having him know — sat in the center of my chest with a weight I was going to deal with later.
“And the coat,” I said, because I could not entirely help myself. “You’re certain.”
He was already half-turned to go. He stopped. He did not look back at me when he said it — he said it to the corridor, or to the air, or to some private audience of his own: “Wear it. You’ll be cold otherwise.”
And then he was gone, and the door was open, and I was alone with my list and the fox on the windowsill and the coat on the chair and the whole of the impossible Hollow outside my window, doing its slow, purposeful, watchful almost-swaying.
I picked up the pen.
I added to the list:
The fox. What it is. Why it is here. Why he looked at it first.
And below that, after a moment, in smaller writing, in the half-margins where I kept things I was not sure deserved their own line:
Why it matters that I put it back.
I looked at the fox. The fox looked at the middle distance with its composed, deliberately still expression.
I reached over and picked up the coat. I put it on. For warmth. Obviously.
Outside the window, the silver trees leaned, very slightly, toward the glass.
Not random. Not wind. Not the natural sway of things that grew.
They were looking at me.
The Hollow itself was looking at me. And I did not yet understand why a realm should care about one mortal woman and a coat she had been given, but the trees were leaning and the Hall was warm and somewhere in the territory the binding was steady and present and aware.
I had the gut-level certainty that I had just done something that mattered more than I knew. And something in the Hall’s deep structure — below the presences, below the warmth — had shifted in response. Not toward me. Toward what I was becoming.
