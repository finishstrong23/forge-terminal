# Chapter Three: Ancient Law and What It Means for My Immediate Future

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 4,060 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

There is a moment, when you arrive somewhere completely new, where your brain performs a very rapid triage: immediate threats, available exits, distance to the nearest other human being. I had done this so many times in so many bad situations that it happened automatically now, without input from the part of me that was supposed to be in charge of decisions.
The Hollow failed the triage in every category.
The immediate threats were not things I had words for. The available exits were not visible. The nearest other human being was, by all available evidence, not human at all, and was standing six feet to my left with his hands clasped behind his back and the patient quality of something that had been waiting in this particular space since before my civilization had invented the concept of space.
I breathed. I looked.
The Hollow was — I want to be precise about this, because imprecision would not do it justice — impossible. Not in the way that things were sometimes called impossible to indicate that they were surprising or difficult. In the way that this place operated by rules that had not been included in my understanding of how reality functioned, and the gap between what I had understood and what was currently in front of me was wide enough that I had to actively resist the urge to sit down.
The sky was the deep cycling dark of something that had never held a sun. Not black. A moving darkness that cycled through blues and greens and a purple that pressed against the eye.
The air tasted like winter and something I didn’t have a name for. Something that predated winter.
I was still holding the knife.
I sheathed it. Partially because it remained ceremonial and partially because I needed both hands to be functional right now, and gripping a knife was giving one of them a job that felt productive and wasn’t.
“All right,” I said. My voice sounded strange here — too crisp, too present, like speaking in a room with very particular acoustics. “Explain the law.”
He looked at me with those silver eyes that held the Hollow’s ambient light the wrong way — gathering it rather than reflecting it, so they appeared to produce their own dim luminescence. In the Hollow, in this light, he looked more like what he was. Less like a man in a dark coat, more like a man-shaped arrangement of very old darkness that had decided to present itself as a man for practical reasons.
I found this significantly less comforting than the alternative.
“The law,” he said, “is as follows.”
* * *
He told me the law the way I suspected he did everything: completely, efficiently, and with the professional remove of someone delivering information rather than having a conversation. No apology in it. No warmth. No visible awareness that this particular information was, for the person on the receiving end of it, something other than routine.
The law was as follows.
Anything caught outside during the Veil Nights belonged to the Hunter who caught it. Not a negotiation. Not a punishment. An absolute, with the weight of a natural law, built into the world’s grammar during the first separation.
“Remain,” I repeated.
“In my territory. In the Hollow. Until a full turning of seasons has passed, at which point you may petition for formal release.”
A full turning of seasons. A year. I was being told, calmly and without any particular inflection, that I had just been acquired for a year by an ancient being who lived in a realm that operated by different physical laws, because I had made the objectively correct decision to go retrieve sixty silver marks that were owed to me.
I thought about the sixty marks in my inner pocket. I thought about whether they had been worth this.
Behind me, in the alley I had just left, something shifted. Not a sound — a quality. The air had changed register, as if the street itself had noticed I was carrying something valuable and had opinions about the carrying.
I decided that was not a productive line of thinking right now.
“The memory option,” I said instead. “If you wipe the evening and return me — I go back to my room in the Warrens, and the Veil Nights just happened, and I woke up this morning and didn’t go out, and none of this—” I gestured at the impossible sky, the silver trees, the ancient being in the dark coat “—is something I know about.”
“Correct.”
“And there are no other effects. Nothing left over. I just don’t know that this happened.”
He paused. It was a very small pause, barely a pause at all, the kind of pause that I would not have noticed if I hadn’t been watching him with the attention I reserved for situations where missing things had consequences. It lasted perhaps two seconds.
“No other effects,” he said.
He said it with the even quality of a person stating something accurate, and I filed the small pause in the place I filed things I didn’t have enough information to interpret yet — a very crowded place on an average day and was filling up rapidly at the moment.
“And the third option,” I said. “Remaining. What does that involve, practically?”
“Safety. Shelter. Food.” He produced this list with the economy of someone who had covered the essentials and considered them sufficient.
“And?”
“And what.”
“Those are the conditions of a decent boarding house,” I said. “They’re also, I notice, about a third of what I’d want to know before agreeing to spend a year somewhere.” I held up a hand, mostly so I could count on my fingers, which helped me think. “What is this place, specifically? What are the rules here? Can I leave the immediate area? Can I communicate with anyone in Zemya? What does the binding—” I had not missed the word binding, said as though it was a technical term with a specific meaning “—actually entail? What are the other residents of this territory, and are they going to be problems? What does ’petition for formal release’ mean in practice? Is a year fixed or is there—”
“This place is my territory within the Hollow,” he said. “It is called Nochval. The rules are that you do not leave Nochval’s boundaries without my knowledge. You may communicate with Zemya through means I will provide. The binding means that I am aware of your general location and wellbeing at all times, and you will have a corresponding awareness of mine.” He paused. “The other residents of this territory will not be problems.”
I counted. “That was four questions. I had eight.”
“A year is fixed,” he said.
“Five. I still have three.”
Something moved in his expression. Not a smile — I was confident it was not a smile. More the way a muscle moved that was adjacent to where a smile would be, a different thing entirely, and it was gone before I’d fully registered it.
“Ask,” he said.
* * *
I asked. He answered, occasionally, which was less useful than it sounds.
The petition for release at the end of the year was a formal request to the law — not to him specifically, though he had input. He could deny it, though he did not say under what circumstances he would. He would not, he said, know my precise thoughts. He said this with the flat certainty of a person stating a fact and not an assurance, and it landed differently than an assurance would have.
He did not explain how a mortal was meant to survive in a realm that was evidently not designed for mortal habitation. I asked this directly. He said: “You’ll survive.” I asked how he knew.“ I asked what that meant.” A pause. “Most of them.”
I processed this. “Most of them,” I repeated.
“The exceptions are avoidable,” he said, in the tone of someone who considered the matter settled.
I noted that he had not actually explained how the Hollow had decided I was manageable, which implied that this was unusual, which implied a number of things I did not yet have enough information to interpret. I added it to the pile. The pile was architectural at this point. I was going to need a better organizational system.
“The memory option,” I said again. I was not, at this point, making a decision — I was testing the edges of things, which was how I always worked. You felt the shape of something before you committed to a position on it. “If I choose that. You can do it right now? Return me to my room, no memory, and this conversation never happened?”
“Yes.”
“And you’d just — have caught a person tonight and released them, and that would be the end of it.”
Another pause. Longer this time. He was looking at me with an expression that was not readable and that I was going to need considerably more data to decode. “Yes,” he said.
“Would that happen often?” I asked. “Catching and releasing. Is that normal.”
“It has been done before.”
“That’s not the same as normal.”
He said nothing. The silver eyes held mine without apparent effort. The Hollow made a sound around us that was not wind — something deeper, something that came from the ground rather than the air, a note that I felt in my sternum more than heard with my ears.
I had been caught by the Hollow Hunt. I had three options: death, amnesia, or spending a year in the territory of an ancient being whose name I still didn’t know.
I found a loophole.
* * *
I understood the shape of it now. Not the mechanisms, not the politics, not the thing they called the Claim that I could feel building in the place below language.
But: I was bound by ancient law to a dying realm. The law had teeth. An immortal keeper watched me with an attention that had nothing to do with duty. And somewhere beyond his territory, other immortal powers were beginning to notice that the realm’s long decline had stopped — and to ask what had changed.
What had changed was me. I needed to be ready for that.
“The law says anything caught outside belongs to the Hunter,” I said carefully. “But I wasn’t outside in the usual sense. I was in the process of returning inside. There’s a meaningful distinction between someone who willfully leaves shelter during the Veil Nights and someone who was caught in transit between one inside and another. I was heading home.”
He looked at me for a long, flat moment.
“You were heading north,” he said. “Away from home.”
“I course-corrected,” I said.
“After four steps.”
“Promptly,” I said. “The point is that my intent was to be inside, and intent is a relevant factor in most legal frameworks, and if this law is as old as you’re suggesting then—”
“Intent is not a relevant factor in this law,” he said. “The law is predicated on physical location at the time of the bells, not on what you meant to be doing.”
“That seems like an oversight.”
“It has been the law for several thousand years without significant complaint.”
“People who are released to the Hollow are dead,” I said. “People who have their memories wiped don’t know to complain. You’ve had a very convenient absence of feedback.”
Something happened in his expression again. The same almost-not-a-smile, slightly more definite this time. I was beginning to think it was the version of amusement available to beings who had been maintaining dignity for a millennium and were not going to compromise it now.
“The law stands,” he said.
“Fine.” I had expected this. I moved on. “The binding. You said you’re aware of my general location and wellbeing. Is the awareness constant, or only when you actively—”
“Constant.”
I did not love this. “And I have a corresponding awareness of yours.”
“A directional sense. Distance. Broad states — if I am injured or in significant danger, you would be aware of it.”
“Would I want to be?”
He seemed to consider this as a genuine question. “Probably not,” he said.
“Wonderful.” I thought about asking how I was meant to feel about having the general location and wellbeing of a Vorath lord constantly present in my awareness, and then decided that I knew how I was meant to feel about it and that asking would not change the situation. “You said the Hollow has already decided I’m manageable. Who made that determination before tonight? I’ve never been to the Hollow. I’ve never had any contact with the Hollow.”
“Have you not,” he said.
It was not quite a question. I looked at him. He looked at me. He did not elaborate, which was, I was beginning to understand, a primary characteristic of this particular ancient being: he said the things he chose to say and the space where other things might have gone was simply empty. Not awkward. Just empty, with the character of a room that has been deliberately cleared.
I filed this too.
“Second loophole,” I said. “The binding awareness goes both directions, which means we are technically in mutual contact. Most legal frameworks treat mutual contact as implying mutual obligation, which means I should have equivalent standing in any formal proceedings about the terms of my stay. If I have standing, I can petition now rather than waiting a year, because—”
“You do not have standing,” he said. “The binding is asymmetrical. My awareness of you is a function of the Hunt law. Your awareness of me is incidental.”
“Incidental,” I repeated. “I have a god in my head as an accident.”
“As a consequence,” he said. “Not an accident.”
“What’s the difference?”
He seemed to find this question more interesting than the loopholes. “An accident implies that something occurred without mechanism. A consequence implies that the mechanism produced this result specifically.” He paused. “The binding produced this result specifically.”
I stared at him. “You make the binding sound like it chose to do this.”
He held my gaze for a beat that was a fraction too long. “Ask your other questions,” he said.
* * *
I asked my other questions. He answered a precise subset of them. By the end of our conversation I had a map of the situation that was functional, though incomplete in a number of places that I suspected were deliberate.
What I knew: I was in a territory called Nochval, in the Hollow — a living realm adjacent to but not the same as the world I’d come from. He would ensure my safety and basic needs. I was not imprisoned in any physical sense. I could not leave Nochval without his knowledge.
I would know, at all times, roughly where he was and whether he was in danger.
I had not yet decided how I felt about this last one.
What I didn’t know: why the Hollow had apparently been paying attention to me before tonight. What the small pause before “no other effects” had meant. What the binding’s consequence — as distinct from its accident — implied.
The not-knowing was not comfortable. It was the particular discomfort of a woman who survived by understanding her situation completely, standing in the middle of a situation she did not understand completely, in a realm where the trees made their own light and the ground remembered things that had happened before memory.
I was going to stay. The death option was ineligible on principle. Which left remaining, with the significant practical advantage that a year spent inside the Hollow learning everything I could about it was a year spent on the correct side of the information gap.
Also the loopholes I’d found so far were both closed, and I needed more time to find one that wasn’t.
These were my reasons. They were sound and practical and said nothing at all about the fact that I had looked at the man-shaped ancient darkness in front of me and found it — him — significantly more interesting than I should.
“I’ll stay,” I said.
He received this with the same flat attention he’d given everything else. “I’ll show you to the Hall.”
“One more thing.” He had already turned. He stopped. “Your name,” I said. “You know mine. The binding means you know mine.”
A pause. He turned back. “Aleksei,” he said.
It was said flatly, the way he said most things — but there was a quality to it, a weight to the way the word landed. Like it was a real thing, given, and he knew it was a real thing, and he was choosing to give it anyway.
I filed this in a different place than the other things. A better-organized place. “Thank you,” I said.
He said nothing. He turned and walked toward the silver trees, and after a moment I followed, because the alternatives were worse, and because the Hollow was watching with the sense of something very patient and very old that had been waiting a long time for this specific sequence of events and was now watching them unfold with the particular satisfaction of a thing whose plans had come to fruition.
I did not know this then. I only knew the cold, and the impossible sky, and the name I was turning over in my mind the way you turned over a coin to see both sides.
Aleksei.
The silver trees moved around us as we walked, and I noticed that they moved slightly toward me, leaning at the edge of my peripheral vision, and that he noticed me noticing, and said nothing.
* * *
He gave me a coat at the boundary of what he called the Hall’s grounds.
It was not offered. It was stated: “You’ll be cold. Take this. Here. This is useful. Take it.
I looked at the coat. I looked at him. He was in shirtsleeves now, apparently unbothered by the cold that had been affecting me since we arrived. The Hollow cold — the deep, considered cold of this place — was not hurting him. It occurred to me that it probably never had.
“You’re not cold,” I said.
“No.”
“Vorath don’t get cold.”
“Not in the way mortals do.”
I took the coat. It was heavy — heavier than it looked, the fabric something I didn’t recognize, dark and dense and clearly old. When I put it on, the warmth was immediate and substantial — the coat itself and not, I told myself firmly, anything to do with residual warmth from the person who’d been wearing it.
It smelled like woodsmoke and something else. Something under the woodsmoke that I had no category for — older, deeper, the smell of a place rather than a person. The smell of the Hollow distilled into something almost bearable by proximity to whatever Aleksei was.
I pulled it closer. For warmth. Obviously.
“The Hall,” he said, which seemed to mean: follow me, or: we’re going now, or simply: here is the next relevant noun in this sequence of events. He was already walking. I followed.
The Hall was larger inside than seemed geometrically possible, which I was going to file under things that the Hollow did that didn’t require explanation so much as acceptance. He led me through corridors of dark stone that held the Hollow’s ambient light in their surfaces — it came from inside the stone, from deep down, slow and patient — and past rooms with closed doors that I catalogued automatically: the shape of the spaces behind them, whether the hinges were on my side, what I could hear or smell from outside. Old habits.
He stopped at a door and opened it. The room inside was — prepared. The fire produced light that was more orange than the Hollow’s ambient blue, and it was warmer, and the combination of the fire and the coat meant I was almost comfortable, which felt like a significant achievement.
Everything in the room was exactly correct. This was the problem with it. The desk had paper and ink already laid out. The fire was at the size that would burn through the night without requiring attention. It was the room of someone who had researched what a person needed and provided it precisely.
Not the room of someone who knew.
The distinction was small and probably didn’t matter and I noticed it anyway because I noticed everything.
“The Hall’s presences will leave you alone tonight,” he said from the doorway. “There’s food in the cabinet by the window. If you need something, you can—” He stopped.
“Can what?” I asked.
He seemed to recalibrate. “You’ll know where I am,” he said. “Through the binding. If something requires my attention, I’ll know.”
Which meant he would feel it if I was in trouble, through the same mechanism that let him feel my general location and wellbeing. I thought about having a Vorath lord constantly aware of my wellbeing, and I thought about all the ways that could be uncomfortable, and I thought about the cold of the Hollow and the fact that I was currently warm in his coat and how none of these things were things I should be thinking about in any connected way.
“Right,” I said. “The binding. Constant.”
“Constant,” he confirmed.
He left. The door closed behind him with the specific quiet of something very heavy moving with more care than strictly necessary.
I stood in the center of the room and looked at the window and the fire and the exactly-right blankets and the view of silver trees going on forever, and I breathed the Hollow air, which tasted like winter and something older, and I was twelve minutes from home by a route that no longer existed, and I was going to be here for a year.
I sat down on the bed. The mattress was, obviously, perfect.
“Sixty silver marks,” I said to the room, to no one, to the fire which crackled with what I chose to interpret as sympathy.
I was still wearing the coat.
I did not take it off.
I was choosing to wear the coat.
The shape of it, now that I had the pieces: I was bound by ancient law to a territory keeper older than anything I had a framework for. The realm he kept was dying. I was carrying something the realm recognized. And the politics of what I carried were going to bring every ancient power in the Hollow to our door.
That was the situation. The other situation — the one I was not examining right now — was that the territory keeper had silver eyes and a silence I wanted to fill and hands that were cold in a way I was starting to find interesting.
Both situations were going to cost me something. I suspected it might be the same thing.
 That was important — not because the coat mattered in itself, but because every choice I made in this place was a choice I was keeping track of. Which ones were mine. Which ones were his. Which ones belonged to the law and which ones I was making freely inside the law’s constraints.
The coat was mine. I was keeping it because I wanted to, not because he had given it to me.
And in the morning I would discover that the Hollow did not observe choices without responding to them — and that its response would rewrite the terms of my stay.
The binding pulsed once — brief, present, the weight of something noting a fact and filing it for later use.
He knew. Wherever he was in this realm, he knew I was still wearing it.
And somewhere in the territory, something else knew too. The Hall’s warmth shifted slightly — not colder, not warmer, simply different, the way a room felt different when someone had just walked in and decided to stay.
I had made a choice about the coat. Every watching thing in this realm had seen me make it.
I did not take it off.
