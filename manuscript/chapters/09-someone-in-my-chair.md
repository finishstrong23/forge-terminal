# Chapter Nine: Someone in My Chair

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 4,087 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

Something moved through the Hall.
I was at my desk when it happened — writing the fourth revision of the question at the top of my sequence, trying to find a version that was precise enough to be answerable, when the Hall did a thing it had not done before. It shifted. Not physically — nothing moved, no sound, no change in the ambient light or the temperature of the room. But it was not the presences. It was something else. Something that occupied the same register the presences occupied and was considerably louder about it.
The binding was doing nothing unusual. Aleksei was in the north study — close, stationary, where he had been for the better part of the day. This was not him.
I sat still for a moment and paid attention to the shift, the way I paid attention to things I could not immediately categorize. It was moving. Through the Hall, in the east corridor, toward the library. The particular feel of something that knew where it was going and intended to get there and was going to find what it found when it arrived and have opinions about it.
I put down my pen.
I thought about staying in my room. I thought about the fact that it was evening, that the Hollow’s sky had been cycling toward its deeper register for the past hour, that I had been writing for three hours and my hand was cramped and my tea was cold, and that whatever was in the east corridor was heading for the library — my library, insofar as I had claimed it as mine over seven days of daily occupation.
I was not going to let something have opinions in my library without knowing what it was.
I picked up my pen, looked at it, put it back down, and went to the library.
* * *
Someone was sitting in my chair.
Not my chair in any formal sense — I had not been given a chair, specifically, had not been told that any chair was mine, and the library had two perfectly functional tables with perfectly functional chairs attached to both. But I had been sitting in the left-side chair at the left-side table every day for a week, and in the same way that a worn spot on a path became the path, that chair had become my chair through the sheer accumulated weight of seven days of habitual use.
Someone was sitting in it.
She was reading my notes.
Not the old notes. The book was where I had left it, pages still open to the mark that had said beginning. And beside it, something new: a folded page, written in a hand that was not mine, in a script I could not read, placed with the deliberate care of someone who wanted it found.
I stood in the doorway.
She did not look up.
She was — I catalogued, because cataloguing was what I did when I needed a moment — approximately my age in appearance, though something in the tone of her presence suggested that appearance was not the relevant measure. The not-quite-color of the membrane when it dissolved on the Veil Nights. The color the sky went wrong in the alley before everything changed.
She was slightly translucent at the edges.
Not dramatically — not in a way I would have noticed if I hadn’t been paying the particular feel of attention I paid to things that were not quite what they appeared. Like looking at something through very clean glass. Like a thing that was entirely real and also, somehow, in two places at once.
She turned a page of my notes.
“These are mine,” I said.
“I know,” she said. She had a voice that matched the rest of her — not quite fully in the room, the way a voice heard from the next room was not quite fully present but was entirely real. “Your handwriting is very legible. Most people who think fast have terrible handwriting. It’s a structural problem — the hand can’t keep up with the mind, so the mind wins and the letters lose. You’ve solved it by writing smaller.”
I looked at my notes in her hands. My handwriting was, objectively, quite small. “You’re reading them.”
“I am.” She did not stop.
“Without asking.”
“Also correct.” She turned another page. “You’ve organized these by question urgency, which is smart, but the sequence has a flaw. You’ve put the foundational question at the bottom because you think it requires all the others to be answered first.” She looked up properly this time, both membrane-colored eyes on mine, and the directness of it was different from Aleksei’s directness — his was the directness of something contained, carefully managed. Hers was simply the directness of something that had decided eye contact was the most efficient use of the available space. “You have it backwards. The foundational question is the one everything else hangs off. It goes first.”
I crossed to the table. I sat down in the other chair — the right-side one, not my chair, but available — and I looked at her across my own notes. “Who are you,” I said.
She closed the notes. Not quickly — with the deliberate care of someone who understood that other people’s things were other people’s things given that she had been reading them uninvited for however long she’d been here. She set them on the table between us. “Sable,” she said. “I’m a Remnant. Don’t worry, most people haven’t heard of them — it’s not a common category. I’ll explain.”
“Please,” I said.
She explained.
* * *
It took, as she had apparently estimated in advance, approximately thirty seconds.
She delivered it directly, without preamble, with the specific economy of someone who valued efficiency because she had spent her life in environments where inefficiency cost you things you could not afford to lose. She was, I was beginning to understand, a person who had been shaped by scarcity into something precise.
“How old are you,” I said, when she finished.
“Older than the Hall,” she said. “Older than Aleksei. Younger than the book on the third shelf from the left, which predates everything currently in existence except the first world itself, and I wouldn’t recommend touching it until you’ve got more of the Claim active.” She said this last part the way you said something that had been established, a known fact being referenced rather than introduced. Then she paused, with the manner of someone registering that she had said something the other person did not have context for. “Ah,” she said. “You don’t know about the Claim yet.”
“I know about the binding,” I said carefully. “And the Old Claim — Aleksei mentioned it. In the context of suggesting there was more to tell.”
“There is considerably more to tell,” Sable confirmed. “He’ll tell you when he has the right words. He’s working on them. He’s been working on them since the second day, which is slow even for him, but the subject matter is genuinely complicated and he is — ” She paused. She seemed to be selecting from a range of options. “He is someone who takes words seriously,” she said — a sentence with a great deal of room in it that I was going to look at from several angles later. “He won’t give you the wrong ones.”
I looked at her across the table. She looked back with those membrane-colored eyes that pulled at my recognition the way the old-language book pulled at it — the reaching-toward, the almost.
“You know him,” I said.
“Eight hundred years,” she said. “Give or take. He built the Hall here because of the membrane-thinning in the east corridor, and the membrane-thinning in the east corridor is where I’m most comfortable, and the intersection of those two facts has made us neighbors since before he finished the east wing.” She glanced at the door, in the direction of the east corridor, with a look that was fond in the specific way of fondness that had been tested regularly and had held. “He tried to make me leave twice. Once in the first century and once about four hundred years ago, when he was going through something. I didn’t leave either time.” A pause. “He hasn’t tried a third time.”
“He just gave up,” I said.
“He recognized that some situations don’t resolve,” she said, which sounded like something she had told him directly at some point and that he had received with the flat acceptance of a person presented with accurate information he did not like. “I’m fairly immovable when I’ve decided to be somewhere. It’s a membrane thing. Once a Remnant is anchored to a space, the space is part of their register, and you’d have to move the space itself to move the Remnant.” She considered. “He has not yet attempted to move the east corridor.”
“I imagine he’s thought about it,” I said.
Something shifted in her expression — not quite a smile but in the same family. “Every decade or so,” she agreed.
* * *
“Why are you here tonight,” I said. “Specifically. In the library.”
“Because you touched the mark on the second page of the first-language book this afternoon,” she said, “and the mark activated, and I felt it from the membrane’s surface, and I thought it was worth coming to see.”
“From the eastern membrane. I’m sensitive to first-language activations — old reflex, from before the separation. The first language was the only language then, and Remnants are calibrated for it.” She tilted her head slightly, which sent her hair — the not-black Hollow-deep color of it — shifting at the edges in a way that was almost luminescent. Not quite. “You received beginning,” she said. Not a question.
“Yes,” I said. I had not told anyone this. Aleksei knew through the Hall, and whatever Aleksei knew Vasilisa probably knew, but I had not said it to anyone directly. I was saying it now without deciding to, which was information about what Sable produced in the people around her — some quality that made the usual management of information feel like unnecessary overhead.
“The first mark on the second page,” she said. “Of course it was. The whole first page is an introduction that doesn’t give you anything until you’ve already understood everything, which is typical of the first world’s approach to communication. Very efficient if you already know. Very unhelpful if you don’t.” She looked at the shelf where the book sat, the same assessing look I’d been directing at it for a week. “You put it back exactly.”
“I always put things back exactly,” I said.
“I know.” She looked at me again. “So does the Hall. So does he.”
This landed with the specific weight of something placed carefully. Not thrown, not dropped — set down, with precision, in a position it was going to stay in. I chose not to pick it up yet. “You said I’m asking the wrong question,” I said instead. “The one at the bottom of the sequence.”
“You’ve put it at the bottom because you think answering it requires everything above it as foundation,” she said. “You’re approaching it academically. Establish the smaller knowns, build toward the larger unknown. It’s a sound method for most things.”
“But not for this.”
“Not for this,” she confirmed. “The question at the bottom of your sequence — I saw it, you wrote it after the mark activated, it’s the last thing on the third page — is the question that answers most of the ones above it automatically. You don’t need the foundation first. The question is the foundation.”
I thought about the question. About the three words I had written at the bottom of the third page in my smallest handwriting, in the margin, after sitting very still for a long time.
Why can I almost read that book.
“What’s the right question,” I said.
Sable looked at me for a moment. The membrane-colored eyes were very steady. “We’ll get there,” she said.
“That’s not an answer.”
“It’s a timeline,” she said. “There’s a difference.” She picked up my notes again, which I was deciding to interpret as a positive sign about the direction of things rather than a boundary violation, because she had set them down with care and was picking them up with care and the picking-up-with-care carried the bearing of someone engaging with something they intended to treat properly. “The right question requires some groundwork first. I’ll give you the groundwork over the next few weeks and then you’ll arrive at the right question on your own, which will be better than if I just told you.”
“Better for whom.”
“You,” she said. “Some things have to be found rather than given. This is one of them.” She turned a page. “Have you eaten? You’ve been in here since the afternoon and the Hall said you didn’t come to the main room for food.”
I stared at her. “The Hall told you that.”
“The Hall talks to me,” she said simply. “I’ve been here eight hundred years. We have an arrangement.” She glanced up. “It’s also extremely obvious that you haven’t eaten — you get a particular quality of focused when you’re running low that’s very recognizable once you know to look for it. All mortals do it. You hold still a little more than usual and your blinks get slightly slower.”
I processed this. “You can tell how hungry I am by my blink rate.”
“I’ve been paying attention to mortals for longer than most,” she said, with a serenity I was going to find either deeply comforting or deeply alarming and had not yet decided which. “It’s not surveillance. It’s pattern recognition.”
“There’s a fine line.”
“There is,” she agreed, pleasantly. “Food. Let’s go.”
* * *
We ate in the main hall, because the food was there and because the main hall was large enough that two people eating in it did not feel like an imposition on the space’s other purposes. The Hall’s presences were, I noticed immediately, behaving differently in Sable’s presence — not the careful quiet they used around me when I was managing something, not the warm approval they produced when I did something they found correct, but the particular feel of things that had been waiting for a particular person to arrive and were now, with the subdued satisfaction of beings who did not express satisfaction loudly, registering that she had.
“They missed you,” I said.
Sable looked at the upper reaches of the main hall with an expression I was beginning to identify as the one she used for things she had feelings about and was choosing not to produce those feelings in full view. “I was here six months ago,” she said. “They have a long memory.”
“Six months doesn’t seem long,” I said. “Given the timescales everyone here seems to operate on.”
“Six months is very long to something that was alone for three hundred years before that,” she said, in the even tone she used for most things and that I was learning did not indicate the absence of feeling but rather the very precise management of it. The presences warmed. She looked at her food.
I looked at my food as well, because she had not looked away from hers for the specific reason of giving herself somewhere to look that wasn’t at me, and the courtesy of the moment required giving her somewhere to look that wasn’t at me noticing her.
We ate in silence for a few minutes. It was a comfortable silence, which surprised me, because I had met this person approximately forty minutes ago and comfortable silence was the kind of thing I associated with people I had known for years and trusted completely. Sable produced comfortable silence the way Aleksei produced productive silence — not by working at it but by existing in a way that made the space around her feel like it was being used rather than unfilled.
“The coat,” she said.
I looked up.
She was looking at the coat — the coat I was wearing, the coat I was always wearing, the coat that was mine now through what I maintained was a genuine transfer of ownership and not anything else. Her expression was the same neutral-with-something-underneath she had been managing since we sat down.
“He gave it to you on the third day,” she said.
“How do you know that,” I said, in exactly the tone I had used when she knew about the notes, because some situations deserved the same response they had produced before.
“The Hall talks,” she said, the same answer as before with the same easy delivery. “You just have to know how to listen.” She picked up her cup, looked into it. “That coat is two hundred years old. He made it himself — he has a period, around the three-hundred-year mark for most Vorath, where they go through an intensive craft phase. Aleksei’s was textiles. Very specific. He doesn’t do it anymore.”
I looked at the coat. I thought about the fact that he had made it with his own hands in what was apparently a phase of his existence that no longer existed. I thought about him holding it out in the dark at the boundary of the Hall’s grounds with the flat declaration of a person for whom the decision was already made.
“He gave away something he made,” I said.
“He gave away something he made,” she confirmed. “On the third day.” A pause. “Because it suited you better.”
I stared at her. He had said that to me directly, the morning of the rules briefing, and I had been sitting with it ever since and had not told anyone, not Tomasz through the mirror, not the presences, not my notes — I had written around it, written about the coat, written about the transfer of ownership, but I had not written those words. “He told you that,” I said.
“He didn’t have to,” she said. “I told you — the Hall talks. And I know him.” She set down her cup. “He has not given anyone anything he made since before the sealed wing had a reason to be sealed. That’s one hundred and fifty years of not giving anyone anything.” She looked at me steadily. “He gave you the coat on the third day.”
I opened my mouth. I closed it. This was the warmth of things that had been paying attention to the same situation for a week and were relieved that someone was finally saying it.
“I don’t know what you want me to do with that,” I said.
“Nothing yet,” Sable said. “It’s just groundwork.” She picked up her food again with the matter-of-fact quality of someone who had said what they needed to say and was now going to eat dinner and let it settle. “What’s the shadow-fox doing now? It was at the treeline when I came in.”
I was sufficiently grateful for the subject change that I answered immediately. “It was there this morning too. I pressed my hand to the glass and it tilted its head.”
“It tilted its head,” Sable said, with the specific warmth of someone receiving confirmation of something they had suspected. “Has it moved closer since yesterday?”
“A few feet,” I said. “Maybe.” I had not been actively measuring. I had been looking at it each morning without quite deciding to and noting its position without quite deciding to and I was absolutely measuring.
“It’ll be at the grounds’ edge within the week,” she said. “And at the Hall’s entrance within the month. They move slowly when they’re being careful.” She glanced toward the window that faced south — the one I had not approached from this room yet, the one I had been in the habit of not approaching because it was too close to the territory that felt like his rather than mine. “It’s being careful.”
“Why,” I said.
“Because you matter to it,” she said simply. “Things that matter require care.”
I sat with this. The Hall’s presences were warm. The Hollow’s sky outside the south window was cycling toward its deepest color, the purple that pressed, and somewhere to the north — close, stationary, the north study — the binding put Aleksei still at his work.
“Sable,” I said.
“Mm.”
“The groundwork you’re going to give me.” I was choosing my words with the care she had used for hers, because she had earned that care and because I was finding that she was the kind of person who noticed when care had been taken. “The question that goes first instead of last. Is it going to change things.”
She was quiet for a moment. The real kind of quiet — not management, not strategy, the kind that meant she was going to say something she actually meant rather than something she had decided to say. “Yes,” she said.
“In what direction.”
She looked at me across the table in the main hall of an eight-hundred-year-old Hall in a realm that ran by different rules, with her membrane-colored eyes and her almost-translucent edges and the particular feel of someone who had been caught in the moment between two worlds for long enough that they could see both clearly. “The right direction,” she said. “I wouldn’t be here if it weren’t.”
I nodded. I picked up my food.
We ate. And Sable, between courses, mentioned without emphasis that the eastern boundary had been active overnight. Not a breach. Pressure. Someone testing response times.
* * *
She stayed until the sky reached its deepest indigo, and then she stood and gathered nothing — she had brought nothing, she had arrived with only herself and apparently needed only herself — and moved toward the door with the particular quality of motion she had, the slightly-not-fully-solid quality that was not graceless but was different from the way solid things moved.
“I’ll come back tomorrow,” she said.
“I didn’t invite you,” I said.
She smiled then — the first full smile, the real one, and it was different from everything I had seen her produce so far in the same way that the real versions of things were always different from the managed ones. It changed her whole face. “No,” she agreed. “You didn’t.”
She left. The main hall settled. The presences, which had been warm and attentive since she arrived, produced a register I had not felt from them before — something like the exhale of a long breath, the particular feel of things that had been waiting and were now, quietly, not waiting anymore.
I sat at the table for a while after she left.
I thought about the coat and the two hundred years and the third day and the sealed wing. I thought about the question at the bottom of my sequence and the right question that went first. I thought about the right direction, and what it meant that someone who had existed since before the world had its current shape thought this situation was worth coming to see.
Beginning of what, I did not know yet.
I was going to find out.
I went to bed with the coat on and the fox watching the middle distance and the shadow-fox watching the window and the binding steady in my awareness — Aleksei in the north study, close, stationary, still working. Still there.
The Hall held its warmth in the way it had been warm since she left: the way of something that had exhaled.
I was, for the first time in seven days, not alone.
I had not known, until the moment I wasn’t, how much I had been.
And now that I knew, I would have to decide what to do with the knowing.
Except that the binding shifted — not her location, not her state. Something finer. A note I had not read before, quiet and particular.
She was dreaming. And the binding was telling me what the dream concerned.
I closed the channel. Immediately. Some information was not mine to have.
But I had already felt what it was, and it was not rest. It was the sub-linguistic register — the same frequency the book used. She was dreaming in first-language.
