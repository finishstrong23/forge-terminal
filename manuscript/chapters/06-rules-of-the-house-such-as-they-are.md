# Chapter Six: Rules of the House, Such As They Are

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 3,879 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 7

---

Three days into my stay in the Hollow, Aleksei summoned me to the Hall’s main room and explained the rules.
I say summoned. The binding, I had established in three days of paying close attention to it, communicated in states rather than signals. He was not calling me. He was simply there, and stationary, and the distinction between that and a summons was technically meaningful and practically negligible.
I came down.
He was standing near the main hall’s center table — not sitting, not leaning, positioned with the deliberate quality of someone who had chosen that specific distance from every wall and every door. The amber of his eyes in the Hall’s luminescence had a quality I could now name: attention. Not surveillance. Care.
I had been thinking about this — about the precise nature of the wrongness he carried, the way it was not threatening exactly but was not safe either, the way you could look at him for a long time and still feel like you hadn’t quite seen him. Three days of cataloguing and I was not significantly closer to a framework that worked.
“Sit,” he said.
“I don’t sit.”
I had not seen him sit in three days, which meant this was likely accurate. “Why not?”
“It doesn’t particularly serve a purpose.”
I thought about this. It was, objectively, a defensible position. “All right,” I said. I sat. He continued not sitting. We were now at different heights. He was looking slightly down at me, which I noted as a dynamic I was going to keep track of. “What are the rules?”
He told me the rules.
* * *
They were delivered in the same mode as the law on the first night: efficiently, completely, without editorializing. The formal briefing of a person who had done this before and intended to do it correctly. Some of the rules I had already established for myself through three days of exploration. Others I had not.
The territory of Nochval extended to a boundary that I would feel as a change in the air rather than see as a physical marker. Within this boundary I could go anywhere I chose, at any time, unaccompanied. Beyond it: no. I filed this under things I intended to acquire the ability to do.
The Hall itself: open to me with two exceptions, which I had already identified from the feeling he had described — the closed heaviness behind certain doors. One of them was on the south corridor, second floor. One was on the east face, at the Hall’s far end. I had found both and had not pressed against either. “What’s in them,” I said.
“Things that are not ready to be opened,” he said.
“Ready,” I said. “As in the things inside have opinions about it.”
“As in I do,” he said.
I received this. “Fair,” I said, because it was fair — he was not obligated to open every room in his Hall to a stranger he’d acquired through legal accident three nights ago, and I was not going to press it. I was going to note it, which I did, in the running list I kept in my head alongside the one I kept on paper: two closed rooms, his rules rather than the Hall’s, not yet.
The not yet was mine again, appearing in my thinking without being invited. I was going to stop noticing that and start noting it, which was different.
Communication with Zemya: a mirror in the Hall’s east corridor, second door from the main stair. It worked once in every three days. He had already ensured that Tomasz had been told to look at his bedroom mirror at a specific time. I had three days of messages stored up and was going to deploy them all at once.
“Tomasz,” I said. “My — he’s a fence. He’s known me for six years.” I stopped. “Why did you find him specifically. You didn’t know his name.”
A pause. “The binding,” he said — not an answer. The binding told him my general wellbeing, not my social network. I looked at him. He held my gaze with the flat silver attention he deployed when he had said what he intended to say and was not going to elaborate. I added this to the list.
The other Vorath: I was likely to encounter them. The Hunt had included six others, of whom one — Radovan, the large one who had been waiting at the territory’s edge when we arrived — was likely to visit Nochval. “Is he going to be a problem?” I asked.
“Radovan,” Aleksei said, and something in the delivery of the name had a quality I was beginning to learn, a very slight shift in register that indicated something approaching, but not reaching, warmth. “No. He won’t be a problem.” A pause. “He’ll be irritating in a different way.”
I found this remarkably interesting. “What way?”
“He’ll want to talk to you,” Aleksei said, with the air of someone describing an inconvenience they had resigned themselves to. “He’s curious about most things and has limited patience for the convention of not expressing it.”
“You mean he’s friendly.”
“I mean he has poor impulse control for someone who has been alive nine hundred years,” Aleksei said. “But yes. Broadly.”
I filed Radovan in the potential assets column and moved on.
* * *
The rules continued. They communicated in impressions, not words. If something in the Hall felt like it was trying to direct me somewhere, it was probably worth following. He said this with the flat certainty of someone stating a thing that had been reliably true for a very long time.
“How long have the presences been here,” I asked.
“Since the Hall was built.”
“When was the Hall built.”
“Eight hundred years ago,” he said. “Approximately.”
I thought about eight-hundred-year-old presences and decided I was probably fine with them, given that the three-day acquaintance so far had consisted of them occasionally making a door open before I reached it and once, memorably, redirecting a draught away from the candle I was reading by. They had opinions. So did I. We were going to get along.
“The Hollow outside the Hall,” I said. “The training for navigating it — you mentioned that.”
“We’ll begin in a week,” he said. “When you’ve had time to acclimate.”
“I’ve acclimated.”
The look he gave me was brief and entirely without expression and communicated, somehow, that he disagreed. “A week,” he said.
I was going to argue this and then I looked at the silver eyes and the specific quality of the not-expression in his face and I made the calculation that this was a battle I would come back to with better ammunition. “Fine,” I said. “A week. What do I do in the meantime.”
“Whatever you like,” he said, as if this were obvious.
“In a strange building in a realm I don’t understand.”
“You’ve found the library,” he said.
I looked at him. He looked at me. He had not been to the library while I was in it. I had checked, through the binding — when he was in the library, the directional sense would put him there, and he had not been there. He had not seen me there. And yet.
“How do you know that,” I said.
“The Hall,” he said simply.
“The Hall tells you where I’ve been.”
“The Hall has a general awareness of what occurs within it,” he said. “I am not monitoring your movements. The Hall’s awareness is passive — it tells me what it finds significant.”
“And me finding the library,” I said, “was significant.”
Something happened at the corner of his expression. Very small. The adjacency-of-a-smile that I’d clocked twice now, the thing that was not a smile and that occupied the precise space where a smile would go on the face of a person who allowed themselves smiles. “The Hall,” he said carefully, “has opinions about who finds the library and how they do it.”
“And I found it correctly.”
“You found it,” he said, “by smell, which is the correct way to find it.”
I stared at him. “Did you just—” I started.
“The rules,” he said, returning to his previous register with the care of a door closing. “Are substantially covered. Do you have questions.”
I had several questions. I organized them by priority.
The first was about the library. Not because the library was the most important room — because it was the room he had not mentioned.
* * *
“The binding awareness,” I said. “The general location and wellbeing. Does it work when you’re asleep.”
“I don’t sleep in the conventional sense,” he said. “But yes, the binding is constant regardless of my state.”
“So you knew I was in the library yesterday.”
“I knew you were in the library.”
“And in the corridor before that.”
“And in the corridor.”
“And this morning, when I was—” I stopped. I had been in the small courtyard off the east wing at dawn, the one I’d found through a door that the Hall had very helpfully left ajar, and I had been doing the exercises I did every morning, the routine I’d built over years of needing to be fast and functional at a moment’s notice, and I had been entirely alone and entirely certain of it and apparently I had been wrong about the certain of it part.
“The binding is constant,” he said, in the tone of someone who had already said this and was not going to apologize for it.
“Right,” I said. “And I have the corresponding awareness of you.”
“The directional sense, yes.”
“Which means,” I said, “that you have also been aware that I have the directional sense, and that I have been tracking your general location through the binding.”
He looked at me steadily. “Yes.”
“And you didn’t mention it.”
“You didn’t ask.”
I looked at him. He looked at me. The exchange had a quality to it that I was beginning to recognize — the specific frequency of two people who understood each other and found the understanding slightly inconvenient. “I’ve been paying attention to where you are,” I said. Not an accusation. A fact.
“I know,” he said. Not an accusation. A fact.
“That’s very mutual of us,” I said.
The corner of his expression did the thing again. Faster this time, and slightly more definite. I was getting better at catching it rather than that he was losing control of it. “It is,” he said, “the nature of the binding.”
“Sure,” I said. “The binding.”
I was aware that I was dangerously close to something I had decided not to examine, and I retreated to safer ground.
* * *
“The fox,” I said.
He had been about to continue the rules. He stopped. The quality of his stillness, which I was developing some fluency in, adjusted — not dramatically, nothing he did was dramatic, but the calibration of it changed in the way that a room’s atmosphere changed when the subject shifted to something that required more attention.
“In my room,” I said. “On the windowsill.”
“Yes,” he said.
“It wasn’t there when I arrived. The first night.”
“No.”
“You put it there,” I said. Also not an accusation. I was trying to establish facts.
“Yes.”
“Why.”
He was quiet for a moment. Not the strategic quiet of someone buying time — Aleksei didn’t buy time, he either spoke or he didn’t, and when he didn’t the silence was final and you moved on. This was a different silence. The silence of someone choosing between versions of an answer.
“It belonged to someone I knew,” he said.
Three days in I had already developed the vocabulary to identify this as a partial answer — true, clearly, but occupying only a fraction of the available space. There was a great deal of room around it. I could push. I was good at pushing; I had made a professional practice of locating the place where the pressure would produce results and applying it with precision.
I did not push.
Some information revealed itself when pushed. Other information closed. I was learning, which category he belonged to — a process that was going to take longer than three days and that I was — apparently willing to take.
“It’s well made,” I said instead.
Something in him settled — a small, subtle thing, the particular quality of stillness that was different from his default stillness, less managed and more simply present. “It is,” he said.
“Whoever made it cared about it.”
“Yes.”
I left it there. He left it there. The not-uncomfortable. Three days in and we had a silence about something that was already not-uncomfortable.
I was going to need to think about what that meant.
Later. But later was getting closer. The questions were accumulating faster than the answers, and the gap between the two was starting to feel structural.
* * *
The rest of the rules were practical. I asked each question I had organized in priority order and he answered each in kind, and somewhere in the exchange the formal briefing became something else — something closer to an actual conversation, with the specific quality of two people who were both aware that it had shifted and neither of whom was going to acknowledge that it had shifted, because acknowledging it would require deciding what it meant.
He told me about the Hollow’s weather — not weather in the mortal sense — not fronts and pressures and the predictable physics of a world that operated by consistent rules — but moods, the Hollow’s own atmospheric states, which arrived without warning and were readable if you knew what to read. He told me that the Hollow ran cold at depth and warm at the surface and that I would learn to feel the difference. He told me that the creatures of Nochval — which I had not seen yet, which were apparently numerous and largely not dangerous and in some cases genuinely strange — would be interested in me and that I should let them approach on their own terms and not pursue.
“What do they look like,” I said. “The creatures.”
“Varied,” he said. “Some you will recognize from mortal stories — the stories got the shapes approximately correct. Others have no mortal equivalent.”
“What’s the closest mortal equivalent to the thing most likely to approach me first.”
He considered this with genuine thought rather than the look of someone producing a convenient answer. “A fox,” he said. “Made of shadow and cold light. It’s been in Nochval’s territory for several decades. It’s curious about unusual things.”
I held his gaze. He held mine. I thought about the carved fox on my windowsill, and the shadow-creature he had just described, and the word unusual landing with the specific emphasis of something placed rather than incidental. “A fox,” I said.
“Yes.”
“Shadow and cold light.”
“Yes.”
“Curious about unusual things.”
“Generally.”
I looked at him for a moment. There was something in this exchange — in the layering of it, the carved fox and the shadow-fox and the word unusual — that I was not yet equipped to read. I could feel the shape of it without its substance. “Good to know,” I said.
“Yes,” he said, and there was something in that yes, something very nearly not-flat, that I was going to think about later when I had time to think about things properly — a category that was expanding.
* * *
We had been in the main hall for somewhere between an hour and two — the Hollow’s sky was giving me a little more to work with now, three days in, enough to approximate — when the rules were, as far as I could tell, substantially covered. He showed no signs of ending the conversation, which I noted, and I showed no signs of ending it either, which I also noted, and for a few minutes we sat — he stood, I sat — in a silence that had completely stopped being the silence of a legal briefing and was now just the silence of two people in the same room who had run out of agenda and had not yet decided what to do about that.
“The coat,” I said. Because I had been trying to find an entry point to this question for three days and had not found a better one.
He looked at the coat. I was wearing it — I had been wearing it since I came down, for warmth, because the main hall was colder than my room and I had made the practical decision to wear it rather than carry it and present it at a significant juncture. This was my justification and I was not examining it.
“Can I keep it,” I said.
“It’s yours,” he said.
“You said that already.”
“I meant it then as well.”
“I mean permanently,” I said. “Not — I’m not asking to borrow it. I’m asking if you actually want it back at some point or whether this is a genuine transfer of ownership, because those are different things and I’d like to be clear on which one is happening.”
He looked at me for a moment with the specific look I was cataloguing as his thinking look — not the flat assessment he deployed for observations, but something with more activity behind it. Then: “It’s yours,” he said. “Permanently. A genuine transfer of ownership, if you want to call it that.”
“Why,” I said.
“You were cold.”
“I was cold once,” I said. “On the night we arrived. I’m not cold every day. The coat is considerably finer than anything I’d wear in Zemya, and it’s yours, and I’d like to understand the logic of giving it away rather than lending it.”
He was quiet for a moment. The Hall’s presences, which I was beginning to develop some awareness of — a slight warmth in the air, a quality of attention — were paying very close attention. I had the distinct impression that I had wandered into something that the Hall found interesting, the same impression I’d had when I asked about the fox, and I stored it in the same place.
“The logic,” he said, “is that I have had the coat for two hundred years and you have had it for three days, and it already suits you better.”
I opened my mouth. I closed it. I looked down at the coat, which was dark and dense and draped across my shoulders in the way of something that had been made for a significantly larger person and had, through some mechanism I was not going to examine, managed to fit anyway. I looked back up at him.
He was already turning away, toward the corridor that led to the north wing, which was where the binding told me he spent most of his time. His shoulders were level. His hands were at his sides. He was, as far as I could tell, entirely composed.
“Aleksei,” I said.
He stopped. He did not turn around.
“Thank you,” I said. “For the coat.”
A pause. I watched his back, the set of it, the specific quality of the stillness he produced when something had landed differently than he had anticipated. It lasted perhaps two seconds.
“You’re welcome,” he said, to the corridor.
And then he was gone, and the main hall was empty, and the Hall’s presences were doing the thing they did when they were satisfied about something, which I had come to recognize as a particular warmth in the air, a quality of the ambient light becoming slightly warmer than its default.
I sat alone in the main hall for a moment.
The coat was mine. He had given it away because it suited me better than it suited him. He had owned it for two hundred years. He had given it away because it suited me better. Two hundred years and three days, and he had given it away because it suited me better.
I pulled it slightly tighter around my shoulders, with both hands, slowly, like a person doing something for a completely practical reason.
“Right,” I said to the empty hall, to the warm air, to the presences that were definitely listening. “Good. Fine. That’s fine.”
The Hall was warm. It stayed warm all the way back to my room, which was longer than the walk should have taken, in the way that distances in the Hall occasionally became negotiable when the Hall had opinions about your pace.
I did not hurry it along.
* * *
That night I sat at my desk and added to my list.
Not new entries — I had enough new entries, the list was at risk of becoming more list than page. What I added was to an existing line, the one that read:
The fox. What it is. Why it is here. Why he looked at it first.
Below it, in my normal handwriting rather than the small marginal script I used for things I wasn’t sure deserved a line:
Also: the shadow-fox. Curious about unusual things.
And below that, after a moment in which I held the pen above the paper and thought about whether to write it and wrote it:
He called me unusual.
I looked at this for a while. Then I put the pen down and looked at the fox on the windowsill, which was sitting in its composed and deliberate way with its ears forward and its tail curled, attending to the middle distance.
“Don’t look at me like that,” I said.
The fox, being carved from wood and dead for some years, did not respond.
I went to bed.
I was wearing the coat.
The Hollow’s sky outside the window moved through its deep, cycling colors — the blues and greens and the purple that pressed against the eye like a held note — and at the edge of the silver forest, just at the treeline, something sat in the dark that was not quite shadow and not quite light and watched the Hall with patient, interested eyes.
I did not know this then.
I found out in the morning, when I looked out the window first thing and it was still there, and I pressed my hand to the glass, and the not-quite-shadow creature tilted its head, ears forward, attending.
It looked exactly like the fox on my windowsill.
Of course it did.
And then, as I watched it through the glass, it tilted its head very slightly — not toward me, toward something else. The direction of the north study. The direction where Aleksei was.
Reporting.
The shadow-fox was not just watching me. It was reporting what it watched. Every morning at the window. Every moment at the treeline. Every detail of what I did and how I did it.
I pressed my hand to the glass. The shadow-fox’s ears came forward, attending. Patient. Composed.
I had not been unobserved for a single moment since I arrived. And the fox was not the only thing watching.
