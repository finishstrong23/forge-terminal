# Chapter Seven: What I Know About Gods, and How Wrong I Was

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 4,670 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 10

---

The shadow-fox was still at the treeline when I woke up.
I had established this conclusively at dawn — or what passed for dawn in the Hollow — the sky shifting from its deepest indigo toward something closer to the blue of very old glass, slow and considered, like a color deciding to become another color rather than simply changing. The creature was in the same position it had been in when I’d first seen it the night before: sitting, tail around its feet, ears forward, the composed and patient quality of something that had decided to be somewhere and was going to be there until it had a reason not to be.
I stood at the window for a long time, watching it watch the Hall.
It was not threatening. What it had was interest. The particular quality of something that had found something new and was paying attention to it with the patience that came from having no more pressing business.
The no-more-pressing-business part was doing something to my sense of scale. I was the most interesting thing in this territory. I, Kira Voss, low-city thief and retriever of unreturned fees, was the most noteworthy development in an ancient, living realm in recent memory. This was not a position I had previously occupied, and I was not sure the pay was worth the exposure and I was finding it unexpectedly complicated.
I pressed my palm flat against the glass.
The shadow-fox tilted its head. Both ears swiveled forward, tracking the movement. Then it settled again, and the composed attending resumed, and I stood at the window with my hand on the cold glass until the morning got proper enough that I had things to do.
Aleksei was north and far, per the binding. He had been north and far since before I woke. He was occupying a part of Nochval I had not yet been to. I had not tried to go there yet, because the binding told me direction and distance but not invitation, and I had decided that occupying space he was currently in without being invited to was something I would establish a precedent for carefully and at a later date.
For now: he was elsewhere. The Hall was mine.
I put on the coat — for warmth, and also because I was keeping it, and you kept things by using them, that was the principle — and I went to explore.
* * *
I had been through the Hall’s main corridors twice now, enough to have a working map of the central spaces. What I had not done was spend time in any of them properly — I had moved through, catalogued, moved on. You could know the shape of a place without knowing what it was like to be in it, and knowing what it was like to be in a place was, I had found, the more useful information.
So I started over, more slowly.
The main hall first — the great central space with the vaulted ceiling that disappeared into the Hollow’s ambient dark above, the accumulated objects along the walls, the table and chairs that had the worn quality of furniture used over centuries. I sat in one of the chairs. I put my feet on the table, because I could, and looked up at the ceiling, and thought about the eight hundred years of people who had and hadn’t sat in this room.
The presences were there. Three of them, I thought, though counting was approximate. They were in the far corners and along the upper reaches of the walls, and they were paying attention to me sitting at the table with the weight of something that found this mildly remarkable.
“Hello,” I said.
The air did something. A slight warming, a particular quality of attention that sharpened without becoming louder. Not a response in any language I had. But not nothing either.
“I’m going to be here for a year,” I told them. “I thought you should know. In case it changes anything about your plans.”
The warmth in the air became slightly more definite. I had the distinct impression that this was the most interesting thing to happen to this room in some time, and that the presences had opinions about it, and that the opinions were, on balance, favorable.
“Good,” I said. “That’s settled then.”
I took my feet off the table, because I had points to make and manners were a separate issue, and I went to look more carefully at the objects along the walls.
* * *
The sword I had noticed before. Up close it was older than I had estimated — the style of it was not from any civilization I could date, the hilt too long and the blade too wide by modern standards, the balance suggesting it had been made for hands or habits that no longer existed. Maintained. Recently. Someone took the sword down and tended to it.
I looked at the sword for a while and thought about Aleksei maintaining a sword that was older than most civilizations, and I held this in the place I was keeping things that complicated my picture of him. The pile there was substantial and growing.
The shelves. I went through them slowly — picking things up, turning them over, putting them back. Most of it was unreadable. Three objects were not objects at all but presences, sleeping in forms that looked like decorative vessels.
I put it back carefully and moved on.
The hourglass with the stopped sand: still stopped. I looked at it from several angles. The sand was at the bottom. The glass had either run out or been stopped partway, and I could not tell which. I turned the glass over in my hands. The sand did not move. I turned it right side up again. Still nothing.
“What stops it?” I asked the room.
The presences gave me the character of warmth that I was learning to read as: we know, and it’s not our information to give. Which was, I was finding, the answer the Hall’s small inhabitants produced for a considerable proportion of my questions. They knew a great deal and they had a refined sense of what belonged to whom.
I put the hourglass back and made a note to ask about it later.
* * *
The east corridor had a quality to it I had not paid attention to on previous passes — something about the air there, a particular density that was not unpleasant but was notable in the way of something that had accumulated over a long time. Not the closed heaviness of the two rooms that were not ready. Something different. The air in the east corridor had the feel of a room where a great deal had happened and the walls had been paying attention.
I walked it slowly. The corridor was longer than it looked — the Hall did this, made distances negotiable when it had opinions about pace. Three doorways: one open, one locked, one that opened when I touched the handle.
I stood in the doorway of the window room for a while.
The silver forest from this angle was different — the trees shorter, the spaces between them wider, the luminescence dimmer at ground level. And the oldest trees, the ones at the territory’s core, were the ones where the dimming was worst.
There was a stillness at the ground level that was wrong. The ground beneath the oldest trees was not moving when it should have been. I didn’t have a baseline for what it should have felt like, but my body registered the difference the way it registered the wrong streets in Vreshka — with the weight of something off.
I wanted to go in there.
This was interesting — not the want itself — a predictable response to something beautiful, but the sense of it. It was not curiosity exactly. It was more like recognition. The specific pull toward a place that felt familiar in the way that the old-language book had felt familiar, the sense of something reaching toward the back of my mind where I kept things I didn’t know I knew.
I would ask about the trees later. I walked on.
* * *
The Hall’s presences, I discovered, were more mobile than I had understood.
I had assumed they occupied their corners and their upper walls with the specificity of things that had found their places and stayed in them. This was wrong. They moved. It was the kind of movement you detected by noticing that the quality of warmth in the air had shifted, that the sense of occupied attention was now coming from a slightly different part of the space.
They were following me.
Not in the way that things followed you when they intended something — not the heavy, directed attention of a threat or an intention. More the way a cat followed you through a house, maintaining proximity not because it wanted anything specific but because it had decided your movement was the most interesting variable in the current environment and it intended to stay adjacent to that variable.
“You don’t have to keep up,” I told the air in the east corridor. “I’m just looking at things.”
The warmth, which had been tracking at approximately a shoulder’s distance to my left, did something that felt like amusement. As much as warmth could feel like anything.
“That’s fair,” I said. “I’m also just looking at things.”
I had been in Vreshka for twenty-three years. I had talked to shadows in dark alleys, to the absence of light at the edges of rooms, to the Hollow-adjacent wrongness of certain streets in the lower city that everyone knew about and nobody acknowledged. This was not so different. It was more domestic. It was, if anything, warmer.
The presences followed me through the rest of the east corridor, through a short connecting passage that smelled of cold stone and something faintly mineral, and back around toward the main hall. They stayed near the high walls and the accumulated objects. The corridors they moved through. There was a distinction, and it mattered to them, and I had passed through it without noticing and they had accompanied me anyway.
“Thank you for the tour,” I said.
The air in the main hall was warm.
* * *
I went to the library deliberately this time.
The first visit had been by smell — the correct way, apparently, though I was not above taking directions when they were accurate. This time I went because I had decided to go, which was different, and I went the fastest route rather than the exploratory one because I knew the route now and the library was where I was spending the day and I was not going to pretend otherwise.
I had brought paper.
I settled at the table on the left — the smaller of the two, the one that had the best light and the closest access to the shelves I most wanted to spend time with — and I arranged myself with the paper and the pen I’d taken from my room and the intention of doing something that was going to take several hours. Then I looked at the shelves.
The Hollow’s sky was in its mid-blue register — midday, the deepest saturation before the shift toward evening. Six weeks and it still surprised me. I should not have been thinking about what that meant. But not right now.
Right now: the shelves.
I started at the far left of the room and I worked my way along the floor-level shelves first — the ones most likely to have been organized by someone using them regularly, the ones most accessible without a step stool or the kind of reach that suggested you were very tall or not quite mortal in the manner of your proportions. The books here were in a mixture of languages: two I read fluently, three I could navigate slowly, two more I had enough of to identify the subject matter if not the specifics, and several that I could not place at all.
I pulled the readable ones and stacked them on the table. History, mostly — the Hollow’s record of itself, written in a language that felt more like music than text. And one book that was not history. One book that the shelf held differently, with the careful grip of a structure holding something significant.
I read for two hours. I took notes. I drank the tea that one of the presences had apparently arranged — when I looked up from the historian’s account, there was a cup on the table that had not been there before, filled with something that tasted like the mortal tea I’d been drinking in Vreshka and that was very definitely still hot — and I thought about what I was learning and what it implied about what I didn’t know yet.
Then I looked at the old-language shelf.
* * *
I had not planned to look at the old-language shelf. I had some context now. More than I’d had on the first visit. But not enough.
I looked at the old-language shelf anyway.
The book was where I had put it back. I could see the slight disturbance where I’d replaced it — the books on either side shifted by the margin of error I’d acknowledged to myself afterward, the gap where it sat a fraction narrower than the rest of the row. I had put it back very and it was still not quite exact — a thing I had thought about more than was warranted since.
I crossed to the shelf. I stood in front of it with my hands at my sides. I looked at the book.
The book looked back with the specific quality of old things that had been waiting — not patiently, not impatiently, simply in the way of things that existed outside the categories that made patience and its absence meaningful. It had been here a long time. It would be here longer. Whether I took it from the shelf today or in ten years or never was, to the book, a detail.
I took it from the shelf.
The weight of it — the familiar weight, already, five days after first holding it — settled into my hands with the specificity of something that had been handled before. My hands knew this weight, which was impossible, because I had held it once for a few minutes and set it back. Hands did not remember things from brief encounters. Hands were not that kind of organ.
I carried it to the table and sat down and opened it.
* * *
The first page.
I had looked at this page before. The same script, the same flowing angular lines with their internal rhythm, the same ink that had not faded. I had looked at it and had the experience of almost-understanding, the reaching-toward that didn’t quite arrive, and had put the book back.
Today I sat with it.
I sat with it for a long time and I did not try to read it — I had given up on trying to read it before I sat down, because trying to read it was approaching it with the tools I had for known languages, and it was becoming clear to me that the tools I had for known languages were not the tools this language required. The script was not a cipher. It was not a variant of something I knew. It was a different system entirely, built on different assumptions, with a different relationship between the marks on the page and the things they were intended to convey.
And yet.
The page did something — not to my eyes or mind but to something below both, in the sub-linguistic register. The mark was not a word or a symbol in any script I recognized. Something between those categories. And it communicated, the way a melody you had heard once and not retained still lived somewhere in you.
I turned to the second page.
The second page was different from the first in a way I would not have been able to articulate until this moment, and could now articulate only as: the first page was an introduction and the second page was the actual content. The distinction was carried in the weight of the marks, in the rhythm, in that sub-linguistic register that I was apparently reading in a language I had never been taught.
I put my finger on the first mark of the second page.
Something happened.
Not dramatic — nothing in the Hollow was dramatic in the way that things in mortal stories were dramatic, with light and sound and the physical expression of significance. What happened was quieter than that. A concept without language, the way very old things sometimes communicated — not through the specific tools that specific civilizations had developed to convey meaning, but through meaning itself, stripped of its vessel.
The concept was: beginning.
Not the beginning of the book. Not the beginning of a story or an account. Something older. The beginning that came before there were other things for it to be the beginning of.
I lifted my finger from the page.
The impression faded immediately — not slowly, the way sensations faded, but in the way of things that had never quite existed in the first place, leaving only the knowledge that they had been there. I sat very still with my hands in my lap and looked at the page and breathed.
The library was quiet. The presences, which had followed me in and settled near the high shelves in the way they settled near the upper reaches of the main hall, were very quiet in a way that was different from their regular quiet. The regular quiet of the presences was attentive. This was the quiet of something trying very hard not to disturb something delicate.
I looked at the page for a long time without touching it.
Then I turned to the third page.
* * *
The third page gave me nothing.
I tried the fourth. Nothing — the marks sat on the page and produced no impression in the sub-linguistic register, no concept without language, no reaching-toward. I tried the fifth. The same. I went back to the second page and put my finger on the second mark and received a faint impression that dissolved before I could hold it. I tried the third mark and got nothing.
Whatever I had accessed was not stable. It was not a system I had cracked open. It was a single instance, a single clear impression, and then the door had closed.
Beginning.
I sat with this word — the closest I could get to what I had received, the word being a thin translation of something that had not been a word — and I thought about what it meant that I had received it. Not what it meant that the book contained it. What it meant that I had received it. That the mark under my finger had communicated to me, directly, in a language I had not learned and could not read, in a register I had not known I possessed until five minutes ago.
I was a low-city thief from Vreshka. I had a passing familiarity with four others from years of professional necessity. I had never had any contact with the Hollow, I had never studied old languages, I had never met a Vorath before six days ago, and I had no framework whatsoever for the experience of receiving a pre-linguistic concept through touch from a text written in a language that, according to everything I understood about history and the world, should not exist anymore.
The Hollow had decided I was manageable. Aleksei had said this.
The book in the first language — the language that predated every civilization I had any knowledge of — had given me a concept through a touch.
The trees bent toward me when I walked in the territory. The shadow-fox had stationed itself at the treeline and was watching the Hall. The binding had activated when it had never activated before.
I looked at the ceiling of the library. At the library’s high, narrow windows and the Hollow’s mid-blue sky beyond them. At the densely packed shelves full of centuries of accumulated knowledge in which I had apparently just become, in some quiet and entirely unexplained way, literate.
I said, to the ceiling, to the presences in the upper reaches who were being so carefully quiet: “I’m going to need to think about this.”
The air warmed, very slightly. The presences knew. They had been knowing, the entire time, with the patience of things that had been waiting for me to know too.
* * *
I closed the book.
I held it for a moment — the weight of it, the density, the specific way it sat in my hands. Then I put it back on the shelf in exactly its position, the angle, the gap between its neighbors, as precise as I could manage. I stood back and looked at the shelf.
My hands were steady. I was not panicking. I was doing the thing I always did with information that I did not yet understand: I was filing it, and carefully, in the place it belonged, so that I could find it again when I had the context to use it.
I went back to the table. I sat down. I looked at the notes I had been taking before — the historian, the natural philosophy, the Veil Night accounts — and I picked up the pen.
I wrote, at the top of a fresh page:
What I Know About Gods, and How Wrong I Was.
I underlined it. Then I sat looking at it, because the title was accurate and the content was going to take some working out, and I did not know yet how wrong I was, which was itself the problem.
I started writing.
The presences settled in the upper shelves above me, warm and quiet, and the Hollow’s sky outside the windows cycled slowly toward its afternoon configuration, and the book on the shelf held the slight warmth of my hands the way the stone walls held their light: quietly, for a long time, longer than warmth usually lasted.
I wrote for three hours. I crossed out more than I kept. What I kept was this:
The stories in Zemya about the Vorath were wrong. Not maliciously — just inevitably. The Vorath were older, stranger, and more careful than any description I’d heard. Aleksei was the most careful of all.
But evil was a story you told about something when you did not have a better framework.
The better framework was going to take considerably more time.
I was, apparently, going to have a year.
* * *
I was back in my room by the time the Hollow’s sky had reached its nadir-adjacent register — not full black, not the deepest indigo, but the shade that preceded it, which I had learned to read as late evening.
The shadow-fox was at the treeline.
I had looked as soon as I came in, which I was going to stop pretending was a coincidence. It was not a coincidence. I came in and I looked at the treeline, and the shadow-fox was there, and I felt the specific satisfaction of something confirming an expectation, and I was going to sit with what that implied later.
I put my notes on the desk alongside the existing list. The new notes were three pages, cramped and crossed-through in places, with questions in the margins that did not have answers yet. You couldn’t answer them all at once. You answered the one whose answer unlocked the next one.
The first question, the one everything else hinged on, was at the top of the sequence. I had been avoiding writing it because writing it made it more real, and it being more real required me to do something about it, and I was not yet sure what doing something about it looked like.
I wrote it.
Why can I almost read that book.
I looked at it. The fox on the windowsill attended to the middle distance. Outdoors, at the treeline, the shadow-fox attended to the Hall. The binding told me Aleksei was closer than he had been all day — still north, but less far, moving in the direction of the Hall. He was coming back.
I thought about the question at the top of the sequence, and the fact that whatever the answer was, he almost certainly already knew it, and the fact that he had said “have you not” when I told him I had never had contact with the Hollow, which had been his version of telling me that the premise of the statement was worth examining.
He knew. Or he knew enough. He had some version of the answer to the question at the top of my sequence and he was holding it in the careful way he held everything — in reserve, offered when he had the right words, given when the time was something he had decided on rather than something that had been decided for him.
When you’re ready, I had told him, when he’d said he’d tell me about the sealed wing eventually.
He was doing the same thing. Waiting. For the right words, for the right time, for me to be ready. And the thing I was finding, sitting in my room with the fox and the shadow-fox and the question at the top of the sequence and the specific warmth in my hands that had come from touching a mark in a book I should not have been able to read — the thing I was finding was that I was not impatient about it.
I was curious. I was very curious — not the same thing. Curiosity could wait. Curiosity gathered information and built context and bided its time because it knew that the answer, when it came, would be better for the preparation.
Impatience wanted the answer now. Impatience didn’t care what state the answer was in when it arrived.
I was curious.
Aleksei was closer. The binding put him at the edge of the Hall’s grounds now, just inside the silver trees. I did not go to the window to look. I sat at my desk and I wrote a note at the bottom of the page — below the question, below the sequence, in the marginal space I kept for things I wasn’t sure yet deserved a line:
He already knows. He’s waiting for me to be ready to ask.
And below that, after a moment in which I held the pen above the paper and thought about whether to write it and wrote it anyway:
I think I almost am.
I looked at the window. The shadow-fox was at the treeline. The binding told me he had stopped moving. He was at the edge of the grounds. Stationary.
Warm, and alert — like it was listening for what came next.
And somewhere in the silver forest, in the ring of oldest trees where the light was thinner than it should have been, a sound beneath the ground. A low resonance, felt more than heard, like a very old engine running slower than it was designed to run.
The shadow-fox’s ears went flat. First time I’d seen that.
Four seconds. Then it stopped. The fox’s ears came forward. The attending resumed.
I stood at the window for a long time, and I could not decide whether what I had heard was the sound of something starting or the sound of something running out.
