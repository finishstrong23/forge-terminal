# Chapter Thirteen: She Arrives at Things Faster Than I Plan For

**POV:** Aleksei · **Words (v1):** 3,713 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

I had been teaching for a long time.
Not in any formal capacity — I had no students, had never designated myself a teacher, had not in a thousand years found a situation that required the specific and sustained effort of transmitting knowledge to another person across daily sessions. I had answered questions. I had given information. This was different.
What made it different was a question I had not examined until the third week of training, when I arrived in the north courtyard before she did — as I always arrived before her, because arriving after would have required her to wait for me and I did not make her wait for things — and stood in the dark earth and considered what it was I was actually doing here.
I was teaching. I had been doing this for three weeks and had not noticed I was doing it until I caught myself, two nights ago in the north study, working through the atmospheric shift that was coming and planning the specific sequence in which I would introduce its components and thinking: she will ask about the deep current first. She always starts with the mechanism before the application.
She always started with the mechanism before the application.
I had a student who had a consistent and identifiable learning pattern, which I had mapped to enough precision to anticipate her questions, and I was planning my sessions around that anticipation. This had happened without a decision. This was simply what the sessions had become over three weeks of daily contact, and I was examining it now, in the courtyard before she arrived, because I had learned that the things which happened without decisions were the things most worth examining.
The binding told me she had left her room. Moving toward the east courtyard first — she always checked on the shadow-fox in the morning before sessions, which she did not announce and which I was not going to mention knowing. I had approximately three minutes.
I used them to arrive at no useful conclusions and stood in the center of the dark earth and waited.
* * *
She came through the courtyard door in the coat.
She was always in the coat. The coat on her was the coat in its correct configuration. I had thought this on the third morning when she came to breakfast in it and had filed the thought in the place I filed things that did not require immediate attention and then kept finding in my way regardless.
She took it off at the courtyard entrance and hung it on the wall and came onto the dark earth in her shirtsleeves with the specific efficiency of someone transitioning between states — the at-rest state and the working state — that had a clean boundary and no ambiguity between them. She had always been like this. She was present before the session started. She had already been working through whatever the session would cover before she arrived. She came in having thought.
This was not a common quality. I had been doing this long enough to know it was not common.
“The atmospheric shift,” she said, by way of greeting, settling into the starting stance with the ease of someone who had made it habitual. “It started last night. The sky had a different quality around the third hour.”
“Yes,” I said.
“I tried to read what direction it was coming from. I couldn’t.” She looked at the sky above the courtyard, which was in its morning register, the deep blue before the shift to noon. “I can read the standard cycle now. The lateral changes are harder.”
“The lateral changes are harder because they don’t follow the cycle’s logic,” I said. “They follow the Hollow’s emotional logic, which is a different system.”
She looked at me. “The Hollow has emotional logic.”
“The Hollow has moods,” I said. “The cycle is the body. The lateral shifts are the moods. They’re related but not identical, and you can’t read the moods through the cycle’s grammar.”
She was quiet for a moment. Not the quiet of someone processing an expected answer but the quiet of someone whose sequence of questions had just reorganized. I knew this quality of her silence by now — the specific shift in her attention that happened when new information had structural implications rather than additive ones. The question order was changing.
“So I need two separate grammars,” she said.
“You need to understand that they’re separate,” I said. “The grammar develops from that understanding.”
She nodded, once, in the way she nodded when something had settled into place. I had learned to read her nods as precisely as she had learned to read my almost-not-a-smile — or perhaps more precisely, since she appeared to have a finer vocabulary for what my expression did than I had ever developed for my own face. “All right,” she said. “Show me the mechanism.”
I showed her the mechanism.
* * *
She asked seven questions in the first hour.
Not seven questions in the way of someone generating questions — that category of person asked questions for the asking, for the performance of engagement, and the questions arrived in clusters with no necessary relationship to each other. She asked seven questions that were a sequence. Each one built from the previous answer. Each one was the question that the previous answer had made possible, the exact next question in a line of reasoning that she was building in real time, out loud, using my answers as the material.
I had taught many things across many years. I had transmitted information in various directions. I had answered questions that ranged from precise and answerable to poorly formed and requiring significant reformulation before they could be addressed. In a thousand years of answering questions, I had never been asked a sequence of seven questions that required me to think alongside the questioning rather than simply ahead of it.
She arrived, on the seventh question, at a conclusion I had not expected her to reach this session. I had planned to introduce it in the third week of atmospheric training. She arrived at it, from her own sequence of reasoning, in the first hour of the first day.
“So the deep Hollow’s pressure system isn’t a closed system,” she said. It was not quite a question — it had the sense of something being stated for confirmation rather than asked for information. “It bleeds into the upper territory through the membrane surface, and the bleed is how the upper territory reads what’s happening below, and that’s why the shadow-fox knew the shift was coming before the sky changed. It was reading the membrane.”
I looked at her.
“That’s the mechanism,” she said. “The grammar is learning to read the membrane’s surface the way the shadow-fox reads it.”
I had been going to teach her this in the third week.
“Yes,” I said.
She held my gaze for a moment with the brown eyes that I had been looking at for five weeks and that I was no longer looking at with the removed attention of assessment. That had changed somewhere — I was not certain when — and I was not going to locate the change right now in the middle of a training session, but I was aware of the change and aware that it was relevant information that I was not examining at the moment and intended to examine later.
“The shadow-fox has been teaching me too,” she said. “I didn’t know that’s what was happening.”
“It often works that way,” I said. “The territory’s creatures teach by proximity before they teach by anything more direct.”
“You knew it was doing this,” she said.
“I suspected,” I said.
“And you didn’t tell me because—”
“Because being told you’re being taught is different from learning,” I said. “And what it was teaching you required not knowing.”
She looked at the sky. She was thinking — the tone of her thinking, which I had also mapped, which involved a stillness that was different from her default stillness, more interior, the stillness of someone whose attention had fully moved inward. “The not-knowing was the lesson,” she said.
“Part of it,” I said.
She looked back at me. It lasted perhaps two seconds before the management reinstated. She looked back at the sky.
I looked at the space where the expression had been.
* * *
The stance corrections were the part of the training I had not planned adequately.
That was the honest accounting of it. I had known the ground work required physical corrections, had known this from the beginning, and had approached it as a practical component of the training with the same removed efficiency I applied to practical components generally. The first session had been exactly that — practical, efficient, calibrated, producing exactly the results intended.
The second session had been the same.
By the third session I had understood that I had not planned adequately.
The problem was not the corrections themselves. The corrections were exactly what they were: purposeful, brief, informational, a physical means of transmitting knowledge that the body retained better than the mind. I had made corrections like this before, in the very occasional situations across a thousand years where physical training had been a component of something I was responsible for. There was nothing unusual about the corrections.
The problem was warmth.
Vorath ran cold. This was the baseline of what I was — not the cold of the air, not the cold of the Hollow’s lower registers, but the cold of something very old that had never run at mortal temperature and had no particular relationship with the concept. The cold was simply the condition of existence, the way gravity was the condition of existence: not experienced as a thing, simply the state in which everything else occurred.
I had not thought about warmth in a very long time.
Mortal warmth: I knew it abstractly, knew it the way I knew the properties of things I had not encountered recently. Mortals ran warm. This was not new information.
What was new was the tone of her warmth.
It arrived the way sunlight arrived: not a thing you reached for, not a thing that required your cooperation or your attention, simply present and then registered as present in the involuntary way of things that the body processed before the mind had weighed in. I had not stood in full sunlight in a very long time. The Hollow did not produce sunlight in the mortal sense. I had not had occasion to remember what sunlight felt like.
Her warmth reminded me.
This was the fact I had been managing since the third training session and that I was managing with the professional efficiency I applied to all things that required managing, which was: acknowledge, file, do not let it alter the functional layer. The training was the functional layer. The corrections were components of the training. Her warmth was a characteristic of the corrections. File, manage, proceed.
The management was becoming slightly more effortful daily.
I had not told anyone this, because there was no one I would tell, except Vasilisa, who would receive the information with the specific expression that had prompted me, on two previous occasions across centuries, to deeply regret having given her any information at all.
* * *
Today’s corrections were at the ankle, the arm above the elbow, and the side.
I noted this afterward, in the private accounting that I maintained in the place that was mine alone, with the same precision she used for her notes. Ankle, arm, side. Each brief, each purposeful, each exactly as long as necessary.
Her warmth at the ankle: three seconds, and then the absence of it, and then the absence noted.
Her warmth at the arm: slightly longer, because the arm correction required the stance to be rebuilt from that point upward and confirming the rebuild took more time than the initial correction. Five seconds. The absence more noticeable after five than after three.
Her warmth at the side: the heel of my hand against her ribs, through fabric — not direct contact and which I had not anticipated would register the way it registered. It registered. It moved through things. It moved through the fabric of the coat she had taken off at the wall, and it moved through the working shirt she was wearing, and it moved into the heel of my hand with the feel of a thing that intended to be felt.
I had said there.
I had meant the position of her centre. I had been, in that moment, entirely correct and fully technical, and I had meant it technically and produced it technically and every element of the correction had been exactly what it was supposed to be.
The warmth in my hand afterward was not technical. The warmth was simply a characteristic of warm things touched briefly. It faded in the time that things faded. It was not significant.
I noted that it faded. I noted when it faded. That we were both tracking something. That neither of us had named what we were tracking. And that the not-naming had a quality to it that was not avoidance but something more considered, more deliberate, something that both of us had apparently decided on independently and simultaneously, which was that this particular thing was worth keeping unnamed until it had fully become what it was going to be.
This was a thought I had not meant to have.
I returned to the session.
* * *
After the session, in the period that was neither session nor departure — which had also become habitual, which I had also not decided to make habitual — she asked about the atmospheric shift’s second component.
“The deep current,” she said. “It moves in the opposite direction from the surface shift. I noticed that when the sky changed last night the shadow-fox went still rather than moving. If it were tracking the surface shift it would have moved. But it went still, which means it was tracking something coming from below.”
I looked at her.
“The deep current moves up when the atmospheric shift moves across,” she said. “It’s the Hollow’s version of pressure equalization. The shift creates a gradient and the gradient pulls from the deep.”
I had been going to teach her this tomorrow.
“You’ve been paying very close attention to the shadow-fox,” I said.
“I’ve been paying close attention to everything,” she said. “But the shadow-fox is the best available model for Hollow-reading that I have direct access to. It’s faster than me and it’s been doing this longer and it knows I’m watching, which means if it’s going to teach me—” She stopped. Something in her expression shifted — the quick reorganization of someone who had just arrived at the same conclusion twice from different directions. “It knows I’m watching,” she said again, slowly. “It let me watch.”
“Yes,” I said.
She looked at the courtyard wall where the shadow-fox was not currently present, having moved to the east grounds after our session began in the way it moved when the session occupied the north courtyard. “It’s been teaching me from the beginning,” she said. “Since the second day. The treeline position — it wasn’t watching the Hall. It was giving me something to watch.”
“The creatures of the territory,” I said, “make their own assessments. I don’t direct them. The shadow-fox decided on the second day what it was going to do and has done it consistently.”
She turned to look at me. The open expression was back — not the two-second version, a longer one, the composure of someone who had stopped managing something for long enough to simply be looking. “You’re not surprised by any of this,” she said. “The shadow-fox. The book. The atmospheric shift. None of it surprises you.”
“No,” I said.
“You expected it,” she said. Not an accusation, a statement — she was cataloguing, as she catalogued everything, organizing new information into existing structures. “Not all of it. But enough of it.” Her eyes were steady on mine. “You knew the territory would respond to me.”
I said nothing.
“That’s on my list,” she said. “On the sequence. Not yet at the top. But close.”
“I know,” I said.
She held my gaze for a moment. This silence had a question in it that was not quite asked. She was deciding whether to ask it.
She decided not to.
“Same time tomorrow,” she said.
“Same time,” I said.
She retrieved the coat. She put it on with the ease of someone whose coat this was and had always been, and she went to the east door, and I watched her go with the weight of attention that I was filing under the things I was going to examine later.
The door closed. I stood in the empty courtyard.
I did the accounting.
Five weeks. Forty-seven sessions if you included the daily navigation work alongside the sparring, which I was now including because separating them required a clarity about what constituted a session that I was no longer certain I had. Forty-seven sessions, and in forty-seven sessions she had arrived ready every time, had asked sequences of questions rather than questions, had reached conclusions I had planned to introduce later every single time I had introduced a new foundation, had maintained every correction after it was made without requiring it twice, had made me think — genuinely, alongside her, in the way of problems that required a different intelligence than you arrived with — in every session that involved the atmospheric systems, the membrane-surface, the deep Hollow’s living properties.
She had made me think.
This was a thing I was noting with the care it deserved. I was not a person who was made to think by conversations. I did not generally require the other person to think alongside me, because the other person generally did not have the foundation to think alongside me, and the gap between foundations was too large to bridge in real time, and the most useful thing was usually transmission of information and then the other person’s thinking happened independently later.
She had no formal foundation for the Hollow. She had five weeks of active construction. The construction had happened fast enough and in the right order that she could now think alongside me in real time on things that were at the edge of what the foundation supported — not because she knew more than she knew, but because the way she built her foundation created the kind of structure that allowed for reasoning at the edges.
She had arrived at the deep current from the shadow-fox and the shadow-fox’s stillness and a logic I had spent three days constructing the session to build toward.
She had arrived at it in the post-session period while putting on her coat.
I stood in the empty courtyard and I made the accounting with the same precision I applied to all accounting, and I arrived at the thing I had been arriving at for five weeks across forty-seven sessions, and I said it to myself, plainly, in the specific interior register I reserved for things that were true regardless of what I did about them:
She was the most interesting thing to happen to this realm in a hundred years.
Not the Old Claim. Not the binding. Not the Hollow’s waking or the shadow-fox’s attention or the book that had answered her touch. Those were significant, and they had implications I was still mapping, and they would require conversation when I had the right words.
Her. Specifically. The person who arrived ready and asked sequences and maintained corrections and made him think and retrieved her coat with the ease of someone whose coat this was and had always been.
Her.
I stood in the courtyard for a while after that.
The Hollow’s atmospheric shift moved through the territory above the high walls, the lateral change in the sky that was the Hollow’s mood rather than its body. The shadow-fox was somewhere in the east grounds, reading the membrane surface, teaching and being watched teaching and knowing it was watched and having decided this was acceptable. The Hall was warm in the way it had been warm for five weeks, steady and specific and pleased about things in the quiet way of things that had been patient for a very long time and were now watching their patience produce results.
The binding told me she was in the library.
I went to the north study. I sat down. I thought about warmth, and the sense of it, and what it meant that I was aware of sunlight for the first time in longer than I could precisely calculate, and I thought about the word she, standing alone at the end of a thought the way a door stood at the end of a corridor, marking the entrance to something I had not yet walked into.
The north study was adjacent to the library corridor.
I sat in it for a long time, with the warmth that had faded from my hand an hour ago and that I was somehow still aware of, and I thought about the question she was building toward and whether I was going to have the right words when she arrived at it, and I thought that I had been working on the words for five weeks and was going to need to work harder, because she was arriving faster than I had planned for.
She arrived at things faster than I planned for.
I found I did not mind.
What I minded was that arriving fast meant reaching the difficult things sooner. And the most difficult thing — the architecture of the telling I had been building every night — was the thing that could make her leave.
The Claim was not the binding. It was not law. It could be carried out of Nochval and I could not follow.
Thirteen days before the sequence required me to tell her everything.
