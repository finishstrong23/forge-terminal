# Chapter Eighteen: Poor Impulse Control

**POV:** Aleksei · **Words (v1):** 5,013 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

She had drawn the Claim as property.
This was the thing I kept returning to, sitting in the library with her diagram on the table between us and the evening cycling through the windows and the Hall warm in the specific way it had been warm since the morning. She had taken the information I had given her — the full architecture of it, the Hollow’s dying and the Claim’s nature and the hundred years of diminishment — and she had built it in the first available hour into a diagram that was structurally more accurate than the framework I had given her to work from, because she had arrived independently at the property understanding that I had not explicitly named.
I had told her the Claim was a living covenant. I had told her it was older than law. I had not, specifically, used the word property — I had not because I was not certain, in the night’s construction of the sequence, that property was the right word, and I had chosen to leave the architecture open rather than close it with a word that was only approximately right.
She had closed it herself with the exactly right word, from first principles, in two hours.
She had also drawn a second version after Sable corrected the first, and the second version was cleaner than anything I could have produced with instruction, because the second version was built by someone who had received the correction and understood why it was a correction rather than a revision — who had not simply moved the lines but had rebuilt the underlying logic and produced a diagram from the rebuilt logic rather than from the corrected original.
She watched me sit with it in the way she watched things she cared about the reception of — not anxiously, not performing nonchalance either, simply present with the resolve of someone who had made a thing and was giving the thing to the person they had made it for and was waiting to find out what the person thought.
She had made the diagram for me. To show me what she had built. To find out where she had gotten it wrong. She had come downstairs with the notebook because the person most likely to tell her the truth about the diagram was in the library and she intended to have the truth.
I had been in the library because I was where I was and had stopped constructing elaborate justifications for it.
“The second version is right,” I said. “There are two things the diagram doesn’t capture yet but those are things that require significantly more foundation before they’re representable in two dimensions.” I looked at her. “You’ll get there.”
“How long,” she said.
“At your current rate,” I said, “faster than I’d plan for.”
The thing in her expression when I said that — the thing I had no name for yet and was building toward — arrived again briefly, the same quality as the morning at the forest’s edge, the same open not-managed quality, and I received it the way I had received everything she gave me that was real rather than performed: completely, without deflecting it elsewhere, in the exact place it was intended to land.
We worked through the diagram together for an hour after that. She asked questions. I answered them. She had learned, over eight weeks of sessions, to feel where the edge was. She did not force past it. She mapped it, circled it, came back to it from several directions to establish its shape, and then retreated to build more foundation before approaching it again.
This was how she had been navigating everything since the first night. The alley, the law, the Hall, the territory, the book, the Claim. She had been doing it before she had language for it, before the framework existed to name the method. It was simply her nature, the way the Claim was her nature — something she had always done that the first world had always known she would do, because the first world had chosen its carriers with the patience of something that understood the match between the nature of the task and the nature of the person was the most important thing about the choosing.
I thought about the alley. About sixty silver marks and a fence named Drev and a woman in the dark who drew a knife she knew would be useless and held it anyway. The first world had chosen well.
The library evening ended the way evenings ended — not with a declared conclusion but with the gradual quality of something that had reached its natural extent. She closed the notebook. She looked at me across the table with the brown eyes in the low evening light and said: “Thank you.” Simply, with the directness she brought to everything she meant.
“For the diagram,” I said.
“For the words,” she said. “That you built all night. The sequence. The architecture of it.” A pause, precise. “For being afraid to give them and giving them anyway.”
I was very still for a moment. “You’re welcome,” I said, to the library, because she was not looking away and I was not looking away and the eye contact was doing something I was not going to examine in the middle of a library evening.
She smiled — not the full real one, something smaller, a smile that knew it had landed something and was not going to press the advantage. She stood, gathered her notebook, and went upstairs.
I sat in the library for a while after she left.
The fire in the grate, which the Hall had lit with the usual courtesy, burned without requiring attention. The books on the shelves had their usual quality. The old-language section had the quality it had had since the second page answered her, slightly more present, slightly more awake.
She had thanked me for being afraid and giving the words anyway.
I thought about what it meant to be thanked for the fear rather than in spite of it. To have someone receive the information that you had been afraid and find it not a weakness but a thing that earned gratitude. I had lived a long time. I had not previously encountered that particular reception, or if I had, I had not understood it when it arrived.
I was, I found, understanding it now.
* * *
The morning was different.
Not in the structure — the courtyard, the exercises through the binding’s awareness before she appeared, the shadow-fox on the wall. Structure was constant and had been constant since the first week and would continue to be constant because structure was the thing that held everything else in place when everything else was in the process of shifting. The quality inside it was not the same.
I had said the afraid thing out loud at the forest’s edge. I had said specifically what I had been afraid of — what she would decide, after she knew — and she had not resolved the fear by giving me the assurance I had been afraid to want. She had told me what she did not know yet and then she had told me what she was most certain about within the not-knowing, and the difference between those two things — the thing she could not yet give me and the thing she could — was a distinction I had not expected her to make and that I was still doing the accounting on.
She had not said: I’m staying and I want the same thing you want. She had said: of all the things I haven’t decided, staying is the most certain. She had been precise. She had been honest about the limits of her certainty. She had given me exactly as much as she had and not a fraction more or less — the bearing of someone who understood that the exact amount was the only amount worth giving.
I had been thinking about the exact amount since the forest.
She arrived in the courtyard with the coat already off, folded over her arm, transition from rest to work state already complete before the door closed behind her. She looked at me. I looked at her. The morning held the feel of the first morning after something significant — the world the same and different simultaneously, the same air and sky and dark earth, a different quality in the familiar things.
“The older territory,” she said. Confirming the session plan she had contributed to the night before, with the composure of someone checking an agreement rather than requesting permission.
“The older territory,” I confirmed.
We went.
* * *
The older part of Nochval’s territory was a forty-minute walk from the Hall, through the silver forest and then deeper, where the tree density increased and the height of the canopy blocked more of the sky and the bioluminescence did not overlap in circles but layered instead, the older trees’ light falling beneath and through the younger trees’ light in stratified columns. The ground here had a different quality from the forest’s outer regions — denser, more communicative, the specific weight of terrain that had been accumulating the Hollow’s memory for longer than the outer forest had existed.
She felt the difference before I named it.
“The ground is louder here,” she said, settling into the deep-Hollow stance at the older territory’s edge. “The communication is — more.”
“More information, or more insistent,” I said.
She held the stance and felt for the distinction. “Both,” she said. “But the insistence is not — it’s not pressure. It’s more like volume. The signal is simply higher here.”
“The older ground carries more accumulated response,” I said. “Every atmospheric shift the territory has experienced, every Hunt passage, every significant event in the Hollow’s last several centuries — the deep ground absorbs and retains the response pattern. The older the ground, the more patterns retained, the higher the ambient signal.”
“Like the oldest trees during the atmospheric shift,” she said immediately. “The same principle.”
“Yes.”
She was quiet for a moment, feeling the ground through the stance, reading the higher signal with the attention she brought to new sensory information — not reaching, not forcing, simply being present to it and letting it arrive. I watched her face as she did this. The thing that happened in it when the Hollow communicated through the ground was specific and had become more visible over eight weeks as the communication had deepened — a slight softening, not of her expression but of the layer beneath it, the place where the body registered things before the mind organized them.
Since yesterday morning. Since the disclosure. Since she knew.
The softening was different today. But the layer beneath the expression was more present. Less protected. The knowledge of what the ground was communicating to her — the knowledge that the communication was not coincidental, not the Hollow’s general awareness of a person on its terrain, but specific, the recognition of the Claim in her — was visible in the body’s reception of it in a way it had not been visible before she knew.
Knowing had changed the quality of the experience.
I had thought it would. She was learning to use it deliberately. Starting now, standing in the older territory with the deep ground’s high signal coming through her feet, she was learning what the Claim felt like when she was paying attention to it rather than simply receiving it.
“It knows me,” she said quietly. Not a discovery — confirmation. The quality of something she had known at a lower level and was now knowing at a higher one.
“It has always known you,” I said.
She looked up at me. “It felt like home,” she said. “When I first came into the forest. I thought that was — I thought it was a response to the beauty. The way beautiful things feel like belonging even when they’re new.” She looked at the layered light in the older trees. “It wasn’t the beauty.”
“No,” I said.
“It was the Claim recognizing the territory,” she said. “The orientation. The compass.” She breathed out, slow and deliberate, the pattern she used for managing something — except this time the managing was not suppression. She was giving it room. “How long has it been pointing here,” she said. “Specifically. How long have I been in transit.”
This was the question I had not addressed in the sequence because the answer required a delicacy I had not been certain of in the night’s construction. “It does not operate from birth — there is a threshold, specific to each carrier, at which the orientation engages.” I paused. “Yours engaged at approximately your sixteenth year.”
She went very still.
“Age sixteen,” she said.
“The age at which you began to develop your particular professional practice,” I said. “The specific qualities of your attention — the exactness, the put-things-back-precisely, the sequence-building. Those are expressions of the Claim coming into active orientation. Not caused by it — those qualities would have been yours regardless. But deepened by it. Directed.”
She was quiet for a long time. I watched the thing working through her expression — not the grief of yesterday, something different. Something that was grief’s cousin: the manner of someone recontextualizing a large portion of their own history and finding it different in the new light than it had been in the old.
“Seven years,” she said. “I have been in transit for seven years.”
“Toward here,” I said. “Yes.”
She looked at the older trees. She looked at her hands. She looked at me. “You’ve been here the whole time,” she said, and the observation had a quality I was not going to examine too closely in the middle of the older territory with the Claim active in the ground and seven years of her history reordering itself around its new center.
“I’ve been here the whole time,” I said.
She nodded, once, in the settling way. She turned back to the ground. She found it in approximately two minutes, which was faster than I had estimated, and she found it correctly.
“Here,” she said, turning to the precise direction.
“Yes,” I said.
She held the direction. She did not look at me again for several minutes. I gave her the several minutes and stood in the older territory with the high ground-signal and the stratified ancient light and the feel of the morning — the quality that all mornings had been having since the forest’s edge and that I was still in the process of naming.
* * *
We were coming back through the outer forest’s edge when the Hunt-sense registered Radovan.
He was at the territory’s eastern boundary — not Zora’s eastern boundary, the northern edge, where the boundary ran along the deep membrane-path that connected Nochval to the territories further north. His signature was as familiar as any signature I knew: large, warm in the Vorath register in the way that some of the older ones ran warm, carrying the specific weight of the War domain that was not aggression but was solidity, the feel of something that had been present through enough of the world’s significant events to have accumulated the weight of them.
He was moving toward the Hall. At his usual pace, which was never urgent — Radovan had learned several centuries ago that urgency was mostly a mortal problem and had made peace with a slower relationship to arrival. He was perhaps twenty minutes out.
I had been expecting this since Zora’s visit. Zora came and looked and left and reported, and the reporting reached Radovan, and Radovan — who had poor impulse control for someone nine hundred years old, as I had told Kira in the rules briefing — had apparently decided that twenty-two days was a sufficient period of waiting before he came to see for himself.
It was not a sufficient period. There was no sufficient period. Radovan was going to come when he was going to come and the management of his arrival was a project I had attempted on several previous occasions with limited results.
She had noticed the shift in my attention — she read my attention through the binding now with the same familiarity I read hers, the same finer-grained-than-it-had-any-right-to-be quality. “What,” she said.
“Radovan is at the northern boundary,” I said.
She looked at me. Something in her expression acquired the bearing of someone who had been told to expect something and was now, with the specific alertness of preparation, preparing for it. “The one who is curious about most things and has poor impulse control,” she said.
“Yes,” I said.
“Is he going to be a problem,” she said, which was what she had asked about him in the rules briefing, two months and a significant amount of history ago.
“No,” I said. I considered. “He will be—”
“Irritating in a different way,” she said.
She had retained the rules briefing verbatim. I should not have been surprised by this. “Yes,” I said.
“How irritating,” she said.
“On a scale,” I said, “of Vasilisa’s visit to Zora’s visit—”
“The former,” I said. “Substantially.”
She processed this with the expression she used for information she was going to deal with. “All right,” she said. “I’m going to go back to the Hall and have tea before he arrives, because I suspect I’ll want to have had tea.”
I looked at her. “That is a very sensible decision,” I said.
The real smile arrived briefly, the full one. Then she went toward the Hall, and I went to the territory’s northern boundary to intercept Radovan before he reached it and have the conversation that was going to happen regardless of whether I intercepted him.
* * *
He was walking the northern membrane-path with an unhurried quality that, in any other context, would have been called administrative. The Hunt was not dramatic. It was logistical — the systematic survey of boundary, membrane, territory, conducted with the patience of someone who had been doing this for a thousand years and expected to be doing it for a thousand more.
He saw me coming and smiled in the way he smiled — the full generous thing, unreserved, the smile of someone for whom warmth was not a performance but a default state. Nine hundred years and he still smiled like that. I had known him since before the Hall was built and I had never fully understood how he had managed it.
“You came to the boundary,” he said. “That’s new.”
“I came to talk to you before you arrived at the Hall,” I said.
“Also new.” He looked at me with the amber eyes that had seen more wars than I had counted and that read people with the particular accuracy of someone whose domain was conflict and who had therefore spent centuries learning to see past what people presented to what they were actually prepared to do. “You look different,” he said.
“I look the same,” I said.
“You look the same and you are different,” he said, with the cheerful certainty of someone who had known me for nine hundred years and was not going to be managed about it. “Tell me about her.”
“I was going to tell you that she’s adjusting well, the training is progressing, the Claim is active, and Zora has been to the territory once,” I said.
“That’s the administrative summary,” he said. “Tell me about her.”
I looked at him. He looked at me with the patience he had developed for exactly this category of conversation — the category of conversations I did not want to have and that he had decided we were going to have regardless. He had been having this category of conversation with me for several centuries on various subjects and he had never once been successfully avoided.
“She’s—” I said.
I stopped.
The word that arrived for the sentence I had started was not the word I had been intending to use. The word that arrived was not from the administrative vocabulary. It was from the accounting I kept in the private register, the one I had been keeping for eight weeks, the one that contained the warmth and the diagram and the library evening and the forest’s edge and the two seconds of her actual smile.
Radovan was watching me not finish the sentence with the expression of someone watching something they had suspected for several weeks become confirmed in real time.
“She’s very—” I said. I stopped again. “The situation is—” The word from the private register was still there, patient, in the way that true things were patient. It was not going to be replaced by an administrative summary. It was the right word and it was going to be the word whether or not I wanted to say it to Radovan at the northern boundary in the morning.
“Aleksei,” Radovan said, gently, in the voice he used when he was going to say something that was going to be annoying because it was true.
“Don’t,” I said.
“I haven’t said anything yet,” he said.
“You were about to say something,” I said, “that you have been planning to say since Zora told you about the binding.”
“I’ve been planning it since before Zora told me about the binding,” he said. “I felt the binding activate on the Hunt night and I have been waiting twenty-two days to come and say it. The patience I have demonstrated,” he added, “has been genuinely exceptional.”
I looked at him.
“I’m not going to say the thing you think I’m going to say,” he said. “I’m going to say this: I know what your face looks like when you’re not trying to manage it. I have known for nine hundred years. And I have not seen your face look the way it currently looks since—” He stopped. The warmth in his expression became something more careful. He and I both knew the end of the sentence and neither of us was going to say it, because the end of the sentence was the sealed wing and the stopped hourglass and the one hundred and fifty years, and that was not a conversation for the northern boundary in the morning with new things happening.
“Since a long time,” he said.
I was quiet.
“I’m glad,” he said. Simply. Without the broad smile, without the performance of warmth — the real register, the one underneath the generous surface, the one he used when something mattered. “I came because I wanted to see it for myself. That’s all.”
We stood at the northern boundary in the morning with the Hollow’s deep blue above us and the silver trees at their edge-of-territory light and nine hundred years of knowing each other between us.
“She knows about you,” I said. “I mentioned you in the rules briefing.”
Something in his expression acquired a quality I recognized: interest, the specific kind, the kind he produced when a situation had more dimensions than he had initially accounted for. “What did you tell her.”
“That you’re curious about most things and have limited patience for the convention of not expressing it,” I said. “That you’ve been alive nine hundred years and have not developed adequate impulse control.”
Radovan looked at me for a long moment. “You talked about me,” he said, slowly, with the composure of someone identifying something significant in a piece of information that appeared ordinary.
“I was briefing her on the other Vorath she might encounter,” I said.
“You talked about me specifically,” he said. “With warmth.”
“I said you had poor impulse control,” I said.
“In eight hundred years,” Radovan said, “you have never described me to anyone. You have mentioned me. You have confirmed my existence when asked. You have never described me.” He was smiling now — the broad generous one returning, with an additional quality underneath it, the specific warmth of someone witnessing something they find deeply, genuinely good. “You described me to her.”
“Radovan,” I said, in the tone I had been using with him for nine hundred years when conversations were heading somewhere I could see coming and had determined was not productive.
“We’re going to the Hall,” he said. “I want tea. I want to meet her. And I want you to know,” he added, falling into step beside me with the easy warmth of nine hundred years of knowing each other’s pace, “that whatever your face is doing right now is the most interesting thing I have seen in several decades.”
“My face,” I said, “is doing nothing.”
“Your face,” he said, “is doing everything.”
I did not respond to this.
We walked toward the Hall, and the shadow-fox — which had emerged from the territory’s deeper reaches at some point during my conversation with Radovan and was now moving through the trees to our left with the accompanying-without-following quality it used for me in the same way it used it for her — tracked our approach with its patient cold-light attending.
Radovan looked at the shadow-fox. He looked at me. “It’s following you,” he said.
“It accompanies,” I said. “It doesn’t follow.”
“The shadow-fox of Nochval’s territory,” Radovan said, slowly, as if each word was a stone being placed carefully. “Which has kept its own company for thirty years. Is accompanying you.” He paused. “To the Hall.” He paused again. “Where the mortal woman is.”
“Yes,” I said.
“Aleksei,” he said.
“Don’t,” I said.
“I was only going to say,” he said, with the innocent quality he deployed when he was absolutely not being innocent, “that it’s a very beautiful morning.”
It was a beautiful morning. The Hollow’s sky was at its deepest blue. The binding told me she was in the library. She had made tea, which she always made tea, and she was at the right-side table in the right-side chair — her chair, the one she had claimed through the same mechanism by which the coat had become hers, through daily use and presence and the accumulated weight of simply being the person she was in the place she was.
“It is,” I said. “A beautiful morning.”
As Radovan stood to leave, he paused at the doorway. Not dramatically — just the small hesitation of someone deciding whether to say something they had not planned to say.
“The eastern territories are talking,” he said. Not looking at me. Looking at the Hall’s main corridor as if it were suddenly very interesting. “About Nochval. About what’s happening here.”
I waited.
“Some of them think restoration is overdue,” he continued. “Some of them think the territorial balance works better as it is. And some of them—” He stopped. “Some of them think a Claim-carrier is a problem that needs solving rather than a tool that needs using.”
The Hall’s warmth did not change. The binding did not flare. But something in the quality of the afternoon shifted slightly a cloud passed over.
“Solving,” I said.
“Politically,” Radovan said quickly. “Through convenings. Through voting. Through the mechanisms we use for such things.” He finally looked at me. “I am not warning you about danger to her person. I am warning you about danger to what you are trying to do.”
“I understand,” I said.
“Good,” Radovan said. “Because some of the talking is going to turn into attending. At the convening. When it happens.” He smiled, but the smile was different now — not amused, not teasing. Something more serious underneath. “You will want to be prepared for that.”
Radovan smiled all the way to the Hall’s door and said nothing else, which was, of all the things he had done in nine hundred years, the most admirable exercise of restraint I had ever witnessed from him.
I was going to tell him that.
Later.
Later, I told myself. And then discovered the Hollow had opinions about timing — and that Radovan had felt them too, because as he reached the threshold he stopped and looked back at me with an expression I had not seen on his face in two centuries.
Not amusement. Not curiosity.
Concern.
He did not say what had prompted it. He simply held my gaze for three seconds, nodded once, and left.
* * *
He left. The door closed.
I went to the north study, because the morning required distance, and I opened the book to the oldest section — the pages I had read once eight hundred years ago, whose contents had not applied to any situation I had then anticipated.
The situation had changed.
The first-language description: a carrier had formed an attachment to a territory keeper beyond the covenant’s functional requirements. The attachment was mutual. The Hollow accommodated it. The covenant deepened. The Hollow’s demands escalated in proportion. And the carrier’s mortal body proved insufficient to sustain what the deepened demands required.
The word the first language used for what happened to the carrier: exceeded. The mortal frame failed under a demand designed for something other than mortal endurance.
The passage noted that the Vorath survived.
I closed the book. I sat in the north study with the window and the oldest trees and understood, with terrible clarity, that the thing I had been allowing — the warmth, the chair beside, the distance closing — might kill her.
The chair would need to go back to across. The corrections would need to be corrections and nothing else. The thing I had been allowing would need to stop, because allowing it was not tenderness. It was negligence of the most precise kind.
I went to the library. I moved the chair. I prepared for the session with the careful quality of someone who had made a decision and intended to hold it regardless of what the holding cost.
The cost would be considerable.
