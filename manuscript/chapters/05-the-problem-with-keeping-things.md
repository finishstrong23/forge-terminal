# Chapter Five: The Problem with Keeping Things

**POV:** Aleksei · **Words (v1):** 4,420 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

I had not slept.
This was not unusual. A thousand years of practice had reduced its necessity to something manageable, something I could schedule around the work of keeping two realms in functional proximity. On a normal night after a Hunt I would spend the early hours in the deep territory attending to the membrane’s condition, then sleep briefly, then resume.
Last night was not a normal night after a Hunt.
I had gone to the far edge of Nochval, where the silver trees thinned and the ground sloped down toward the older part of the territory, the part that had been here before I built the Hall, before I had a reason to build anything. Less filtered through my presence. I went there when I needed to think without the Hall’s attention, which cared about things before I was ready.
I stood in the old territory for several hours and thought about the binding.
The binding had activated. In forty-seven Hunts across a thousand years, the binding had never activated. The law had acknowledged each catch in the way it acknowledged all legal events: the notation, the door latched, and nothing else. Nothing that persisted. Nothing that stayed in my Hunt-sense after the fact.
The binding was still in my Hunt-sense.
Not as an intrusion — it was not uncomfortable, which was itself notable. A direction. A distance. The warm, specific, wholly alive signature of a mortal woman who was currently, the binding informed me without being asked, awake and stationary and not in distress. She was somewhere in the Hall. Her state was what I was learning to categorize as her baseline: alert, processing, keeping her own counsel.
She had been awake since before I returned from the old territory. She had been moving through the Hall. I knew this because the Hall knew it and the Hall and I were not separate entities in any meaningful sense — its awareness was an extension of mine. She had found the library.
I had felt her find the library, which is to say I had felt the Hall’s specific quality of interested attention shift when she went through the door, a shift it produced when something it cared about happened. The Hall cared about its library the way it cared about nothing else in the building — it had opinions about the library with a consistency that I had stopped arguing with centuries ago. It had opinions about who could find things in the library, about how books were handled, about the weight of attention brought to the room.
It had, this morning, the feel of something quietly pleased.
I stood in the old territory and I thought about what it meant that my Hall was quietly pleased about a mortal woman in its library, and I thought about the binding, and I thought about the book she had taken from the shelf.
I had felt her take it. She had taken the book in the first language. The oldest book in the library. The one I had not taken down myself in two hundred years because there was nothing in it I did not already know, and the weight of old things being read without understanding was not a weight I went looking for.
She had opened it. She had held it in the Hollow’s light and looked at it.
And she had almost read it.
I stood very still in the old part of Nochval for a long time after that.
* * *
Vasilisa arrived at mid-morning.
She did not send word ahead. She never sent word ahead. She appeared at the edge of the Hall’s grounds in the way she appeared everywhere: without transition, fully formed, as if she had simply decided to be there and the world had accommodated the decision.
She was wearing something that had too many layers to count and was the color of the Hollow’s sky at the nadir — not black, too complex to be black. Her hair was loose. She stood at the edge of the grounds with her hands in her layers and looked at the Hall and then at me.
“You look terrible,” she said, with the warmth of someone offering a compliment.
“Vasilisa,” I said.
“Don’t use that tone. I came as soon as I heard.” She was already walking toward the Hall. “How long has she been here? Is she all right? What’s she like? Radovan said she had a knife. I find that very encouraging. Show me.”
“No.”
“Aleksei.”
“She has been here one night,” I said. “She is in the Hall. She is not available for your inspection.”
“I’m not going to inspect her,” Vasilisa said, in the tone of someone who intended exactly to inspect her. “I’m going to introduce myself. Appropriately. As a concerned — what am I? A colleague? A neighbor? An extremely interested observer of the situation?” She considered. “A friend of the household. I’m a friend of the household.”
“You’ve never visited this household in three centuries,” I said.
“I’ve been busy.” She smiled at me with the specific brightness she deployed when she had already decided something and was letting me catch up. “Also there was nothing worth visiting for. There is now. Show me.”
“No,” I said.
She looked at me for a moment with both of her mismatched eyes and the expression of a woman who had known me for so long that she could read the negative spaces in my sentences — the things I was not saying with the same fluency she read what I was. Something shifted in her face. Not the bright social performance she’d been running since she arrived. Something underneath it, quieter and more genuine.
“The binding,” she said. Not a question.
“Yes.”
“How does it feel?”
I looked at the Hall. At the window of the room I’d given her — the third floor, south face, the one I’d had prepared because it had the best view of the forest and the most natural light that the Hollow produced. The window was empty. She was not at it. The binding told me she was at her desk.
“Present,” I said.
Vasilisa was quiet for a moment. This was notable — Vasilisa’s silences were strategic and deliberate and she did not produce them accidentally. This one had the tone of her choosing her next words with unusual care. The next words were going to be words she had already composed and was now deciding whether to deliver.
She delivered them.
“You know,” she said, “in a thousand years, the binding has never activated.” She paused. “Not once. Not for any of the caught.”
“I am aware,” I said.
“Not for any of the mortals you returned. Not for the three you gave options to in the past four hundred years.” Another pause. “Not for the one you kept, three centuries ago.” She was looking at the Hall now, not at me, which gave me the particular freedom of being observed without being watched. “You kept that one because the law specifically required it — a child, if I remember, who had wandered out during the Hunt and had no home to return to. The binding did not activate for them either.”
“I am aware,” I said again.
“So.” She turned back to me. The bright expression was completely gone. What was underneath it was something I had seen very rarely across the centuries — the real Vasilisa, the one who was not performing warmth because she was experiencing it. Careful. Concerned. Something else, buried below those, that looked almost like relief. “The binding activated,” she said. “For a mortal woman with a knife who was outside because she was owed money.”
“Sixty silver marks,” I said.
A pause. Then Vasilisa made a sound that was somewhere between a laugh and something more complicated. She turned back toward the Hall and began walking. She had been the Vorath of Memory for as long as I had been anything, and memory required knowing which things needed to be given time to settle before you looked at them again.
“I’m going to look at the grounds,” she said over her shoulder. “The south side. I haven’t seen the south silver trees since you replanted them four centuries ago.”
“The south side,” I said, “is adjacent to the Hall’s south-face windows.”
“What a coincidence,” Vasilisa said.
I let her go. There were battles I chose and battles I did not, and this one had been over before it started.
* * *
She spent twenty minutes at the south side of the grounds. I knew she was there the way I knew everything that happened within Nochval’s boundary: not by watching, but by the Hall’s ambient awareness, the constant low current of the territory’s attention that ran through me as naturally as my own pulse.
I was inside when the Hall told me that she had found a window to examine.
It was the south-face window. The third floor. The one with the best view.
I was in my study, three floors down, maintaining the membrane at the city’s eastern border. This was real work. The membrane required regular attention; I was correcting this.
The membrane’s eastern section produced a note I had not felt in decades. Not a breach. A listening quality, as if something on the other side had pressed an ear to the wall.
I felt Kira move to her window.
Not through the window. She did not look out. The binding told me she was near it — the directional sense was specific enough to locate her within a room at close range, and she was at the window, not far from the glass, and the Hall told me that Vasilisa was below in the grounds with her face turned up and her mismatched eyes very bright.
I did not go to the south grounds. I returned to the membrane.
Vasilisa came inside twelve minutes later. I heard her in the corridor. She was not bothering to move quietly. She wanted to be heard approaching. A courtesy she occasionally extended when she had something to say that she thought I should be seated for.
She appeared in the doorway of my study. Her expression had the specific quality it had when she was containing something — a category of expression I had learned to identify across centuries because it appeared reliably in the moments before she said something that reorganized my understanding of a situation.
“She’s making a list,” Vasilisa said. “I could see her writing from the grounds. She has a great deal to write down, apparently.” A pause. “She’s also wearing your coat.”
I said nothing.
Vasilisa looked at me with both her mismatched eyes. “You gave her the coat,” she said.
“She was cold.”
“Of course she was.” She came into the study and sat down in the chair across from my desk with the ease of someone who had been sitting in that chair for centuries, which she had. “Tell me about her.”
“I have told you what I know.”
“You’ve told me facts,” Vasilisa said. “Mortal woman, caught in the Hunt, binding activated. That’s the summary. I want the actual account. The details.” Her eyes were attentive in a way that had nothing to do with curiosity and everything to do with a very old intelligence gathering the information it needed to work. “What was she like, in the alley.”
I set down what I was doing. Not because I was done with it — I was not done with it — but because Vasilisa asking for details in that particular tone was something I had learned required full attention, and dividing attention between her and anything else was a strategy that consistently produced worse outcomes than simply giving her what she was asking for.
“She didn’t scream,” I said.
Vasilisa waited.
“She drew a knife and held it. She knew it wasn’t going to be useful. She held it anyway.” I thought about the alley — about the set of her, the specific steadiness of it. “She had already run the options before I finished crossing the space. By the time I reached her she had assessed the exits and found them insufficient and arrived at the conclusion that what happened next was going to happen next, and she was managing that rather than the fear.”
“Managing,” Vasilisa said.
“Deciding what she could control,” I said. “She lowered the knife herself. Deliberately. As a choice rather than a capitulation.” I paused. “She looked at me directly. She held eye contact without difficulty. Most mortals cannot do this with a Vorath at full presence. She did it without apparent effort.”
Vasilisa had a particular silence she used for the moments when she was satisfied with a thing she had already suspected. This was that silence.
“She negotiated,” I said, because Vasilisa was not going to stop looking at me with that expression until I continued. “She found two loopholes in the law within fifteen minutes of hearing it. Both closed. She moved on immediately and started gathering information instead.” A pause. “She asked good questions.”
“What kind of good.”
“The kind that locate the edges of things,” I said. “She was not asking what she could ask. She was finding out where the questions I wouldn’t answer were.”
Vasilisa looked at me for a long moment. Then: “She sounds delightful.”
“She’s a complication,” I said.
“Those aren’t mutually exclusive.” She stood. She was done. She had what she’d come for. I had told her more than I’d intended to — a thing she reliably accomplished when she wanted to and I reliably failed to prevent. She moved toward the door, then stopped at the threshold and said, without turning back: “You checked on her this morning. I felt you go past the room.”
“The binding requires proximity,” I said. “For the first period.”
“Does it.”
The way she said this indicated that she was not unaware that this was not a thing the binding required. I did not respond to it.
“You gave her three options,” Vasilisa said, still in the doorway, still not looking at me. “I’ve been thinking about that since Radovan told me. Three. You haven’t given anyone three options since—” She stopped. A particular kind of stop — not because she didn’t know the end of the sentence, but because she knew it well and was choosing to leave it where it was. “Why three.”
I considered the question. It was not a question I had asked myself yet — I had been occupied with the binding, with the anomaly of it, with the book in the old language and what it might mean. I had not asked myself why I had offered three options when the functional options were one.
“I don’t know,” I said. It was the most honest thing I’d said all morning, and also the most dangerous, which was likely why I said it to Vasilisa rather than to anyone else.
She received it with the quiet of someone handling something fragile. “All right,” she said. Just that. She was nearly out the door.
“Leave her alone today,” I said.
“Of course.” She was gone — I heard her in the corridor, moving toward the south face again. Her footsteps had a particular quality when she was pleased about something, slightly lighter than her baseline. She was pleased about something.
I returned to the membrane.
The binding sat in my awareness with the ease of something that had always been there. She was at her desk. She was still writing. She was a mortal in my Hall for whom I had a legal obligation, and the binding told me she was well, and that was sufficient.
I returned to the membrane.
The membrane did not require this much attention. I was aware of this.
* * *
Vasilisa left in the early afternoon.
She had not spoken to Kira directly — I knew this because the Hall’s awareness of Kira had not changed quality in the way it changed when Kira interacted with anyone or anything, the slight heightening that indicated her attention had been engaged. Vasilisa had, however, spent three separate periods at the south grounds looking at the windows with her mismatched eyes and the expression of someone extremely satisfied with what they could see from a careful distance.
She found me in the Hall’s main corridor on her way out.
“I’ll come back,” she said. This was a statement of fact rather than a request. She would come back regardless of what I said about it, and we both knew this, so I said nothing about it.
“Vasilisa,” I said instead.
She looked at me with both mismatched eyes.
“The binding,” I said. “In your memory — in everything you carry. Has it been documented. Any account of it activating for a mortal.”
She held my gaze for a moment. The quality of the look was the one she used when she had an answer and was deciding how much of it to give. “There are accounts,” she said carefully, “of the binding activating in the early period. Before the separation of the realms.” She paused. “Not since.”
“What were the circumstances?”
“Complex,” she said. She was not going to tell me today. She turned toward the Hall’s entrance. “I’ll look into it.” A pause at the threshold. “Aleksei.”
“What.”
“Show her the territory. When she’s ready. The Hollow responds to her.” A beat. “You felt it last night. I know you felt it.”
I had felt it. I had felt it from the old territory while I was trying to think about the binding without the Hall’s attention complicating it — the Hollow’s ambient awareness shifting in a way I had not felt in a very long time. Not threatening. The opposite of threatening. Something the Hollow did when it recognized something it had been waiting for.
I had felt it. I had not known what to do with it. I still did not know what to do with it.
“Go home, Vasilisa,” I said.
She went. At the edge of the grounds, where the silver trees began, she paused and tilted her face toward a particular window. Then she was gone, her Vorath signature withdrawing past Nochval’s boundary like a tide going out.
I stood in the Hall’s entrance for a moment.
The binding told me Kira was still at her desk. Still writing. She had been writing, on and off, for most of the day. The Hall was pleased about this in the quiet way it was pleased about things — a particular quality to its attention, satisfied and warm.
I went back to the north study. I had not accessed the Hall’s visual awareness of the room today. Her room was her room and the Hall’s awareness of what happened inside it was an intimacy that did not belong to me — the binding gave me what the law required, and I did not take from it what the law had not offered.
I had not watched her in the library. I had known she was there. I had felt the shift when she took the old-language book from the shelf and then — and this was the thing I had been arranging my thoughts around since it happened — the particular stillness that had moved through the Hall’s awareness, the sense of something momentarily suspended, when she opened it.
She had almost read it.
I sat down at my desk and looked at the north study’s window, which showed me nothing but the old silver trees at the far edge of the grounds, and I thought about the word almost with more attention than it strictly deserved.
* * *
She found the fox.
I had left it in the room because I had not been certain whether to leave it in the room. This was an unusual state of uncertainty for me — I was not, as a general rule, uncertain about the small practical decisions of territory management, which included the furnishing of rooms that had not been used in three centuries. I had furnished the room from the Hall’s stores with the efficiency of someone who had been maintaining a large building for a long time and knew what it contained.
And then I had stood in the doorway of the room with the fox in my hand and I had thought about the windowsill and I had put it there, because the room without it felt like a room with something missing — a thing I had not felt about the room in three centuries and did not examine now.
The Hall told me when she found it. I felt her take it from the sill. I felt the pause — the duration of attention she gave to it — and I felt when she set it down.
She put it back.
I was in the north study when this happened, and I had a piece of work in my hands, and I set the work down and I looked at the wall that pointed south and I thought about the fact that she had put it back.
Mortals took things. Objects in other people’s spaces were objects to be collected or ignored. The ones worth collecting got collected. I had watched this across a thousand years, across any number of mortal visitors to the territories, and it was so reliable as to constitute a law of its own.
She had held the fox long enough to read its history — I knew this because the Hall knew the duration of contact. She had understood it was important. And she had put it back exactly where it had been.
Exactly. The Hall’s awareness of spatial position was precise, and exact was the word — not approximately, not close enough. Exactly. The angle, the position of the tail relative to the window frame, the orientation of the ears. Exactly.
I sat in the north study for a while after that, not working.
Later, when I went to her room to confirm the evening arrangements — food, the Hall’s common room, the practical facts of habitation that I had told her about last night and needed to elaborate on — I came through the door and my eyes went to the windowsill before I directed them anywhere else.
She saw me look. I saw her see me look. We did not speak about it — a choice we made simultaneously without discussing it — the kind of agreement that did not require words and which I had not made with anyone in a very long time.
I told her about the food. About the Hall’s common room. I gave her the coat because she was going to be cold without it and she was going to argue about the coat if I let her and the coat was hers now, which I had decided last night for reasons I was not going to look at directly.
She said: “Thank you.”
There was something in the way she said it — not surprised, not effusive, just honest. The specific honesty of someone who did not say thank you automatically but meant it when they said it. I noted this and noted that I was noting it and left before either observation could develop into anything I was required to address.
The binding sat in my awareness with the ease of something settled.
That evening, after she had eaten and returned to her room and the Hall had gone into its night configuration, I sat in the library for the first time in several weeks. The old-language book was exactly where she had replaced it. I could see, in the slight disorder of the books on either side, that it had been removed and returned — the small disturbance of a careful person who had tried to leave no trace.
She had left a trace. The warm impression of hands on the cover, already fading. The slightly different angle at which the book now sat against its neighbor — close to exact, not perfect, the margin of error that even the most attentive person could not entirely eliminate.
I reached out and touched the cover.
Still warm.
I sat for a while and then I went to find Vasilisa, because I had more questions now than I had started the day with, and Vasilisa was the only being in existence who might have answers. This was, I was beginning to understand, going to be a regular feature of the coming months.
The binding was present in my awareness, steady as a compass north.
The Hall was pleased about everything. I found this enormously unhelpful and entirely accurate.
And then, as I stood to go find Vasilisa, the binding shifted.
Not her location. Not her state. Something else — a third awareness, brief and distant, touching the edge of Nochval’s territory like a finger pressed to glass. Checking. Measuring.
Another Vorath had just noticed something about the binding.
The awareness withdrew before I could identify the source. But the fact of it remained: someone else was paying attention to what should have been a routine Hunt catch and a routine year-long consequence.
The binding was not routine.
* * *
The communication from the eastern boundary arrived in the night’s second quarter.
Not Vasilisa. Not Radovan. The membrane carried it in the cold register — the one that belonged to a specific territory and a specific keeper and that I had not felt directed at Nochval in forty years.
Zora.
Not a message. A probe. The membrane equivalent of someone pressing a palm against a wall to feel whether the wall was hollow. She had felt the binding’s activation from her territory. She had felt the Claim. And she was, with the careful patience of someone who had been accumulating information for eight hundred years, beginning to establish what she was dealing with.
I reinforced the eastern membrane without responding. A response would confirm. Silence would merely delay.
But Zora did not need confirmation. Zora needed a crack, and the binding’s activation was the largest crack in Nochval’s boundary in a century.
I would need to tell the others. Not yet — but soon. The window for managing this quietly was narrowing.
And now someone else knew it.
