# Chapter Eight: What the Hollow Is Telling Me

**POV:** Aleksei · **Words (v1):** 3,946 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 4

---

I had been spending too much time at Nochval.
This was a fact I had established by the fifth day and had not done anything about, because doing something about it would have required acknowledging it as a problem, and I had not yet decided whether it was a problem or whether it was simply a change in the distribution of my attention that had not yet been assigned a category.
The territory required management. The membrane at the city’s eastern border had needed attention — I had given it attention.
The membrane’s condition was what it always was: thinner than the start of the season, by a fraction that had been accumulating for a hundred years. I had ceased counting. The answer was always the same. Less than before. Steadily. The kind of loss that was too slow to call a crisis and too consistent to call anything else.
 The Hunt’s administrative residue from the Veil Nights required processing — I had processed it. There were two other areas of the Hollow’s outer boundary that bore monitoring in the weeks following the membrane’s dissolution and reconstitution, and I was monitoring them. All of this was legitimate work that had a legitimate claim on my time, and all of it could have been conducted from anywhere in Nochval.
I was conducting it from the north study.
The north study was adjacent to the corridor that ran past the library. This was a coincidence of architecture rather than intention, and I was not going to examine it further.
The Hall’s awareness was the constant low current it had always been — the ambient knowing of a building that was also, in its particular eight-hundred-year-old way, alive. The Hall knew what moved through it. The Hall knew what was handled, what was opened, what was left where it had been or left slightly not where it had been. The Hall had been the keeper of Nochval’s interior memory since before most mortal civilizations had the concept of memory-keeping, and it performed this function with the thoroughness of something that found it intrinsically worthwhile rather than obligatory.
In the first week of Kira Voss’s residence in the Hall, the Hall had not stopped telling me things.
Not urgently. Not with alarm. The Hall found the mortal woman in the south-facing room significant in a way that it had not found anything significant in a very long time, and it communicated this continuously, and I had been receiving the communication with the flat professional attention I applied to the Hall’s reports and not examining what it meant that I was paying attention to every single one.
What I knew about the first week:
She slept in the coat.
This the Hall told me on the first morning, as information it considered relevant. She had taken it off, folded it over the chair at the desk with a care that the Hall registered as the care used for things belonging to other people, and had then put it back on before sleeping, which the Hall registered with the quality it used for things that were interesting. I received this information and set it aside and did not think about it for the rest of the morning and thought about it at irregular intervals for the rest of the day.
She had found the library on the first day, which I had expected — the library was findable by smell, for anyone paying the right quality of attention to their environment, and she paid that quality of attention to everything. She picked things up and put them back. The Hall noted, with something that translated approximately as approval, that she put things back precisely.
She had talked to the presences.
This was less expected. She had not just detected them. She had addressed them directly, in the conversational tone she used for everything, as if introducing herself to a building’s inhabitants were an ordinary courtesy and not something that had not happened in three centuries. She had told them she was going to be there for a year. She had thanked them for the tour.
The presences had been warm about it for days. They were still warm about it. They had not been warm about anything in three centuries and the warmth was, in the specific quiet way of eight-hundred-year-old accumulated attention, very loud.
I had not been to the library while she was in it. The decision was: her spaces were hers. The binding gave me what the law required. The Hall told me what the Hall told me. I was not going to add my own presence to that accounting.
These were the principles. They were clear. They were also, I was finding, easier to state than to maintain in the face of a Hall that was continuously reporting on what she was doing and a binding that was continuously reporting on how she was, and the gap between the report and the room was becoming, daily, slightly more noticeable.
On the seventh day, the Hall told me she had gone back to the old-language shelf.
* * *
I set down what I was working on.
She had found the book on the first day — I had felt her take it, and the Hall had communicated its particular quality of careful attention, and I had felt the shift when she opened it and then the different shift when she put it back exactly where it had been. I had gone to the library after she left and confirmed the exact. I had stood in front of the shelf and not taken the book down and had gone to find Vasilisa because I had questions I did not know how to ask yet.
Vasilisa had given me pieces. The Old Claim, she had said. Beings chosen by the first world to carry its memory forward. The last one died before the separation. The line was thought gone.
I had not told Vasilisa about the book. I had said: the binding activated. I had not said this because I had not yet decided what it meant and I did not discuss things I had not decided the meaning of, as a principle, and also because saying it aloud would have produced a register of reaction from Vasilisa that I was not prepared for in that particular conversation.
The Hall told me she had taken the book from the shelf again.
I stayed in the north study.
The Hall told me she had sat down with it. That she had opened it. That she was looking at the first page.
I stayed in the north study.
The Hall told me she had turned to the second page.
I stayed — with somewhat more effort than I generally applied to staying — in the north study.
And then the Hall told me something it had never told me in eight hundred years of telling me things, in a register I had not felt it use before: not the warmth of approval, not the weight of significance, not the careful quiet of something protecting something delicate. Something older than all of those. Something the Hall used for the first time in my eight centuries of knowing it, because this was the first time it had had occasion to use it.
She had touched a mark on the page.
And the mark had answered.
I went very still.
I had been still before — I was always still, it was the default condition of something that had lived long enough that movement required a reason — but this was a different quality of stillness. The kind that came from the outside in rather than the inside out. The kind that happened when something external produced a state that the body arrived at before the mind had processed the cause.
The mark had answered her.
In eight hundred years of that book sitting on that shelf, in the centuries before the Hall was built when the book had existed in the old territory and the Hollow had known it was there and left it alone with the respect the Hollow afforded to things that were older than it — in all of that time, across everything the book had witnessed and survived and held — the marks in it had never answered anyone. The book was written in the first language. The first language had two speakers remaining in existence. I was one of them.
The mark had answered her.
I looked at the wall that faced south. I looked at it for a considerable amount of time. Then I said, quietly, to the north study and the work I was not doing and the eight hundred years of accumulated walls and stone and careful construction around me: “Not yet.”
Not yet meaning: I am not ready for what this means. Not yet meaning: let me have another day of not knowing before I have to know. Not yet meaning the thing underneath all of it that I had no adequate language for, which was that for the first time since before the sealed wing had a reason to be sealed, something in the world had surprised me badly enough that I needed a moment before I could face it.
Not yet.
The Hall was very quiet. Even the presences had stilled — the ambient warmth of them reduced to something barely perceptible, the specific quiet of things that understood the weight of a moment and were giving it the space it required.
In the library, the binding told me, she had lifted her finger from the page. She was still. She was breathing slowly and deliberately — the pattern she used — I had learned it across seven days of the binding’s constant directional awareness — when she was managing something rather than reacting to it. She was managing.
Of course she was.
* * *
I went to Vasilisa in the late afternoon.
Not to the Hall — she was not at the Hall, had not been since her visit on the second day. Vasilisa maintained her own territory in the older part of the Hollow, a region that predated most of the current geography, accessible through the deep membrane-paths that I knew by long practice and that most other beings used maps for. I went through them quickly, with the economy of something that had made this journey more times than it could usefully count, and arrived at the edge of her territory as the Hollow’s sky was cycling toward its mid-afternoon saturation — the deepest blue before the slow descent toward evening.
Vasilisa’s territory did not look like Nochval. It had everything — colors the Hollow’s palette didn’t include, textures from several ecosystems at once. The ground shifted underfoot like a landscape that enjoyed being different. Through the shifts I caught glimpses of stone walls and the dim shape of a hill, all of it transparent, all of it real.
She was in the thin-membrane region when I arrived. She was sitting on a rock that existed in both registers — solid in the Hollow, the barest suggestion of itself in Zemya — with a book open in her lap that she was not reading. Her mismatched eyes were on the ghost-landscape of the mortal world, the one-amber-one-grey tracking something in the middle distance that had nothing to do with what was physically present.
She said, without looking up: “You felt it.”
“Yes,” I said.
“So did I.” She closed the book. Not marking her page — she did not mark pages, she had the Vorath of Memory’s perfect recall of text and had never needed a bookmark in her existence. “Tell me exactly what you felt.”
I told her. The Hall’s report, the mark responding, the quality of the activation — as precise as I could be about something that had no precedent in my experience and therefore no existing vocabulary. I told her what the activation felt like from the Hall’s end: not the warmth of approval, not significance. Older. Something the Hall had been waiting to use.
Vasilisa was quiet for a long time after I finished.
Her quiet was different from mine. I was still because movement required a reason. She went there the way other people went to libraries: purposefully, with a specific question, returning with whatever the archive chose to give her.
When she came back, her expression had the particular quality I had learned to read as: I have found something significant and I am deciding how to give it to you.
“The Old Claim,” she said, “is not passive.” She stood from the rock. “The Claim is the Hollow recognizing something in the carrier. A capacity. A specific resonance with the deep current that goes beyond what the binding measures. The Old Claim is the Hollow saying: this one. Not for us. For itself.”
“The book,” I said.
“The book is one of them,” Vasilisa said. “The marks in it are not writing, Aleksei. They are not a record. They are the first world’s own language — not a language that was developed to describe things, but a language that is things. The marks carry the actual content, not a representation of it.” She stopped walking. She turned to look at me with both mismatched eyes. “The first language can only be read by something carrying the Old Claim. It cannot be learned. It cannot be translated. It responds to the Claim or it does not respond at all.”
The clearing was quiet. The ghost-landscape of Zemya moved through its afternoon in the thin-membrane shimmer — clouds passing over a distant hill, the suggestion of wind in grass that was not quite there.
“She received one mark,” I said.
“One,” Vasilisa agreed. “The first one on the second page.”
“Beginning.”
Vasilisa looked at me steadily. “How do you know what the mark says.”
I said nothing. She asked this one because she wanted me to say it aloud. Because saying things aloud was the mechanism by which you moved them from the private register into the world, and she had decided this particular thing needed to be in the world.
“I wrote part of that book,” I said.
“I know,” she said. Gently, for Vasilisa — not a word she deployed without intention.
“Before the separation,” I said. “Before most of what currently exists had been decided. The second page — the beginning of the actual content — I wrote that page.” I looked at the ghost-landscape. At the hill in Zemya that was barely a shadow of itself here in the thin membrane. “The mark she touched was mine.”
The clearing absorbed this the way the Hollow absorbed most things: quietly, without drama, with the patience of something that had been present for all of it and had opinions about none of it. Vasilisa did not say anything for a moment. When she spoke, her voice had the quality it used when she was being careful on behalf of someone else rather than herself.
“You need to tell her,” she said. “Not all of it — not yet. But something. She is building a sequence of questions and she has the wrong one at the top.” A pause. “She’s going to get to the right one on her own very soon. It would be better if she arrived there with some of the foundation already in place.”
“I know,” I said.
“When.”
I thought about the not-yet I had said to the north study walls three hours ago. About the first-world language that had two speakers remaining. About a mortal woman in a coat that was hers now, sitting in a library building a sequence, already almost at the right question.
“When I have the right words,” I said.
Vasilisa looked at me for a long moment. “You wrote part of that book,” she said. “Before the separation. Before most things. You have had the longest possible time to find words for this.”
“I know,” I said.
“So the question,” she said, with the particular Vasilisa quality of saying the hard thing in the lightest possible tone, “is not whether you have words. It’s whether you intend to use them.”
I looked at the ghost-landscape of Zemya. At the hill. At the distance.
“I’m working on it,” I said.
Vasilisa smiled, the real one, the one she kept behind the bright social performance. “I know,” she said. “I can tell.” She retrieved her book from the rock and turned toward the deeper part of her territory. “Go home, Aleksei. She’s been alone all day.”
“She is not alone,” I said. “She has the presences. She has—”
“She is alone,” Vasilisa said, over her shoulder, already walking. “Go home.”
I went home.
* * *
The binding recalibrated as I crossed back into Nochval’s territory — the directional sense settling from the extended range into the closer resolution it maintained within the Hall’s grounds. She was in her room. Stationary. At her desk, from the specificity of the sense, which I had learned to read to that degree of precision over seven days and which I was not going to examine the implications of tonight.
I went through the silver trees slowly.
Slowly because there was no urgency — she was in her room, she was well, the binding reported nothing that required action. Not because I was finding, in the late-Hollow-afternoon light of the oldest trees, that the boundary between working out what to say to her and simply returning to the building she was in had become less clear than it had been a week ago.
The shadow-fox was at the treeline. It watched me pass without moving. Its eyes, which were the cold-light quality of all its kind, tracked me through the trees with the patient following attention it had been directing at the Hall since the second day.
It was not watching the Hall.
It was watching the Hall’s south-facing window, third floor.
I looked at the shadow-fox. It looked at me. We maintained this for a moment — the specific quality of two beings who both understood something about the situation and were not going to discuss it, because the shadow-fox did not speak and I was not going to be the one to articulate it.
I walked on. The shadow-fox returned its attention to the window. There was something in its quality of attending that was not territorial and not predatory and that I recognized, with the particular discomfort of recognizing something you have been trying not to name, as the same quality I produced when I stood in the north study adjacent to the library corridor.
The Hollow was paying attention to her. The Hollow’s creatures were paying attention to her. The Hall’s presences were warmer than they had been in three centuries. My binding — the one that had activated when no binding had activated in forty-seven Hunts — sat in my awareness with the ease of something that had always been there.
I had said not yet to the north study walls.
I was going to need better walls.
* * *
I was at the Hall’s entrance when I felt the membrane-signature.
Not a threat — nothing in the signature was hostile, nothing was wrong. The double-presence of it. The way it felt in the Hunt-sense like two things occupying the same point, overlapping with the precision of something that had been overlapping for a very long time.
I recognized the signature the way you recognized something you had known for centuries: not because you had to think about it, but because recognition was the immediate and involuntary response to a familiar thing.
I stopped walking.
I looked at the membrane’s surface at the eastern edge of Nochval’s territory, where the thin-place ran along the old boundary I had maintained since before the Hall was built. The signature was there. It had been there, I understood, for some hours — in the way of something that had arrived at the membrane’s surface and had been sitting there, in both registers at once, feeling what was on the other side.
And now it was moving.
Toward the Hall. Specifically, with the directional certainty of something that knew exactly where it was going, toward the Hall’s east face. Which contained the east corridor. Which contained the library.
I stood at the Hall’s entrance and I thought about the mark on the second page of the book in the first language, and the thing that had answered when she touched it, and the membrane-signature that had been sitting at Nochval’s eastern boundary since she had been in the library this morning, and the movement that had started exactly now.
I said nothing for a long moment.
Then, to the evening air, to the silver trees, to the shadow-fox watching its window: “Of course.”
I went inside.
The Hall, which was aware of everything that moved within its boundary, was already warm with the quality it used for things it found significant. It had been waiting for this particular significant thing, I understood, for considerably longer than it had been waiting for Kira Voss, and this was saying something, because the Hall had been waiting for Kira Voss since before she had a name.
I did not go to the east corridor.
Whatever was about to happen in the library was going to happen whether or not I was present for it, and my presence would complicate it in ways that the library and its new inhabitant did not require from me tonight. They were going to have a conversation that had nothing to do with the binding or the law or the position I occupied in this territory, and that was the kind of conversation that was best had without me in the room.
I went to the north study. I sat down. I picked up the work I had left undone all day. The membrane at the eastern border required attention and I was going to attend to it.
The binding told me she was still at her desk. Still stationary. And then — after a pause that the binding communicated as a shift in her awareness, the recalibration she produced when something unexpected had entered her register — no longer stationary.
She was moving toward the library.
I set down the work again. I looked at the north study wall. I thought about the membrane-signature now moving through the Hall’s east corridor, and about a mortal woman with a list of questions and a fox on her windowsill and a second-page mark that had said beginning to her through a touch, walking toward whatever was waiting for her in the library.
And then I thought: not anymore.
The Hall’s warmth deepened, slow and certain, in the way of something that had been patient for a very long time and was now, finally, watching things begin to go the way they were always going to go.
I picked up the work.
I did not put it down again.
This was progress.
The binding pulsed once — not urgent, not alarming, simply present in a way it had not been present before. The quality of something that had been waiting and was now beginning to move.
I did not know what it meant.
But the membrane at the eastern boundary had thinned by a measurable degree since the last time I had checked it. Three days ago.
Three days was not enough for measurable thinning. Not under normal conditions.
The conditions were no longer normal.
