# Chapter Fifteen: How Interesting

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 4,769 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 6

---

I knew something was wrong before I was fully awake.
Not wrong in the way of danger presenting itself — wrong in the way of an environment that had changed register overnight, the register of a space that had been one thing when I went to sleep and was another thing when I woke up. I had developed this sense in Vreshka over years of sleeping in places that were not always safe, where the difference between a room at rest and a room with a problem in it was sometimes the only information you had before you had to be already moving.
I lay still for a moment and gathered the available data.
The Hall was cold. Not the ambient cold of the Hollow that I had acclimated to over six weeks and that registered as normal now — colder than that. Specifically colder, in the way of a temperature that was not produced by the environment but imposed on it, pushed into the air from somewhere external and sitting there in the Hall’s rooms and corridors like a presence rather than a climate.
The presences were quiet.
This was the thing that moved me from lying still to sitting up. The presences were quiet in a way I had not felt them be quiet before. Not the exhaled quiet of after Sable’s first visit, satisfied and settled. This was smaller. Pulled in. The particular feel of things that had made themselves less visible, less present, less loud, because something was in the Hall that they did not want to attract the attention of.
The presences were afraid.
I was out of bed before I finished forming the thought.
The binding: Aleksei was at the Hall’s main entrance. Not the north study, not the territory — the entrance, stationary, with the feel of state the binding communicated as alert in the specific way I had come to read as: he is positioned, he has made a decision about positioning, he is between something and something else.
I put on the coat. I went downstairs.
* * *
She was already inside.
I heard her before I saw her — or rather, I felt her, different from the feeling of Aleksei’s presence or Vasilisa’s or Sable’s or Radovan’s brief visits, all of which had a quality I could identify and place. It was the quality of winter. Not winter as a season, not the cold of low temperatures and bare trees — winter as a force. The specific property of a thing that isolated other things from each other, that turned the air between people into a barrier rather than a medium.
Then I came around the corner of the main hall corridor and saw her, and I understood that I had not prepared adequately for what she would look like.
She was standing near the main hall’s center table — the Vorath never sat. She was tall the way Aleksei was tall, more present than the space usually accommodated. Her eyes, when they found me — and they found me the instant I came into the room, before I had fully turned the corner, before I had made any sound — were a pale grey so close to colorless that in the Hall’s ambient light they registered as silver and in another light would have been almost transparent.
She was beautiful in the way that very dangerous things were sometimes beautiful: completely, without apology, as a property of what they were rather than an effort they made.
Aleksei was between her and the room’s interior. Not dramatically — the geometry of it was not a blockade, not a statement. But he was positioned between the door and where I now stood, with the specific arrangement of a person who had placed himself there before I arrived and had done it quietly enough that only someone paying the right kind of attention to geometry would notice.
I noticed.
He looked at me when I came in. Something in his expression was controlled more completely than I had seen it controlled in six weeks — not the ordinary controlled nothing, but a different layer of it, a managed quality that had more effort in it than usual, which told me everything I needed to know about what the usual cost of that control was and what it was costing him now.
“Kira,” he said. His voice was even. Completely. “This is Zora.”
Zora looked at me with the near-transparent eyes. She smiled.
The smile did not reach her eyes, not because she was concealing something, but because her eyes were a different system from the smile entirely — they were assessing, precise, the weight of something that looked at things to know them rather than to communicate about the looking. The smile was courteous. The eyes were working.
“Kira,” she said. Her voice was the voice of the Hall’s cold this morning — the same quality, the same register, something that moved through the air in a way that left the air a little less warm after it had passed. “I’ve heard about you.”
“I’ve heard about you,” I said.
It was not, technically, true. I had inferred her at the eastern boundary from the Hollow’s alertness and Aleksei’s geometry and the presences’ behavior now. I had not been told her name until thirty seconds ago.
But she did not need to know the specifics.
Something moved in her expression — not warmth, something sharper than warmth, the quality of interest arriving in a face that had not been expecting to find something interesting. Her eyes completed their assessment and settled. “Did you,” she said.
“You were at the eastern boundary yesterday,” I said. “The Hollow went on alert.”
The room was very quiet. The presences, which had been making themselves small since before I came downstairs, were doing their absolute minimum — barely a warmth, barely a presence, the posture of things that had pressed themselves flat against the walls and were hoping not to be noticed. I was aware of Aleksei’s stillness to my left, the particular quality of it, the stillness that was working.
“It did,” Zora said. She tilted her head slightly — a small movement, the specific gesture of something recalibrating an estimate. “You read the Hollow’s atmospheric state.”
“I’m learning to,” I said.
“In six weeks,” she said. Not a question. A measurement.
“I have a good teacher,” I said.
* * *
The conversation that followed was the most precisely managed conversation I had ever been in, including negotiations in Vreshka’s lower city where the wrong word cost money and the word after that sometimes cost something more permanent.
Zora was not hostile. That was the first and most important thing to establish, and I established it in the first five minutes with the attention I applied to reading rooms: she was not hostile to me. She was something that required more precision than hostile — she was interested, in the way of something that had encountered an unexpected variable and was assessing its properties and implications with the thoroughness of someone for whom variables were either assets or problems and the determination of which category applied was a matter of gathering sufficient information.
She asked about Zemya. Zora’s questions about Zemya were specific: which city, which district, what work, how long had I been doing it, had I been in the lower city all my life. Not interest in Zemya. Interest in me, specifically, in the contours of the life that had produced me and whether those contours told her anything useful.
I answered accurately and without elaboration — the strategy for conversations where the information you gave was going to be used in ways you hadn’t agreed to. Accurate, because she was the kind of person who would identify a lie with the efficiency of someone who had been listening to people for centuries. Without elaboration, because elaboration was what gave you away — not the facts, the shape you put around the facts.
She asked about the coat.
She did it so smoothly that it was nearly not a question — a passing observation, a small comment about what I was wearing worked into a longer sentence about the Hall’s temperature. “The cold has been unusual this week,” she said. “Though I see you’ve come prepared.” “Old,” she said. “Aleksei’s, unless I’m mistaken.”
“Mine,” I said.
Her eyes moved to him. His face gave her nothing — the full controlled nothing, the absolute surface. She looked back at me. “Of course,” she said pleasantly. “My mistake.”
It was not her mistake. She had not made a mistake. She had taken a measurement and received an answer she had been expecting and had filed both the question and the answer in whatever accounting she kept, and the pleasantly was the polished surface over a quality that was not pleasant and was not interested in being pleasant, it was interested in information and it had gotten some.
I held her gaze. She held mine. We maintained this with the understanding of two people who knew exactly what had just happened and were not going to discuss it directly.
“You’re not afraid,” she said.
The observation had the feel of something being said aloud that had been noted internally some time ago. “Should I be,” I said.
“Most mortals are,” she said. Not unkindly — factually. “At this proximity. The Vorath presence is uncomfortable for mortal sensory systems. You’ve been here long enough to acclimate to Aleksei and to some degree Nochval’s territory generally. I’m a new presence. The discomfort should be significant.”
“It’s significant,” I said honestly. “You’re very cold. The Hall has gotten cold since you arrived and I can feel that it’s you producing it rather than the environment. It’s not comfortable.” I paused. “That’s not the same as afraid.”
She looked at me for a long moment with the near-transparent eyes. Then something shifted in her expression — not warmth, not softening, but the quality of genuine attention arriving on a face that had previously been deploying polished performance. She was actually looking at me now. Not measuring, not assessing for the accounting. Looking.
“No,” she said. “It isn’t.” A pause, exactly calibrated. “How interesting.”
These were the two most alarming words anyone had said to me in six weeks, and I had been caught by an ancient god-king in an alley during the Hunt.
* * *
She stayed for forty minutes.
She and Aleksei spoke for a portion of that time in the specific register of two beings who had a great deal of history and were not going to perform warmth about it — not cold exactly, not hostile, simply the feel of two people who knew each other too well for courtesy to be necessary and too cautiously for ease to be comfortable. I sat at the table and I listened and I gathered the things available in the listening.
Zora’s territory bordered Nochval’s to the east. This I had established from the boundary visit. What I established now was the specific nature of that border — it was old, and had been renegotiated twice in the last century, and the renegotiation had not gone smoothly, and the way Aleksei referenced the second renegotiation, very briefly, with a quality in his voice I had not heard him use for anything else, told me the second renegotiation had cost something beyond territory.
She had known about my presence since the night of the Hunt. This was not explicitly stated — it was present in the way she talked about the Veil Nights and the Hunt’s outcome and the specific question she asked about whether all thirteen Vorath had been present for the Veil Nights, which she knew the answer to, which told me the question was not about information but about what the answer would tell her about what he would say. He said yes, all thirteen, and gave nothing further, and she received this with the pleasantly that was not pleasant and moved on.
She had not come for forty minutes of courtesy. She had come to see me. I understood this by the twenty-minute mark and confirmed it at thirty-five when she turned back to me in the middle of a sentence directed at him and asked, with the specific precision of something that had been working toward a question the entire time: “How are you finding the Hollow’s library?”
I went still.
Not visibly — I had been keeping my visible stillness consistent throughout and I was not going to change it now. But internally, in the place where the question landed: still. She knew about the library. She knew about the books. She knew about the old-language shelf, or she was feeling for whether she should know.
“Extensive,” I said. “I’ve been working through the historical sections. I don’t have enough of some of the languages yet for the older materials.”
“The older materials require particular preparation,” she said, with the same pleasant surface over the working machinery of her attention. “Some of them are not safe for mortals without the right foundation.”
“I’ve been careful,” I said.
“I’m sure you have,” she said, and looked at Aleksei, and the look between them was a conversation I was not part of and that lasted perhaps two seconds and that communicated more in those two seconds than most conversations communicated in an hour.
He said nothing. His face gave her nothing. But the nothing had a quality — resolute, the feel of a door that was not going to open regardless of what was applied to it — and she read it and looked back at her hands and said, “Well,” in the tone of someone setting a conversation down temporarily.
She left five minutes later. The Hall’s warmth did not fully return for eleven minutes. I counted. Zora’s passage had cost us something, and I did not yet know what.
* * *
The Hall took ten minutes to warm up after she left.
Not gradually — in increments, each one perceptible, the specific process of a space recovering from a cold that had been imposed rather than natural. Then the ambient temperature of the rooms, rising degree by degree back toward the normal that I had spent six weeks establishing as my baseline. Then the presences fully, with the specific warmth I had come to know as the Hall at ease — present, attending, warm with the sense of something that found its current state satisfactory.
I sat at the table and watched the Hall warm up and catalogued the process with the same attention I gave everything, because the process was information and the information was that even the Hall, which was eight hundred years old and had been built by a being older than that and was not a thing that alarmed easily, had been suppressed by Zora’s presence for the full forty minutes.
Aleksei came back into the main hall after seeing her out. He had the expression that was not any of the catalogued ones — not the controlled nothing, not the almost-not-a-smile, not the new one from the forest. This was another new one, and it had the manner of someone who had just managed something difficult and was now assessing how the management had gone.
“She was not hostile,” I said.
“No,” he said.
“That’s worse.”
He looked at me with the silver eyes in the warming room and something shifted in the expression — not becoming warm exactly, but less managed. More present. “Yes,” he said.
“She came to see what I was,” I said. “She already knew I was here. She came because she needed to determine for herself what the information meant.”
He was quiet.
“She asked about the library,” I said.
“I know,” he said. And then: “I heard the question.”
“She knows about the old-language section.” I was not asking. I was building the sequence out loud, because building it out loud was faster than building it silently when the person across from you had more information than you did and you needed them to confirm the architecture so you could work with it. “She doesn’t know what I’ve done in it or she was testing whether you would tell her. You didn’t tell her anything.”
“No,” he said.
“You’re not going to tell her,” I said.
“No,” he said, with the flat certainty that was the door that would not open. Not protective in the performed sense. Decided. The way of someone for whom the decision had been made before the situation presented itself and was not going to be reconsidered by the situation.
I looked at him across the table in the warming hall. The presences were at full warmth now, recovered, producing the quality of the Hall at ease. The coat was on my shoulders. The question that had been working toward the top of my sequence for two weeks pressed against the back of my throat with the specific urgency of something whose time was nearly here.
Not yet. Not this moment. This moment was for the Zora accounting, for the building of the sequence that now included her.
“She’s going to come back,” I said.
“Yes,” he said.
“And she’ll be watching now. For whatever she came to find.” I thought about the near-transparent eyes and the measurement quality of them and the specific sharpening of her attention when I had said it’s not the same as afraid. “I think she found it interesting that I wasn’t afraid.”
“So did I,” he said.
I looked at him. This one was simpler than all of those. Simpler and, in its simplicity, more legible. As if the management had gotten tired for exactly one moment and stepped back from the door.
I held it for as long as it lasted, which was perhaps three seconds, and then he moved — back to the door, toward the north wing, with the economy of motion that always indicated the conversation had reached its end and he had somewhere to be. “I’ll tell you about her,” he said, at the threshold. “When I have—”
“The right words,” I said.
“Yes,” he said.
“How long,” I said. Not impatiently — genuinely. I wanted to know the timeline.
He stopped at the threshold. He did not turn around, but the set of his shoulders changed — a small shift, something processing. “Soon,” he said. “Before she comes back.”
Then he was gone. I sat in the warming hall with the presences and the coat and the fox on the windowsill upstairs and the question that was now pressing against the top of its sequence like something that had run out of patience with being second.
* * *
Sable arrived forty minutes later.
She had not been in the Hall during Zora’s visit — I had noticed her absence and had not had time to think about it — and she came in through the east corridor door with the quality she sometimes had of arriving from the membrane’s surface rather than through the Hall’s normal entrances, a slight density to the air around her that resolved into her solidity over the first few seconds of her presence in a room.
She looked at me. She looked at the hall around us. She assessed the residual cold with the specific attention of someone taking a temperature reading. “How long was she here,” she said.
“Forty minutes,” I said.
Sable sat across from me at the table. Her hair was in its deepest register today, nearly the not-black of the Hollow’s nadir sky, which I had learned to read as: the membrane had been active recently, the Hollow’s atmosphere had been doing something that required Sable’s particular attention. She looked at the table for a moment and then looked at me. “Are you all right,” she said.
“Yes,” I said. “She wasn’t hostile. She was—”
“Interested,” Sable said. “I know.”
“You’ve met her,” I said.
“Many times,” she said, in the tone she used for things that had a history she was not going to provide all of right now. “The membrane runs along Nochval’s eastern boundary because Zora’s territory is cold enough to keep it thin. We have overlapping interests in the thin-membrane regions and therefore overlapping presence in the same spaces.” A pause. “It is not an easy relationship.”
I thought about the Hall going cold. About the presences pressing flat against the walls. About Sable, who had been in this Hall for eight hundred years and who the presences adored, choosing not to be in the Hall while Zora was in it. “You stayed outside,” I said.
“Her presence and mine in the same space produces complications,” she said carefully. “Winter and the membrane do not run together easily. I thought it was more useful for you to meet her without that complication in the room.”
I filed this in the place I was keeping information about Zora and what she represented. “She asked about the library,” I said. “The old-language section.”
Sable went still in the specific way — not her usual not-quite-solid quality, but a sharpening. “What did you say.”
“That I didn’t have enough of some of the older languages yet,” I said. “I didn’t mention the book. Or what happened with the mark.”
Sable held my gaze for a moment. Then something in her expression settled — not relief exactly, the Remnant version of it, which was more like: the thing that was a risk is still contained. “Good,” she said quietly. “That was exactly right.”
“Why does it matter what Zora knows,” I said. “About the book. About the Claim.”
Sable was quiet for a moment. This was not the calibrated quiet she used for deciding how much information to give — this was the real kind, the kind where she was working something out in the private interior of her own understanding before she brought it out. When she spoke, it was in the voice she used for things she was saying because they needed to be said rather than because she had decided the moment was right.
“The Old Claim makes you significant,” she said. “Not to me, not to the Hall, not to Aleksei — those are personal responses. The Claim makes you significant to the Hollow itself. You are the first carrier with a full Claim resonance in three hundred years. Do you understand what that means for the politics?”
“And when she does,” I said.
“When she does,” Sable said, “things become considerably more complicated. For you. And for him.”
The hall was fully warm now. The presences were present, attending, producing their ordinary warmth. The coat was on my shoulders, mine now in the way things were mine that had been given with care, and the shadow-fox was somewhere in the territory, and the binding put Aleksei in the north wing still, close and stationary, thinking something through in the room he had gone to when the forest had brought the atmospheric shift and the shadow-fox had done the unprecedented thing.
For you, Sable had said. And for him.
The and for him was the part I was going to think about.
“Sable,” I said.
“Mm.”
“The right question,” I said. “The one that goes first. Is it about the Claim.”
She looked at me with the membrane-colored eyes that always pulled at my recognition and said nothing for a moment. Then: “It is adjacent to the Claim,” she said. “The Claim is the answer. The question is about you.”
“Soon,” she said. “I think you’re ready.”
I had been almost ready for three weeks. I was not sure ready was the right word for what I currently was. But I nodded, and Sable went to make tea, and the Hall was warm around us with the specific warmth of something that had been suppressed and had recovered its full nature, and somewhere upstairs the fox sat on the windowsill attending to the middle distance with its composed ears forward expression, and I sat at the table and thought about the question that was about me.
About what I was.
About why an ancient being with near-transparent eyes had looked at me, for two seconds, with genuine attention rather than assessment and said: how interesting.
I added a line to my mental notes, in the medium handwriting, below the entry about Zora’s visit:
She is not the danger. The danger is what she represents — the world beyond this territory learning what the Hollow already knows about me, before I know it myself.
And below that, very small, in handwriting that had no category because I only wrote it to have written it:
He said soon. Before she comes back.
I thought: then let her come back quickly.
I was ready.
Sable appeared in the main hall doorway an hour after Zora left. She did not come all the way into the room — she stood at the threshold with the manner of someone delivering information that was not going to be pleasant.
“She will tell them,” Sable said. “About you. About the binding’s behavior. About the library.”
“I know,” I said.
“And they will have opinions,” Sable said. “About what you are. About what you mean for the territorial balance. About whether restoration is actually what Nochval needs or whether it’s what Aleksei wants.” She paused. “Some of those opinions will not be friendly.”
* * *
The book was wrong.
I went to the library the morning after Zora’s departure and the reaching-toward quality — the mutual pull between carrier and text that had been constant for weeks — was gone. The book was paper. Ink. Binding. Nothing else.
Aleksei was in the courtyard. The binding had already told him something was wrong. I could see it in his posture — the shift from territory-attention to her-attention.
“The book,” I said.
He was already moving.
He held it for a long time, the silver eyes listening rather than reading. “The suppression came from outside the territory,” he said. His voice was even but the binding was telling me something I hadn’t felt from him before. Cold anger, running deep. “Through the membrane. At the eastern boundary, where Zora’s passage weakened the barrier.”
“She did this?”
“Not Zora. The political cost of direct sabotage to a Claim artifact is absolute. But her passage created a window. Someone used it.”
Maren. Neither of us said the name.
“How long until it comes back?” I said.
“I need to close the breach at the boundary. Four hours. While I am there, I am not here.”
“Go,” I said. “The Hall won’t let anything else through.”
He looked at me with something that cracked the controlled surface — the weight of a person about to leave the thing they were protecting.
“I’ll be here,” I said. “I am not the kind of person who needs to be told twice to stay inside.”
“You went out on the Veil Nights for sixty silver marks.”
“That was my money. This is my book.”
He left at Vorath speed. The Hall went quiet around me. The presences pulled tight.
I sat in the library with my hand on a dead book and waited.
The book came back in the fourth hour — gradually, like warmth returning to a room after a fire is relit. But different. The reaching-toward was urgent now, less patient, as if the suppression had taught it something about the cost of interruption.
When Aleksei returned: “The breach is closed. I found evidence of deliberate manipulation. Not proof enough for a formal accusation. But enough to know.”
“Enough to know it’s not theoretical anymore,” I said.
“Yes. The politics have become practical.”
I lost two days. Two marks I could not get back. And the timeline to the convening had just gotten shorter by exactly what we could least afford.
I looked at her. “How unfriendly.”
“The kind that sends representatives to convenings,” Sable said. “The kind that votes.”
I thought: then let her come back quickly. I was ready.
I was ready, but I suspected I was ready. But the book on the shelf had gone quiet again — not the reaching-toward silence of something waiting, but the held-breath silence of something that had been told to stop. Someone had reached into our library a second time. And this time they had not taken anything. They had left a message.
