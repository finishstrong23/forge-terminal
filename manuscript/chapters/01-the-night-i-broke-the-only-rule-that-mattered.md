# Chapter One: The Night I Broke the Only Rule That Mattered

**POV:** Kira · **Words (v1):** 2,678 · **Scene breaks (v1):** 5

---

The first rule of the Veil Nights was also the only rule, because it was the only one you needed: stay inside.
Every door in Vreshka was locked by sundown. Every shutter latched. Mothers had been telling children for generations that the Veil Nights were not a time for curiosity, not a time for bravery, not a time for anything except sitting very still in rooms with thick walls and pretending the sounds outside were just the wind.
I knew all of this.
I was also the only person moving in the lower city of Vreshka. I had either made a calculated decision or a catastrophic error, and I was currently telling myself it was the former.
Drev had my money.
That was the beginning and the middle and the end of my reasoning, and I stand by it: Drev had my money. He’d also conveniently forgotten to be anywhere in Vreshka that I could reach him. For four days he’d been a ghost, and I’d been patient, because I am a patient person when patience is strategically useful.
Patience had stopped being strategically useful approximately six hours ago, when I’d spotted him ducking into an alley off the Saltfish Market and realized that he’d been in the city the whole time. He’d just been inside, because he knew what tonight was. He’d been counting on me being inside too.
A reasonable person would have been inside.
* * *
The lower city of Vreshka was built for fog. In daylight it smelled of woodsmoke and fish and the river mud that got into everything — into your clothes, your hair, the cracks in your boots. At night, the fog came off the Vres River and filled the streets to knee height, and the gas lamps on the main thoroughfares did nothing except make the fog glow a sickly amber, arguably worse than darkness.
Tonight the gas lamps were out. Every one. Someone had gone through and extinguished them before sundown, because light attracted attention, and attention was the last thing Vreshka wanted on the first night of the Veil Nights.
I moved by memory and ambient gray. I knew this part of the city — every shortcut, every dead-end, every shop that stayed open late for the wrong reasons. Drev’s place was four streets south, in the canal district where the buildings listed and the rents were low.
The city was not quiet.
That was the thing that had been sitting wrong since I’d left my room two hours ago — not the emptiness of the streets, but the texture of the silence. Vreshka was never truly quiet. Tonight there was none of that. It was the silence of a city holding its breath.
Everyone in Vreshka knew what the Hollow did during the Veil Nights. The descriptions were always wrong. The reality was: the world softened at its edges. Sounds arrived from the wrong direction. Distances became unreliable. And for six hours, something ancient moved through the streets.
Tonight every street felt like those streets.
The cold that came with this particular brand of wrong was different from winter cold. Winter cold was honest — it bit at your ears and your fingers and it was trying to. This cold was incidental. It was the cold of something very large moving through the air nearby, leaving a wake the way a ship left a wake, not intending the disturbance but creating it anyway.
I kept moving. I turned up my collar and kept moving, because Drev had my money.
* * *
I found him in the cellar of a boarding house on Tanners’ Row.
This took me forty minutes, two wrong turns, and the services of a woman named Ora who ran numbers for six different crews in the lower city and owed me a favor from last winter. Practical woman. I respected her enormously.
The boarding house cellar smelled of mildew and old wine. Drev was sitting on a crate with my sixty silver marks in a leather pouch on his knee. He had the expression of a man who had expected this conversation and had rehearsed several versions of it, none of which were going to work.
The problem had appeared.
I sat down on the crate across from him and waited. I was comfortable with silence. Drev was not.
“Kira,” he said. “I was going to come find you.”
“Were you,” I said.
“Things got complicated.”
“Did they.”
He shifted on his crate. The bottle moved in his hands. “The merchant — Petrov, the one you got the ledger for — he came asking questions. Wanted to know where I’d sourced it. I had to go quiet for a few days until it blew over.”
“That,” I said, “sounds like a problem you have. Not a reason I don’t have sixty silver marks.”
A pause. He was recalculating something behind his eyes — I could see it, the way I could always see it, the little internal machinery of a person deciding which angle to try next. I’d had this conversation, or versions of it, more times than I could count. I was very rarely surprised by where they went.
“I can get you the marks,” he said finally. “Not tonight. I’d need to access the warehouse, and tonight—” He gestured vaguely upward, indicating, presumably, the entire situation happening above street level.
“Tonight works for me,” I said. “I have flexible hours.”
He stared at me. “Kira. It’s the Veil Nights.”
“I’m aware.”
“The Hunt—”
“The Hunt is for people who are outside after the bells,” I said. “We’ll be quick. The warehouse is four streets from here, which means we’ll be back inside before the hour turns.” I paused. “Or you can give me your keys and I’ll go myself, and you can trust me with access to your warehouse, which I notice you’re hesitating about.”
He was quiet for a long moment. Then: “You’re insane.”
“I’ve heard that,” I said. “The keys, Drev.”
He gave me the keys.
We went to the warehouse. He counted out sixty marks into my hand with the mechanical speed of a man who wanted this transaction to be over as quickly as possible. I counted them again, because you always count again, and the number came to sixty, and I put them in the inner pocket of my coat where they’d be uncomfortable for anyone who wanted to relieve me of them.
Then I told Drev to go back to his cellar and stay there, and I headed for home.
* * *
I should have made it.
My room was twelve minutes from Tanners’ Row by the route I took — through the Saltfish Market, up the Weavers’ Steps, across the small bridge over the canal that smelled of brackish water year-round, into the neighborhood called the Warrens where the buildings were older and cheaper and the streets barely deserved the name. Twelve minutes. I’d done it in eight when motivated. I was very motivated.
I had made it through the Saltfish Market and was halfway up the Weavers’ Steps when the city changed.
It happened between one step and the next. No warning. No transition. The street was empty and then the street had a man in it.
And somewhere to the north of the city — far away, then less far, then no longer far at all — something that was not quite sound began.
Every person in Zemya knew about the sound of the Hunt. Not frightening the way a human threat was frightening, not the spike of adrenaline that came with danger you could fight or run from. This was the fear that came before thought. The kind that lived in the oldest part of the body, the part that had been afraid of the dark long before it had words for dark.
I stood on the Weavers’ Steps and I listened to the sound of the Hollow Hunt moving through my city.
And then I turned north.
Not toward home. North, toward the sound, toward the part of the lower city that pressed closest to the river where the Hollow bled through worst, and I’d taken exactly four steps in that direction before some functional part of my mind caught up with my feet and asked, with considerable urgency: what are you doing.
I stopped.
I stood very still on the fifth step of the Weavers’ Steps and looked north, at the direction I’d been walking, at the direction the sound was coming from. The sound had not gotten louder. The not-color in the sky was shifting, moving, pulled in some direction that had nothing to do with wind.
My feet wanted to go north.
I had been ignoring inconvenient impulses my entire life, and I was quite good at it, so I noted the impulse and set it aside the way you set aside a tool you don’t have a use for right now. What I had a use for right now was the twelve-minute walk home, which had become urgent in a way I was trying not to examine too closely.
I turned south.
I walked.
* * *
I made it three streets.
The Hollow did not stop me. I want to be clear about this, because the stories made it sound like the Hollow acted on you. It did not act. It simply changed the terms of the space you were moving through, and you adapted or you did not.
The logic of this particular shadow was that it was moving against the nonexistent light source, which meant it wasn’t a shadow at all but something else wearing a shadow’s shape.
I took the alley to my left instead.
The alley brought me out two streets over — not the direction I wanted to be going, and when I tried to correct back toward the Warrens the streets did the thing that the lower city streets did on Veil Nights, which the old scholars called membrane-drift and which practically meant that the city’s geography became slightly negotiable. Not impassable — nothing moved, nothing changed visibly — but the internal logic of the space shifted, so that the route I’d taken a thousand times felt different underfoot in a way I couldn’t account for. Like walking a familiar room in the dark and finding the furniture had moved two inches.
I stopped. Breathed. Thought.
The sound of the Hunt was closer.
I had sixty silver marks in my inner pocket — not helpful, and a knife at my hip, which was also not helpful but felt better to think about. I had the Saltfish Market behind me, the river somewhere to the north and west, and the growing conviction that the twelve minutes between Tanners’ Row and my room had become theoretical.
Every child in Vreshka was taught what to do if the Veil started while you were outside: get indoors. Any indoors. Lock the door. Do not look out the window. Do not open the door until the bells ring at dawn.
I was, in fact, a person. This was going to be very difficult to communicate through a locked door.
* * *
The sound changed.
I had been moving — carefully, keeping to the walls, trying to navigate by feel toward anything that looked like a main street where the buildings were sturdier and the doors less likely to be the hollow wooden kind that didn’t actually stop anything — when the Hunt shifted from background to foreground. Not louder. Oriented. It had a direction now, a specific vector, and the vector was this street.
I did not run, because running was how you fell, and falling in a situation like this was something I didn’t want to think about. I moved faster, which was different. Fast and controlled, close to the wall, watching the street ahead and behind simultaneously with the particular awareness that came from ten years of moving through places I wasn’t supposed to be.
The shadows at the far end of the alley shifted.
I stopped.
The shadows weren’t moving the way shadows moved. The air around it was the not-color I’d seen in the sky. The temperature in the alley dropped another ten degrees.
Then it stepped out of the shadows and it was a man.
Except that it was very obviously not a man. It had the shape of one — tall, dark-coated, moving with the economy of something that had never in its existence needed to hurry — but the shape was the beginning and the end of what it had in common with anyone I had ever met. Silver. Not pale gray — silver, the metal, cold and deep and without warmth.
Those eyes found me immediately.
I had my knife out. I didn’t remember drawing it. My hand was steady, which I was grateful for, because the rest of me was conducting a very rapid internal debate between the part that wanted to run and the part that knew, with absolute certainty, that running was not an available option.
The not-a-man looked at the knife.
Then it looked at me.
It said nothing. It moved nothing. It simply stood at the end of the alley with the particular patience of something that had been alive long enough that patience was not an achievement but a state of being, and it watched me with those silver eyes, and I understood — in the part of my mind that processed things my conscious thought hadn’t caught up to yet — that I had already been caught.
That the moment its attention had oriented toward this street, it had been over.
That the knife was ceremonial.
I kept it in my hand anyway. On principle.
“The Veil Nights began an hour ago,” it said. The voice was low and unhurried, and it carried the feel of something that had been using language for so long that it no longer needed volume to be heard. “You knew this.”
“I did,” I said. My own voice came out steady. I was surprised, and then I was not surprised, because I had been in bad situations before and steadiness was the thing I defaulted to when everything else failed.
“And yet here you are.”
“And yet here I am,” I agreed.
The silver eyes did something that was not a blink — a slower movement, deliberate, like recalibrating. “You are aware of what I am.”
“I am aware of what you are,” I said. I was also aware that my heart was doing something very unsteady that I was going to deal with later, privately, when I was not required to be functional.
“And of the law.”
It moved then — not toward me, not yet. Just a shift in weight, a small reconfiguration of that enormous stillness. Up close, the cold was extraordinary. Not the cold of the air, not even the Hollow-cold I’d been feeling all evening: this was the cold of something ancient, the particular temperature of a thing that had existed before the concept of warmth.
“Then you understand what happens now,” it said.
I understood what happened now.
The knife was absolutely ceremonial.
I lowered it anyway — and I looked at the thing with the silver eyes that was the Lord of the Hunt and the oldest of the Vorath and something the world had been afraid of for longer than the world had had words for fear, and I said:
“I want it noted that I had a very good reason for being outside tonight.”
The silver eyes looked at me.
Something in the long, still face shifted — not much, barely at all, in a way I wouldn’t have caught if I hadn’t been watching very carefully.
It was gone again before I could name it.
“Noted,” it said.
And then its hand closed around my wrist, and the alley went sideways, and the world I had lived in my entire life disappeared.
I did not let go of the knife.
